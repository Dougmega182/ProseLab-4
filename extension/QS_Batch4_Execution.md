The decryption key arrived at 6:41 AM.

Not a file transfer — a single-use cipher that would route his device through a dark network tunnel for exactly three hours before the channel collapsed and the QSA's deniability restored itself. He confirmed receipt and set the device face-down on the aluminum table and sat with his hands flat on the surface for ninety seconds.

The plant room had been dark. The Fitzroy office was not quite dark — the pre-dawn grey of a winter morning pressing through the window, the kind of light that made everything in the room look provisional, documented but not confirmed. He had been in this room for four hours. He would be in it for three more.

He picked up the device and opened the tunnel.

The journals were not what he had expected, which was clinical documentation. Emily's handwriting ran margin to margin across the pages — not the compressed, efficient script of the operational reports he had seen her produce, but something faster and more private, the pen moving at the speed of thought rather than record. He recognised the register. It was the register of someone writing to understand rather than to document.

The first volume: the early deployments. His own ICS readings, the Bleed episodes, the proximity field measurements from each Threshold visit. He recognised all of it. He had lived all of it. What he had not seen was the commentary that ran alongside the data — Emily's analysis of each episode, the pattern she had been building since his first deployment, the fourteen months of watching his architecture respond to the Sphere's field and recording, in her own handwriting, what the data confirmed.

He read this slowly.

The second volume: the theoretical framework. Emily had reconstructed Solis's residue theory from the journals — not transcribed it, rebulit it from first principles, the way a mathematician rebuilds a proof to understand rather than to copy. The handwriting here was different from the data sections, the letters slightly larger, the pressure lighter, the pen moving with the specific quality of a mind working at the edge of its own understanding.

He recognised this quality also. It was what his handwriting looked like when he had found the gap.

The third volume was the mapping data. 847 coordinates, their coherence offsets, the spatial geometry of the Sphere's field rendered in blue ink across graph paper that Emily had taped into the journal's pages with the care of someone who understood she was building an archive rather than notes. The fourth volume continued it. The fifth volume contained something different.

He was on page thirty-one of the fifth journal when the three hours ended.

The cipher expired at 9:41 AM. The channel closed. The tunnel collapsed. The device returned the standard QSA network notification and he set it on the table and looked at the window for sixty seconds.

The pre-dawn grey had become the flat, indifferent illumination of a cold morning. Outside, a tram ran south on Smith Street. A delivery vehicle ran north. The city was conducting its ordinary operations in the specific, indifferent way of a thing that did not know what had been decided in its direction.

He had three hours of operational window before the sweep teams located the router's signature.

He had read enough.

He packed the laptop into the bag, secured the journals under his left arm, and left the Fitzroy office for the last time. The door closed behind him. The router's green light held for another forty-two minutes before the sweep team reached junction eleven and the Fitzroy node went offline.

By then he was already at the waste bay on Punt Road.
