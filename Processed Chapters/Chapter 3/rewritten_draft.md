# **CHAPTER 3 — THRESHOLD**

The corridor stretched for forty meters, its fluorescent lights untouched by sunlight, creating a harsh, clinical brightness. The illumination seemed to judge rather than simply light the space. The granite walls, sealed doors, and the back of Emily's jacket—just ahead of Kain—were marked with an air of familiarity, indicating she had come to terms with this environment before.

Kain's right hand rested in his jacket pocket, a tremor starting during the last part of the drive—a subtle vibration in his ring and little fingers that had become as predictable as the weather over the past four years. To quiet it, he pressed his thumb against the seam of his pocket, a gesture unnoticed by those around him.

A pressing sensation filled his head, reminiscent of altitude changes. It was neither painful nor comforting, a reminder that the air here affected him in ways he was unprepared for.

Emily stopped before a door marked with a single horizontal blue tape stripe—a straightforward identifier typical in high-security settings, where complex digital signage often failed at critical moments. She placed her palm on the panel, and the door opened.

"Aspect is already inside," she said.

\*\*\*  
Inside the room were a table, three chairs, and a wall-mounted display that was turned off. In one corner stood an aging Gen-3 medical scanner, its casing yellowed with age, its sensor array directed at the nearest chair, waiting as if it had been dormant for too long.

The man next to the scanner was not what Kain had envisioned, although he had no particular image in mind. Aspect—a term the QSA used for personnel who required a level of institutional detachment—looked to be in his sixties, with a lean frame shaped by years in environments where excess was unwelcome. He wore a QSA medical uniform without insignia and rested his hands on the table, calm and composed, embodying the professionalism expected of someone who regularly delivers difficult news.

"Mr. Kain," he said. "Please take a seat."

Kain obeyed. Emily chose a chair against the wall, positioned behind him to the left, avoiding his direct line of sight. He did not turn to look at her.

"I will conduct a standard biometric intake," Aspect said. "This will take about four minutes. I'll explain what I'm assessing as we go along."

"You already know what you're looking for," Kain replied.

Aspect's hands remained still. "Yes."

"Then skip the intake and just tell me."

There was a brief pause—perhaps two or three seconds—before Aspect responded.

"Your PQ genotype is a variant we classify as Carrier-Proximate," he explained. "It appears in approximately 0.3 percent of the population. For most carriers, it remains dormant—showing no symptoms, causing no measurable neurological deviation, and detectable only through specialized genetic sequencing." Maintaining eye contact with Kain, he continued, "In a small subset of Carrier-Proximate individuals, about one in twelve, the genotype activates. We refer to this activation as the Bleed."

The fluorescent light bore down on the table between them. Kain's right hand rested in his lap, thumb pressed against his trouser pocket seam.

"The Bleed initially manifests as peripheral fragmentation," Aspect continued. "These visual disruptions last less than a second, often attributed to fatigue or stress, leading patients to avoid assessment." He paused. "Over time, these disruptions extend. The visual disturbances lengthen and start impacting other sensory channels—auditory processing, proprioception, and the brain's capacity to maintain a stable perception of its surroundings." Another pause. "In the final stages, the ability to differentiate between current sensory input and stored neurological patterns deteriorates completely. The brain defaults to memory instead of perception, and the patient effectively ceases to engage with their environment."

Silence enveloped the room. The Gen-3 scanner remained focused on the empty chair, indifferent to the situation.

"How long?" Kain asked.

"The average duration from the first signs of peripheral fragmentation to complete cognitive dissolution is six years, with a range of four to nine years." Aspect's hands stayed motionless. "We need to determine when your fragmentation first occurred to provide a more precise timeline."

"Four years ago," Kain replied. "In March. I was in a parking lot in Fitzroy North when a neon sign fragmented for about half a second before it reassembled."

Aspect made no notes. "Then your timeline is between now and two years from now, with the median probability clustering around fourteen months."

The weight of that number struck him with brutal clarity, halting all other thoughts.

Fourteen months.

Not a rough estimate. Not a range. A precise statistical median marking his future as a countdown. Two years felt like an allowance; less than that was cruel. Fourteen months was a number his body had already sensed, confirmed by a system indifferent to loss.

He felt the pressure in his skull shift, transforming from external discomfort to an internal resonance—a Bleed ignited by emotional upheaval rather than neurological disturbance. The tremor in his right hand, once suppressed against the pocket seam, now felt like an affirmation rather than a failure—a tangible expression of a reality he could no longer deny.  
He opened his eyes and fixed his gaze on the fluorescent light. It no longer resembled an examination; it had become a timer, counting down the seconds he had left under its sterile scrutiny.

For four years, he had navigated the possibility of this outcome, structuring his life around it. The fragmentation, the tremor, and the way certain combinations of light and movement could displace him from the present—these weren’t symptoms he had ignored but data points he had cataloged and managed. He had devised strategies—the grip on a glass, his pulse count, the specific route through the Pearl’s parking lot—because facing a reality like this one was unbearable. His strategies indicated he understood the stakes; the number was a countdown reaching a critical threshold.

He turned his head.

Emily was seated against the wall, hands in her lap, her gaze fixed on a point in the distance between her and the table. The tension in her jaw had dissipated, replaced by a controlled absence—indicating she had processed this information and was now grappling with its implications. Her composure was a counterbalance to the chaos surrounding them.  
She had known.  
A sharp realization cut through the shock of his diagnosis. She had understood his fate before entering the Black Pearl. She had been aware before sending the file, before detailing the nuances of Chen’s absence, before appealing to his unique understanding of negative space. She had designed the operation around this knowledge, calculating his impending death as an acceptable variable.

The Varn case had been a betrayal of his career; this was a betrayal of his existence—the manipulation of his death. She viewed the fourteen months not as a tragedy but as a calculated parameter, one she had already analyzed and deemed manageable.

The raw injustice did not evoke anger but rather a cold, analytical certainty. Her betrayal was quantifiable, with practical implications.

He looked at the empty chair where Aspect had been sitting and then at the sealed door. The air felt cold, but the pressure in his skull escalated, signaling the onset of an emotional Bleed—a rejection of the framework she had imposed on him.

He withdrew his right hand from his pocket and placed it flat on the table. He no longer cared if the tremor was visible; it had become part of the truth she had acknowledged.  
His gaze returned to the table. The fluorescent light was a falsehood. The stillness of the room was a pretense. The only reality was that number: fourteen months, and the woman who had exchanged it for a neatly documented operational history.

"What else?" he asked.

\*\*\*  
Aspect left at 2:40 AM, leaving them with the files.

The official records were positioned on the left side of the table—three folders with covers stamped in descending order of the QSA's classification hierarchy. Emily had legitimate clearance for all of them. She had opened them before Aspect closed the door, her movements efficient and practiced, showing her familiarity with this space and its contents.

The journals were placed on the right side of the table.

Three volumes. A5 hardcovers, their dark green covers faded to near-gray at the edges. The spines lacked titles—only the series of small, precise handwritten dates tilted left, suggesting the author had been trained to write quickly and retained that speed.

Kain picked up the first volume.

The paper absorbed ink rather than merely holding it on the surface—a slight roughness typical of a journal intended for practical use rather than aesthetics. The first entry was dated fourteen years prior, filled from margin to margin without paragraph breaks, indicative of someone writing faster than they could organize their thoughts.

*The insertion window is narrower than theoretical models suggest. Coherence degrades at the cellular level within 71 hours of initial quantum state transfer—not 96 as indicated by the 2128 trials. The discrepancy lies in the substrate. We have modeled against synthetic tissue. The living substrate responds differently. It resists.*

He flipped through the pages. The entries varied in dating—some with complete timestamps, others with just a month and year, with a few lacking any date. The handwriting remained consistent: small, precise, left-leaning, pressed firmly into the paper, leaving impressions visible on the back of each page.

*Subject 7 presents the first clean insertion at 68 hours. Behavioral integration is 94% at 72 hours. The remaining 6% is what I have termed the residue—the behavioral patterns deeply encoded in the original subject's neurology that survive the transfer and reemerge in the replacement as habits the new subject cannot consciously account for. The replacement does not know why it reaches for the coffee with its left hand. It simply does.*

Kain set the first volume down and picked up the second.

The dates on the spine of the second volume indicated it was four years younger than the first. The handwriting was consistent, but the pressure had lightened, the pen moving more quickly, the letters compressing at line ends as if the writer anticipated needing this space.

He located the entry he sought on page forty-three.

*The program has been officially classified under Article 7. I have been told this is a protective measure—that this classification shields the research from political interference and ensures continuity despite changes in QSA leadership. I have heard this from three separate directors over the past eighteen months. I have started to count how many times I am told something is for my protection.*

He examined the inside front cover of the second volume.

The QSA's standard intake protocol mandated that all personal effects removed from a sequestered individual be logged in the intake manifest—item description, condition, date of removal, and the authorizing officer. The inside front cover of a journal processed through intake would typically bear an adhesive label in the upper right corner: a barcode, a manifest reference number, and the date of logging.

The upper right corner of the inside front cover was clean.

No label. No barcode. No manifest reference.

He checked the first volume. Clean.

The third. Also clean.

He placed the third volume back onto the table and picked up the closest official folder—the one from the top of the left-hand stack, labeled SOLIS, M. / PERSONAL EFFECTS MANIFEST / INTAKE DATE \[REDACTED\]. He opened it to the manifest page.

The manifest detailed forty-seven items: clothing, personal electronics—all wiped and archived—one framed photograph, two books—titles listed—and one wristwatch.

No journals.

The manifest was complete. The journals were not included. They had never been logged.

Across the table, Emily was engrossed in the third official folder, her eyes moving steadily across the page as if she were reading rather than skimming. Her right hand lay beside the folder, while her left rested in her lap.

The tension in her jaw had vanished. Her breathing was steady. The folder commanded her full attention, or she feigned that focus with the same precision she had applied to everything else in the room since Aspect had left.

Kain closed the manifest folder.

He picked up the second Solis journal and turned to page forty-four.

*The residue is not a flaw. I have spent three years treating it as an error in the insertion process—a contamination of the replacement's behavioral profile by the original subject's encoded neurology. I was mistaken. The residue is proof. It serves as evidence that the original subject existed. It is the aspect that the replacement cannot erase because it does not know it’s there.*

*To find a replacement, you do not search for what is wrong. You identify what is right in a manner the replacement cannot explain.*

He read the entry twice, then laid the journal flat on the table and gazed at the ceiling—at the fluorescent light imposing its stark brightness on every surface in the room—and reflected on Marcus Chen’s left hand reaching for a book that no longer existed.

"She was right," he said.

Emily lifted her gaze from the folder, her eyes flicking to the journal, then back to his face.

"About the residue," he clarified. "Chen's book. The coffee. He wasn’t making mistakes. The replacement carried Chen’s habits unknowingly. The residue."

"Yes," Emily confirmed.

"She figured this out fourteen years ago."

"Yes."

"And yet, the program continued."

Emily met his gaze for two seconds before returning her attention to the folder.

Kain picked up the journal.

\*\*\*

He read for ninety minutes. Emily remained beside him, steadily working through the official folders with the meticulous patience of someone who had already read them and was now revisiting them in his presence. The fluorescent silence enveloped the room. The Gen-3 scanner continued to point at the empty chair.

At 4:15 AM, he set down the third journal.

The Bleed had subsided since Aspect left—no fragmentation, no peripheral disturbances—only the tremor in his right hand maintaining its low, reliable frequency. He pressed his thumb against the seam of his pocket and counted his pulse. Sixty-one. The proximity field pressed against the inside of his skull, its persistent sensation reminiscent of altitude change.

Fourteen months, median probability.

He had two years at most. Fourteen months more likely. He had, at the lower bound, even less than that—a figure Aspect refrained from stating because it would require precision unsupported by the data and cruelty that served no operational purpose.

He had enough time to locate the network. He did not have enough time to approach it slowly.

"The residue," he said. "Solis identified it fourteen years ago. The QSA had this information and continued the program."

"The program had been classified under Article 7 before she wrote that entry," Emily replied. "That classification meant the research findings were operationally contained. They did not propagate to the divisions that would have acted on them."

"Who authorized the Article 7 classification?"

Emily closed the folder before her. Her hands rested flat on its cover, still and composed.

"That's in the redacted section," she responded.

"Which you have clearance for."

"Which I have clearance for."

She did not reopen the folder.

Kain observed her hands on the folder's cover, noting the specific quality of their stillness. A decision lay within that stillness, made before he asked the question, perhaps even before she sat down.

He picked up the first Solis journal.

"I'll need copies of these," he said.

"I can arrange access to the official record," Emily said. "The folders can be—"

"These," he interrupted, placing the journal on the table between them, spine facing her, its faded green cover, handwritten dates, and the clean inside front cover devoid of a barcode or manifest label all clearly visible beneath the fluorescent light.

Emily stared at the journal.

Two seconds. Three.

"I’ll arrange it," she conceded.

He reclaimed the journal and turned to page forty-three.

*I have begun to count the number of times I am told something is for my protection.*

Outside, the mountain exuded its granite silence. The proximity field pressed against him. The fluorescent light scrutinized everything it illuminated. Kain continued reading, while Emily sat across the table with her hands resting flat on the redacted folder, the Gen-3 scanner focused on the empty chair with its patient, indifferent attention. The room remained exactly what it was.
