# **CHAPTER 4 — ALFRED HOSPITAL**

Reality B-734 was marked by a unique scent, a blend of copper and stagnant water.

This was the initial impression upon arrival—an aroma lingering in an environment that had hosted innumerable occupants, now barely distinguishable. The air in B-734 carried the mineral tang of aging pipes, fused with the odor of oxidized copper from outdated setups. The QSA had overlooked necessary upgrades for this branch, treating it merely as a monitoring site, leading to maintenance only once every seventeen years.

Hayden had been using this node as his entry point for the last eight months.

Positioned in the maintenance corridor behind the hospital's eastern substation, he waited for his legs to regain their strength.

This was his second realization—always the second. Shifting from B-734 to baseline required his body to recalibrate completely. The gravitational pull in B-734 was 0.3 percent stronger than in baseline, causing his muscles to adapt accordingly. Returning to baseline meant his legs expected more weight than the ground provided, resulting in roughly ninety seconds of instability, during which he traversed baseline as if the floor were unsteady.

He had experienced four hundred and twelve transitions, each marked by ninety seconds of erratic leg movements.

Leaning against the wall, he monitored his pulse—not at his wrist, as the radial pulse had become unreliable months ago due to vascular thickening, which Winters had termed *coherence calcification*. Instead, he checked the carotid artery. Ninety-one beats per minute—elevated but acceptable for post-transition recovery. In four minutes, it was likely to decrease to seventy-eight.

A flash of light pulsed on his subdural monitor—one blue flicker behind his left eye, a notification set to replace the audio alert he had disabled months prior to avoid triggering auditory fragmentation. The reading: *34*.

A decrease from thirty-seven, recorded six days ago.

The Identity Coherence Score reflected the consistency of his body's quantum state across repeated transitions. A baseline human, never transitioned, would score between 95 and 100. The QSA established a threshold of 70 for active personnel. Scores under 60 necessitated mandatory stand-down and treatment for coherence stabilization.

Scores under 40 had a separate protocol. Hayden had once perused the file, which he should not have accessed, and had chosen not to revisit it.

Thirty-four.

His legs steadied. He pushed off the wall.

\*\*\*

The hospital's eastern service entrance employed standard QSA-adjacent security measures—biometric locks utilized in civilian medical facilities under the 2149 Public Infrastructure (Quantum Security) Agreement. Hayden possessed the override codes, obtained from a QSA internal archive during his third month in operation when his ICS was still at 61, his hands steady, and his transition headaches lasted four hours instead of eleven.

The lock disengaged. He stepped inside.

\*\*\*

The service corridor extended across the eastern wing's ground floor, paralleling the public corridor but separated by a reinforced concrete wall installed during the 2134 renovation for seismic compliance. Motion-activated lights illuminated sections as he moved—a thirty-meter pool of fluorescent light followed by darkness, then another illuminated patch as the next sensor activated.

Jacob awaited him at the junction.

He was dutifully performing his task—positioned at the intersection of the service corridor and stairwell access, back against the wall, focused on a tablet in his left hand, a shift log updated every thirty minutes. A large man with broad shoulders, he emanated the confidence of someone accustomed to occasional physical demands. His ID badge dangled from a lanyard at chest level.

He looked up as the motion sensor activated the next pool of light.

Hayden was already within the sensor's range. He maintained his pace.

Jacob instinctively reached for the radio at his hip—standard protocol dictated that unscheduled personnel in a restricted area required immediate communication and inquiries.

In four swift steps, Hayden closed the distance. His right hand struck the precise junction of Jacob's neck and shoulder—the brachial plexus, a nerve cluster that, under targeted pressure, could instantly disrupt motor function and elicit overwhelming pain. Two seconds of incapacitation was enough. With his left hand, he controlled the radio before it could be drawn. He guided Jacob to the floor with practiced efficiency, aware that a controlled descent often determined whether a guard would awaken with only a headache or remain unconscious entirely.

Jacob’s head rested on the floor, breathing steady and calm—an unconscious rhythm indicating he had been removed from the situation without harm.

Hayden seized the radio and the tablet, checking the shift log for the next scheduled check-in: forty-two minutes.

Leaving Jacob in the corridor, he ascended the stairs.

\*\*\*

The neurology department was situated on the third floor's eastern wing. At 2:20 AM, only two nurses were present at the central station, with a resident in the on-call room at the far end of the corridor. The ward exuded a profound silence, a sign of both sedated patients and staff fatigue, where conversation required more energy than it was worth.

Hayden moved confidently through the space, embodying someone who belonged. The oversized white coat he had borrowed from the ground floor locker did not draw attention at this hour.

Dr. Sarah Winters occupied office 3-14. Her name appeared on the door in standard hospital font beneath her title: *Consultant Neurologist, Quantum Adjacency Disorders Unit.*

The lock operated on a standard keycard system—neither QSA-adjacent nor biometric. He possessed the master override from a card he had cloned three weeks earlier while conducting reconnaissance for this very purpose.

The door swung open. He entered and closed it behind him.

\*\*\*

The office reflected the disarray typical of someone deeply engrossed in work. Files cluttered the desk, both physical and digital. A whiteboard along the left wall bore a mix of medical shorthand and what appeared to be preliminary attempts at a theoretical framework—notations surfacing as a researcher pushed against the boundaries of existing terminology, creating new symbols for yet-unnamed concepts.

He first photographed the whiteboard, followed by the desk files.

Next, he accessed her terminal.

The QSA's civilian medical data-sharing protocols granted Winters's unit access to the agency's anonymized PQ genotype database, maintained to study quantum adjacency disorders in the general population. Winters had been publishing from this database for six years. Her most recent paper, published eighteen months prior, detailed a treatment protocol for early-stage Bleed that had reduced fragmentation frequency by 34 percent in a cohort of twelve patients.

Hayden had read that paper four times.

He had scrutinized it because the treatment protocol outlined in section four—the coherence stabilization approach involving targeted quantum field exposure and cellular-level calcium chelation—was the closest documented solution for ICS recovery in the literature. Not a cure, as the paper explicitly stated. Winters was meticulous in her reporting. But a method of stabilization—an approach that could, if applied to a subject whose ICS had not yet fallen below a critical threshold, extend operational continuity by an estimated eight to fourteen months.

Eight to fourteen months.

His ICS was thirty-four. The critical threshold was forty. He had fallen below that six days ago, when his score dropped from forty-one to thirty-seven, and he had not informed anyone, as there was no one to tell. Acknowledging it would require revealing his identity, actions, and the toll of four hundred and twelve transitions—a conversation that would lead to one of two undesirable conclusions.

The paper detailed twelve patients. The database contained anonymized records of all twelve. However, what the paper lacked—what no published study could include due to ethical protocols prohibiting identifying information—was the detailed treatment methodology necessary to replicate it without Winters's involvement.

That methodology lay within her unpublished notes.

He located them in the third subdirectory of her research partition, secured by a password he cracked in four minutes—not due to complexity, but because his hands had begun faltering under pressure. The arterial coherence calcification had started affecting his fine motor control. He adapted by typing slowly, verifying each entry before confirming, treating the additional time as a cost of operation rather than a failure.

He copied the entire directory to an encrypted drive: notes, raw data, correspondence with the QSA's research liaison, and a draft of the follow-up paper that Winters had yet to submit, which included the specific methodology omitted from the published version.

The copying process took six minutes. He stood by the terminal, watching the progress bar, absorbing the ward's 2:20 AM silence while counting his pulse at the carotid.

Seventy-nine. A decrease from ninety-one. On schedule.

The ICS monitor pulsed once behind his left eye. He refrained from checking the reading.

\*\*\*

He returned to the service corridor at 2:41 AM. Jacob remained as he had left him—steady breathing, unchanged position, the shift log tablet beside his right hand. Hayden placed the radio down next to it. Jacob would awaken in roughly twenty minutes, nursing a headache concentrated in his left shoulder and neck, with no memory of the preceding moments. He would file an incident report, accurately describing an unscheduled individual in the service corridor, yet ultimately inconsequential.

Hayden exited through the service entrance and into the parking lot.

\*\*\*

The transition point was the maintenance corridor behind the eastern substation. He had forty minutes before the B-734 insertion window closed—the coherence alignment between baseline and B-734 lasted ninety minutes at this location, and he had already utilized fifty.

He stood in the corridor, preparing.

Preparation was not a ritual; it was a self-administered medical procedure carried out in darkness. He checked his pulse: seventy-eight—normal for post-transition recovery, indicating his body had stabilized from the inbound crossing and was functioning within its normal range, though not necessarily healthy. He flexed his fingers—testing all ten in sequence for fine motor response. The ring and pinky fingers of his right hand lagged by approximately forty milliseconds—a consistent pattern from the last six transitions. It hadn’t worsened.

He checked the ICS monitor: *Thirty-two*.

Two points lower in forty minutes. Transition stress was accelerating the decline. He had anticipated this—Winters's notes described it as *transition-induced cascade*, where quantum state disruptions from crossing destabilized his body's coherence faster than recovery could compensate. Below a specific ICS threshold, each transition drained more than the recovery period could replenish.

He was below that threshold. It had been six days.

He initiated the transition.

The first sensation was auditory—a frequency below perception, a pressure felt rather than heard, starting at his sternum and radiating outward through his body, culminating in his skull, where it transformed into excruciating pain as every sinus cavity attempted to adjust to a non-existent pressure differential. Through four hundred and twelve crossings, he had learned not to resist this sensation; fighting would only prolong it. Acceptance wouldn’t shorten the suffering but would preserve the muscle control needed for what followed.

The pressure reached its peak. His vision failed—not dark or white, but absent; the visual cortex momentarily disconnected from input as the quantum state transfer surged through his neurological structure. He counted. One. Two. Three.

The air felt heavier.

B-734's 0.3 percent gravitational increase hit his legs first—the unwelcome sensation of added weight, his body beginning its recalibration. His vision returned gradually, peripheral first, followed by clarity in the center, as the maintenance corridor reformed around him, saturated with the familiar scent of copper and stagnant water from a reality that had existed longer than any living person.

He had made it through.  
The collapse he experienced was not a muscle failure but a physical betrayal.  
His legs did not simply give way; they became unmoored from his spatial perception. One moment he was a cohesive being; the next, his body dissolved into chaotic static, severing his connection to the ground. He hit the concrete with a jarring thud, pain radiating through his body as the transition-induced cascade transformed from theoretical concern to a visceral predator. It felt as though molten lead replaced his marrow, a searing heat pulsing in sync with his failing heart.  
He attempted to push up, but his right hand did not meet the wall; it passed through a ghostly image of it, leaving him clawing at empty, copper-scented air. His vision splintered into a nightmare where the maintenance corridor battled with strobe flashes of countless realities he had encountered and discarded. The acrid smell of burnt ozone filled his lungs, suffocating him, as the ICS monitor behind his eye pulsed violently, a branding iron of light searing the number **32** into his consciousness.  
The psychological horror was the overwhelming certainty that the Hayden Marsh who had entered the corridor had vanished, leaving behind a mere collection of residual habits and fading echoes. He wasn’t just losing his stability; he was losing the very essence of 'I' that felt that loss. Desperation surged as he gasped, the thick, suffocating air of B-734 choking him, and for five seconds of pure, unfiltered panic, he could not recall his name. He was merely a terminal frequency, vibrating toward oblivion.  
Fueled by sheer desperation, he clawed at a gap in the wall's conduit, dragging himself upward. The friction of the brick against his palm proved his material existence. He locked his spine against the vertical plane, breathing in ragged hitches. He stood there, a broken entity stitched together by defiance and adrenaline, peering into the darkness while counting the beats of his heart—one hundred and seven—feeling them like a countdown to his own erasure.

The encrypted drive was in his jacket pocket. Winters's notes. The treatment methodology. The specific parameters omitted from the published paper, which could be the key to improving his score from thirty-two to something higher.

He had forty minutes before the B-734 monitoring cycle initiated its passive scan. He needed to exit the corridor and reach the safe house before that time.

His pulse: ninety-four.

With determination, he pushed off the wall and walked.
