# **CHAPTER 5 — THE APARTMENT**

The intake station marked the final twenty meters of the Threshold facility's exit corridor. This narrowing was intentional, designed to ensure that nothing could leave the building without passing through the sensor array embedded in the walls on either side. The array was passive; it simply read data without inquiry.

Behind the desk sat a technician in her thirties, moving with the precise efficiency typical of someone who performed the same tasks repeatedly and had eliminated all unnecessary actions. She did not look up as Kain approached, extending her right hand, palm up, while her focus remained fixed on the terminal before her.

He placed his wrist against the scanner in her hand. The device felt warm from constant use. It conducted a biometric sweep in four seconds, measuring pulse, temperature, and the subcutaneous markers used by the QSA to monitor personnel movement within secured areas.

Then came the PQ genotype flag. He noticed the change immediately as the technician's gaze shifted—not toward him or the terminal, but to a point about thirty centimeters to the left, a blank stare signaling she was processing unexpected information and deciding on the next steps.

One second. Two.

She withdrew the scanner, her eyes returning to the terminal as her fingers moved across the input surface with the same precise efficiency, logging his exit and completing the record.

"Clear," she stated.

He walked through.

The corridor beyond the intake station extended twelve meters to the facility's outer door. He traversed it at the same measured pace he had adopted in every other corridor—neither slow nor fast, just the demeanor of someone with a destination and no need to rush. His right hand rested in his jacket pocket, thumb against the seam, as a low, reliable tremor pulsed within.

Behind him, the technician's terminal would now indicate a flag on his biometric record—not a diagnosis, as the QSA's intake system only recorded markers. But that marker would be there, visible to anyone with access to the intake logs.

He tucked this thought away and continued onward.

---

Chen's apartment was located in Southbank on the fourteenth floor of a building constructed in 2139. It had aged with the characteristic wear of structures built during a time when architects had briefly believed that quantum-adjacent materials would maintain themselves. They did not. The building's facade displayed a faint iridescent patina from coherence-reactive polymer that had suffered too many years under Melbourne's variable electromagnetic environment, beginning to deteriorate at the molecular level.

Emily had provided him with the access code at 6:00 AM, just before he left Threshold. She did not accompany him, having a meeting with the QSA's internal liaison—the official, paper-trail version of the investigation, which diverged from Kain's current path. He understood the division of labor but did not fully trust it.

The building lobby housed a Gen-3 autom-unit at the concierge station—older than the Pearl's Gen-4 model, its joints exhibiting the characteristic micro-hesitations of its generation. It registered his entry and cross-referenced his access code without comment. The lift ascended to the fourteenth floor.

Chen's door was secured with a QSA evidence lock—a secondary device affixed over the standard biometric panel, displaying the agency's classification stripe in the same blue tape marking the Threshold facility's internal doors. He entered the code, and the lock released.

He donned gloves and stepped inside.

---

The apartment had the distinct feel of a space that had been lived in and then abruptly stopped being used. It wasn't abandoned; the QSA's forensic team had meticulously processed it, leaving behind an order that felt more like a reconstruction than a genuine living space. The furniture remained in place. The objects were undisturbed. The air held the faint chemical scent of the scanning agents used on the surfaces.

Kain paused in the doorway for thirty seconds, surveying the room without stepping inside.

The living area stretched left to right—kitchen at one end, a window facing north toward the river, a desk against the eastern wall, a sofa, and a low table. This was the standard layout for a fourteen-floor apartment in Southbank of this size. Nothing appeared amiss at first glance.

He began with the bookshelf.

The *Maltese Falcon* was present—a physical paperback, its spine facing outward, positioned on the third shelf between a hardbound collection of Chandler stories and a QSA policy manual that Chen had apparently kept at home. The spine was outward, as it should be. The QSA's passive optical record had documented a prior anomaly—a reversal with the spine turned inwards, the title concealed—logged as one of the eighteen-month anomalies. The forensic team had replaced it spine-out, restoring its position from the nine years prior.

Kain took the book from the shelf.

A left-handed reader develops specific habits around physical books. The spine crease—the point where the book is held open most frequently—tends to form on the left side for left-handed readers, as their left hand bears the book's weight while the right hand turns the pages. The *Maltese Falcon* exhibited a spine crease running down the right side.

The replacement had held it in the right hand. For eighteen months, whenever it read this book, it had used its dominant right hand, failing to adopt Chen's left-hand dominance, which had been ingrained through nine years of muscle memory. It was too minor a habit, too far below the threshold of what the replacement had been programmed to memorize.

He replaced the book and moved to the bathroom.

---

The shower's water temperature control was a manual dial—a heritage fitting retained during the 2139 construction, as the developer had marketed the apartments based on their period detailing. The dial ranged from blue to red, with Chen's preferred setting marked by a faint calcium deposit at the seven-o'clock position—the accumulated residue of nine years of consistent use.

The QSA's passive monitoring had flagged a shower duration anomaly: fourteen minutes, up from Chen's established average of eight. The forensic team had noted this in their report, categorizing it as a lifestyle variation.

Kain examined the dial.

The calcium deposit marked the seven-o'clock position, while the dial's current setting—where it had been left after its last use—was at nine o'clock. Warmer. The replacement preferred a hotter shower than Chen did. The difference was not drastic—just two positions on the dial—enough to denote a preference rather than a significant change.

However, the fourteen minutes indicated something deeper. The replacement required that time because it was acclimating to a body it was still learning to navigate, still checking and mapping, running its hands across a physical form that was not its original. Chen's familiarity allowed him to wash in eight minutes; the replacement, still adapting, needed the extra six minutes to be thorough.

Fourteen minutes signified a replacement still behaving cautiously.

He exited the bathroom and approached the desk.

---

The desk was configured for a left-handed user, not in its design—a standard rectangular surface—but in its arrangement. The terminal occupied the right side, centering the keyboard and leaving the left side clear for a left-handed user to spread papers, rest their arm, and access items instinctively. The QSA's policy manual had been placed on the left side for the past nine years, according to the passive optical record. The forensic team had restored it there.

The desk lamp was positioned on the right side.

Kain scrutinized the lamp, then shifted his gaze to the terminal and the clear space on the left.

A left-handed person instinctively places their desk lamp on the left side—not from conscious thought, but because the lamp needs to illuminate the workspace, which is on the left, and such positioning is typically a one-time decision made during the initial use of the desk. The passive optical record confirmed the lamp had been on the left for nine years.

The forensic team had moved it to the right.

He checked the optical record on his tablet—the complete eighteen-month log, which Emily had transferred to his device before he left Threshold. He scrolled to the lamp entry.

In the first month of the anomaly period: the lamp shifted from left to right. It had been flagged by the passive system as a workspace reorganization event and classified within normal parameters.

The replacement had relocated the lamp because it was right-handed and found the left-side lamp obstructive. It had not realized that the lamp's position was a record. It hadn’t comprehended that nine years of left-side positioning had left a subtle, circular indentation on the desk's surface—slight compression of the material beneath the lamp's base, visible only at a low angle with the overhead light off.

Kain crouched down, angling his tablet's light across the desk surface.

The compression mark was on the left side. The lamp was now on the right.

He stood up.

---

Without warning, peripheral fragmentation struck—his left visual field vanished for a full second, the eastern wall of the apartment simply gone, replaced by the disorienting void of a visual cortex devoid of input. He halted, pressing two fingers to his carotid artery to count his pulse.

Eighty-eight. High.

The fragmentation subsided. The eastern wall reappeared—the desk, the terminal, the lamp in the wrong place—all reassembling with the slightly over-sharp clarity characteristic of a Bleed episode, the visual cortex compensating for the interruption.

One second. That duration exceeded the Fitzroy North episode from four years ago, which had lasted half a second, and was longer than the car park incident behind the Pearl two nights prior, which had lasted three-quarters of a second.

The average window was fourteen months. He was not at the beginning of that median.

He filed this observation away and continued his investigation.

---

The kitchen drawer next to the sink was the last item to examine.

Chen's passive optical record indicated a consistent pattern over nine years: the drawer opened with the left hand, as it was positioned to the left of the sink. A left-handed person reaching for it while standing at the sink would naturally use their dominant hand. The drawer contained Chen's most frequently used items—dish brush, small fruit knife, and the cloth he used to wipe the counter.

The handle exhibited a wear pattern: nine years of the same grip, angle of approach, and pressure—slightly more polished on the left side, where a left hand had gripped it thousands of times.

The wear was on the left.

The smudge on the handle—evidence of the most recent contact, still faintly visible on the brushed metal surface—was on the right side.

Kain stood at the kitchen sink, studying the drawer handle, recalling Martina Solis’s writing on page forty-three of her second journal: *The replacement does not know why it reaches for the coffee with the left hand. It simply does.*

The replacement in Chen's apartment had not reached for the drawer with its left hand. It had reached with its right, lacking the imprint of Chen's left-handedness. The drawer was too minor, too peripheral—a detail the replacement had not been programmed to remember. It had only assimilated Chen's major habits, professional patterns, and institutional memories. The drawer had been deemed insignificant.

Kain photographed the handle, the compression mark on the desk, the shower dial, and the spine crease in the *Maltese Falcon*. He logged each anomaly against the passive optical record, timestamping them and adding a notation referencing Solis’s residue theory.

Four anomalies. Four signs of a right-handed individual navigating a left-handed existence, leaving behind subtle, invisible traces of the discrepancies in areas too small for memorization and too habitual to perceive.

The QSA's instruments had overlooked all of them.

He gazed out the kitchen window, observing the river fourteen floors below—the Yarra flowing steadily through the city, indifferent to the apartment above, the deceased man who once lived there, and the entity that had replaced him.

His right hand remained in his jacket pocket, the tremor pulsing steadily. His left visual field was stable.

He had four anomalies, a methodology, and fourteen months—the median probability.

He retrieved his tablet and began drafting the report.
