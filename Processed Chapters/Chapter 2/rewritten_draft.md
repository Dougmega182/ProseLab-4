# **CHAPTER 2 — THE MOUNTAIN**

Betty’s lone headlight sliced through the downpour as Kain veered south onto the M1.

Emily sat beside him, the tablet illuminating her lap. She had been silent since their departure from Brunswick Street, and Kain made no effort to break the quiet. His mind continued to turn over Chen's schedule, examining every detail meticulously, searching for gaps.

7:30 tram. Coffee with cream. Workout at 7:00.

Three deviations. Three traces left by a man aware of his impending disappearance. Not random. Calculated. Evidence requiring prior knowledge of the original sequence.

Chen had left no message. He had left a puzzle, and unlocking it required understanding him.

At midnight, the M1 south of the city transformed into a different entity. During the day, it was clogged with freight traffic — heavy lift hover-utes transporting goods between the port and distribution hubs, their magnetic seals producing a persistent vibration that Dandenong residents had long considered mere background noise. By night, the road belonged to long-haul drivers and an occasional private car, making Betty’s solitary headlight seem misplaced in the enveloping darkness.

The rain lashed against the windshield, and the windshield wipers struggled at their highest speed, still failing to prevent fleeting moments of obscurity at the end of each sweep. Kain adjusted his speed, navigating carefully without relinquishing control.

“How far?” he inquired.

“Two hours. Maybe more in this.” Emily gestured toward the rain.

“You want to tell me where we’re going?”

“Wilsons Promontory. The mountain.”

“There’s nothing on that mountain except hiking trails and a car park.”

Emily remained silent, her fingers tapped across the tablet screen, revealing a route Kain had never seen — a road branching from the main trail that snaked up Mount Oberon’s eastern face in tight spirals resembling a wound.

“That road doesn’t exist,” Kain remarked.

“It does if you know where to look.”

\*\*\*   
They rolled through Dandenong at 12:40. The city's southern boundary felt like an incomplete thought — heritage terraces faded into the flat, practical layout of logistics precincts, which then gave way to the dark expanse of the Cranbourne stretch of the South Gippsland Highway. The sodium lights thinned. The road narrowed. The rain grew heavier.

“Seventy-two hours to erase someone and put a copy in their place,” Kain stated.

“Yes.”

“And you’re telling me this because it’s interesting, or because there’s a clock running right now?”

Emily hesitated before responding. That silence spoke volumes.

“Three weeks ago,” she explained, “we intercepted a communication suggesting the next replacement is scheduled for someone in Melbourne. Someone connected to the original investigation into Chen’s death.”

Kain’s grip on the wheel tightened.

“How long?”

“The intercept was timestamped six hours before I contacted you. If the seventy-two-hour pattern holds, we have roughly sixty-five hours from now.”

“Who’s the target?”

“We don’t know. But the connection to Chen’s investigation narrows the list.” She paused. “It’s a list that includes me. And now it includes you.”

They drove in silence for twenty minutes. The Strzelecki Ranges loomed in the southeast — not yet visible, but palpable, as if large landforms were present in the darkness, a heaviness in the air, altering how sound traveled. The road began to curve away from the coast and into the ranges, and Kain felt the temperature drop two degrees within a kilometer.

Emily broke the silence.

“I need to tell you something,” she said. “Before we get there.”

“Another thing you’re not telling me, or one of the ones you are?”

She took her time responding. He kept his gaze fixed on the road.

“Elias Varn,” she finally said.

The name struck like a stone. He maintained his grip on the wheel, choosing silence, and she let the stillness linger.

\*\*\*  
Fifteen years prior, Elias Varn had been a mid-level procurement officer in the Department of Infrastructure's quantum relay division — overseeing civilian contracts for the entanglement relay towers spanning Victoria. He had held the position for six years, his performance reviews adequate and his security clearance at Level 2. By all institutional standards, he was unremarkable.

Kain had stumbled upon him by chance. A routine audit revealed a billing anomaly — a rounding error that had accumulated over the years, surfacing as a discrepancy too small for fraud yet too consistent to be a mistake. Kain was assigned to the case due to availability, with the division chief eager for a quick resolution. It was not a case that advanced careers.

The first week was spent combing through Varn's contractor files, finding nothing. The second week involved scrutinizing movement logs, still yielding nothing. By week three, he found himself in a coffee shop across from the Department of Infrastructure's Level 4 entrance, observing Elias Varn enter work.

Varn arrived precisely at 8:47 each morning. Not 8:45. Not 8:50. 8:47, with a variance of plus or minus ninety seconds tracked over eleven consecutive days. He came from the north, taking the 8:31 tram from Fitzroy North — a route requiring a transfer at Smith Street, depositing him at Flinders Lane at 8:44, leaving three minutes to cover the two blocks to the Department entrance. The timing was not accidental; it was the product of a man whose commute had been optimized to the point of becoming instinctive — where the 8:31 tram was not a choice but a reflex, as fixed as a heartbeat.

On day twenty-three, Varn appeared at 9:12.

9:12, from the south — wrong time, wrong direction. He must have taken a different tram from an alternate stop on another line. He held a coffee from an unfamiliar vendor — a flat white in a paper cup with a blue logo, from a chain operating out of Southern Cross concourse. His pace was quicker than usual. He clutched the coffee in his left hand, contrary to his right-handed nature, where he had always carried it in his right hand on the previous twenty-two days.

Kain returned to his desk and retrieved Varn's movement history from the past six months.

The 8:31 tram from Fitzroy North. Every working day. Without exception. For six months.

Until the twenty-third day.

He then pulled Varn's personal records — requiring a Level 4 clearance and a justification Kain meticulously crafted in forty minutes. Medical history. Financial records. Social contact logs. The passive optical record from Varn's apartment building indicated Elias entered at 6:23 PM on the twenty-second day and did not leave until 7:41 AM on the twenty-third.

However, the man who entered at 6:23 PM had a gait pattern — specific measurements of stride length, hip rotation, and weight distribution — that differed from the man who exited at 7:41 AM by 4.3 percent.

4.3 percent. Within the range of normal variation. Acceptable noise for every automated system.

Kain flagged it anyway. His supervisor dismissed him. His Commander echoed the same sentiment with less patience. He filed the flag regardless, attaching it to an unremarkable report, and continued monitoring Elias Varn.

Over the subsequent six weeks, he documented thirty-seven additional anomalies. The coffee. The commute. The hand. The way Varn held his security pass now — right hand, badge facing out — when he had previously held it left hand, badge facing in, for six years. The way he no longer stopped at the third-floor vending machine on Tuesdays, avoiding a specific brand of crackers that the fourth-floor machine lacked. The way he took the lift instead of the stairs, despite having a documented knee condition that made the lift's acceleration uncomfortable.

The replacement possessed Varn’s face. His voice. His fingerprints. His retinal pattern. His entire institutional history was memorized in such detail that it fooled every colleague, every automated system, and every security protocol of the Department of Infrastructure.

It had not memorized the crackers.

Kain presented his findings to the QSA's Civilian Interface Division — the sole authority capable of acting on his discoveries. He laid out thirty-seven anomalies, six weeks of movement logs, and a gait analysis he commissioned privately from a biomechanics lab specializing in forensic motion capture.

The QSA accepted his report. They expressed gratitude and assured him they would follow up when there were updates.

He was never contacted.

Three months later, his role at the Department of Infrastructure was eliminated during a restructuring. His contractor license was suspended pending an indefinite review. His access to the government movement log database — the one he used to build the Varn case — was revoked on the grounds that his clearance was no longer valid.

The following four years saw him dabble in work that didn’t require a clearance. Missing persons cases. Insurance fraud. Occasional domestic surveillance jobs taken to cover rent.

\*\*\*  
“You knew,” he said.

Emily’s hands remained on the tablet, unmoving.

“I was the one who received your report,” she replied. “I was a senior analyst by then. Seven years in. I read it, assessed it, and escalated it to my division director with a recommendation for immediate action.”

“And?”

“My division director agreed. The operation to extract and assess the Varn replacement was authorized within forty-eight hours of your report landing on my desk.” She paused. “The operation was successful. The replacement was removed. The intelligence gathered informed the QSA's understanding of the Replacement Network for the following decade.”

The road ascended. The ranges closed in, exerting a slow, steady pressure, ancient terrain folding in on itself, indifferent to drama, to the sudden elevation of alpine regions further north. The eucalypts crowded the roadside, their bark pale and damp in Betty’s light, their canopy obscured above the rain.

“And my career?” Kain asked.

“Was a necessary operational cost.” Her tone was flat, devoid of inflection or remorse. “If you had remained in your position with your clearance intact, the network would have identified you as the source of the Varn extraction. You would have become a target.”

“So you rendered me unemployable.”

“We made you invisible. There’s a difference.”

“There isn’t,” Kain retorted. “Not from where I was standing.”  
He had spent those four years learning to navigate the phantom limb of a career she had severed without anesthetic. It was more than just the revoked clearance; it was the systematic dismantling of a decade's worth of professional relationships. Every contact he had nurtured in the Department — colleagues with whom he had shared favors, analysts who understood his intuitive shorthand — had gone silent the moment his status switched to “administratively terminated.” He switched lanes without signaling, then signaled too late, for no one. The high-fidelity access to government movement databases was replaced by scraping public records and desperate, low-stakes data-mining of insurance fraud claims. To survive, he had to forge a cynical framework within himself — a self-contained bunker where he stowed his resentment beside his principles, both becoming equally negotiable against the mounting burden of rent. She had made him invisible to protect the QSA, yet in doing so, she imprisoned him in a version of himself he neither recognized nor embraced.

He drove. The rain poured. The ranges absorbed both.

\*\*\*   
The road the QSA utilized diverged from the main Wilsons Prom access track at a point unmarked on civilian navigation systems, invisible from the main track unless approached at the precise angle. Emily guided him with the calm precision of someone reciting from memory rather than glancing at the tablet — left at the second fire trail marker, then two hundred meters on the unsealed surface, then right at a stand of mountain ash that had once been struck by lightning and had since grown back in a way that was less tree than an argument.

The unsealed surface was slick clay over granite, and Betty’s tires struggled for traction with incremental, hesitant grips. The switchbacks wound up Mount Oberon’s eastern face in tight turns that had seemed like a scar on the tablet’s screen. Up close, in the dark and rain, they felt like one.

At the third switchback, Kain’s stomach churned.

Not from the road itself. The road was treacherous yet manageable. The nausea stemmed from elsewhere — an undefined pressure, a low-frequency disquiet registering in his gut before it reached his mind. His vision remained clear. His hands remained steady. Something in the air felt wrong in a way his body had no prior terminology for.

“Proximity field,” Emily explained, without lifting her gaze from the tablet. “You’ll adjust in a few minutes. Most people do.”

“Most….”

“The ones who don’t are usually the ones we’re more interested in.” She glanced at him. “You’re adjusting.”

The field — a coherence-suppression perimeter designed to prevent any quantum-capable device from functioning within a two-kilometer radius of the facility without authorization — had been installed in 2119 during the QSA's efforts to consolidate its eastern Australian infrastructure following the collapse of the Canberra relay network. The mountain was selected for its inherent qualities — granite bedrock absorbing specific electromagnetic frequencies more effectively than any engineered material ever could. Elevation and seclusion completed the criteria.  
The facility itself was nameless in any document accessible at a civilian clearance level. Internal QSA communications referred to it as the Threshold. The name was chosen, Emily explained, by the facility’s first director — a woman named Martina Solis, who spent twenty-two years transforming the quantum insertion program from theoretical to operational, retiring in 2141 with a commendation classified at the same level as the program.

“Solis,” Kain noted. “That name appears in Chen’s file.”

“Chen worked under her. He was part of her original research team.” Emily’s voice was measured, hinting at a controlled sharing of information. “Her notes on the early insertion trials are part of what we’ll be working from.”

“Where is she now?”

Emily returned her focus to the tablet. “Retired,” she replied.

The word settled heavily, bearing the weight of accuracy yet lacking completion. Kain stowed it and continued driving.

The nausea persisted. It had shifted from the acute discomfort of initial contact to a low, constant reminder that his body was detecting something beyond its design. He steered through it as he navigated everything else: hands on the wheel, eyes on the road, attention on what was next.

The facility’s entrance materialized from the darkness at the top of the final switchback — an area of the mountain's face that felt different from the surrounding rock. A door, set flush against the granite, lacked any visible handle or lock, featuring a single recessed panel at shoulder height glowing a faint, cold blue amid the rain.

Emily exited the car. The rain hit her instantly; she showed no reaction. She approached the panel, pressed her palm flat against it, and held it there for four seconds.

The door opened inward — a sound akin to a breath held too long being released, the hydraulic seals disengaging with a low, pressurized hiss, followed by the steel’s weight pulling it through its arc with the slow inevitability of something that had waited a long time to move. The light beyond it pierced the wet ground before Kain’s eyes adjusted — bright and stark, illuminating shadows that seemed to accuse him.

Kain turned off Betty's engine. He lingered for a moment in the darkness, exposed to the rain, the persistent pressure of the proximity field. Thoughts of Elias Varn’s crackers flashed in his mind. The 4.3 percent variance in a gait pattern that automated systems had dismissed as mere noise. The three years spent chasing insurance fraud due to his principles being anything but fixed.

He considered Martina Solis, now retired.

He grasped the door handle and stepped out.
