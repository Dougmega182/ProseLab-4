# **CHAPTER 10 — DEAD MAN'S SWITCH**

The meeting was not in the sterile briefing room where they had discussed the Chen anomalies. It was in Emily’s private operations office on the third floor of the QSA annex—a room configured for continuous operational status, its walls shielded against all external signals, its single window facing a bank of server stacks that ran the central processing for the entire eastern seaboard. The air conditioning was low, cold, and smelled of ozone and recycled paper.

Kain stood opposite her desk. He had spent the time between the hospital and the office running the third step of the cross-reference from a dead drop in a derelict library basement, using the three-part designation the man on the tram had given him: Reyes alias, QSA internal file, Voss operational history. The result had confirmed everything the man had said. The file was under Operative Delta-6 (Voss, E.). The operational history detailed the acquisition and sequestration of Martina Solis. The log was stamped with the same denial code that had appeared on the Reyes insurance claim. The seam was real.  
Emily sat at her desk with her arms crossed, not touching the terminal, her attention fixed entirely on him. She had run her own checks since his call from the hospital. She knew about the eleven-second delay on his last transmission. She knew about the unapproved cross-reference he had run from a civilian terminal. She knew about the logic of the three-step data chain that now pointed directly at her.

"You should have come back when I told you to," she said.

"I needed the third step." He set the data chip—the one containing the Solis journals—on the edge of her desk. "Operative Delta-6. Emily Voss."

She did not flinch. The stillness of her body was absolute—the professional containment of someone who had prepared for this moment for four years and had decided, long ago, that composure was the only remaining operational asset.

"The classification is accurate," she said. "The Solis journals were sequestered off-book to prevent the findings from being utilized by unauthorized divisions. The operation was approved by Division Director Tamsen."

"The operation sequestered Solis, but not her journals. You took them."

"I secured them. The intake log was corrupted. It was a failure of the retrieval protocol, not of the operational mandate."  
"The intake log was empty. The manifest was clean. You took them for four years and you’ve held them because the journals are the origin point of the replacement methodology. They tell the network how to defeat its own system."

He leaned across the desk, closing the physical distance, forcing the conversation onto the axis he required.

"Tell me about the ICS Log, Emily. The one you run on Hayden Marsh."

The question was a calculated strike, moving the conflict from the abstract past (Solis) to the immediate, emotional present (Bell and Hayden). 

The effect was immediate. A flicker of something crossed her face—not shock, but the fractional, momentary exposure of a nerve that she had thought was shielded.

"That's outside your clearance," she said. "That data is compartmentalized."

"The ICS log for Jump 380," Kain continued, ignoring her. "Where Hayden calculated that without Anchor Proximity, his trajectory projects full cascade within 14–20 jumps. And with Anchor Proximity, his window extends to 40–55 jumps. The ICS log where he documents his partner, Bell, as the Anchor Variable, and recommends continued non-disclosure to anchor variable because the cost of disclosure outweighs the cost of non-disclosure."  
He pushed the words across the desk: the clinical cruelty, the calculated betrayal, the perfect utilitarian logic that he had just seen mirrored in her own actions with Solis and himself.

"You approved that calculus," Kain said. "You approved the systematic deceit of Bell. You approved the instrument who calculated his partner's functional utility in terms of a jump window."

Emily’s eyes did not leave his. "I approved the operational window required to achieve mission success. The mission is the continuity of the QSA. The continuity of the QSA is the continuity of the civil structure. Hayden’s life, or Bell’s integrity, is secondary to that equation. So is yours."

"The man on the tram told me I’m terminal."

She finally moved. She released her hands from her shoulders, opened the top drawer of her desk, and placed a single piece of paper on the blotter between them. It was a printout of his final QSA Baseline Protocol report.

"Aspect's finding," she said. "The dormant carrier activation. Your ICS is not tracked, but the severity of your Bleed is. You are operating at an irreversible level of neurological degradation."

"How long."

"The median probability Aspect gave you—fourteen months—was a statistical kindness. It accounts for baseline degradation in a non-operational environment. You are in an operational environment, under extreme sleep and neurological stress, with repeated active Sphere exposure. You have run thirty-one jumps in the last four days alone, either through travel or the sensory collisions."  
She paused, allowing the clinical weight of the number to settle.

"The PQ genotype activation is a metabolic process. It consumes the micro-structural integrity of the cellular architecture. It's a terminal decline that does not respond to pharmacological intervention in the way Hayden’s ICS does, because the process itself is the body’s attempt to process the incompatible data load. The Bleed is not a symptom, Kain. It is the core mechanism of your body’s erasure."

"The number, Emily."

"You have two jumps left." She said it with the quiet certainty of someone stating an undeniable physical fact. "Two high-stress, full-spectrum transitions. The third will accelerate the neurological collapse to a point that you will not recover. You will fracture in transit and remain fragmented—a ghost in a reality that is not your own."

She leaned forward, her expression finally changing. It was not pity. It was the specific, terrible clarity of a utilitarian presenting a final ultimatum.

"The network knows this. The man on the tram told you what he told you because he knows you're terminal, and he knows that a terminal man is not manageable by the QSA. He gave you the file to send you off-network. He gave you the truth about Solis, and about Hayden and Bell, because he knew that the calculated cruelty of your institutional handler would be the final thing that broke your loyalty."

Kain looked at the printout on the desk—his name, his biometrics, his death sentence, written in QSA shorthand. The fading birthmark on his left shoulder, which he had felt during the drive, was not a clinical aside; it was the visible proof of his cellular integrity dissolving.

"What is the operational calculus now, Emily?" he asked. "The number I represent."

"I am offering you a choice," she said. "The QSA has a theoretical stabilization protocol for your specific variant, the TUBA1A-PQ genotype. It requires immediate, mandatory stand-down, twenty-four months of sequestration in the Threshold facility, and zero Sphere exposure. You will not investigate the network. You will not see Solis's journals. You will spend the next two years of your life in a sterile room under observation, but you will survive the Bleed."

"And if I refuse the stabilization protocol?"

"Then you complete your mission. You execute your final two jumps, you find the network, and the QSA gains the intelligence it requires to prevent the Accords from collapsing. You will be erased in transit, but you will have performed the ultimate operational function."

She waited for his answer, her stillness absolute.  
Kain looked at the data chip on the desk, then at the single piece of paper with his final ICS assessment. The choice was not between life and death; it was between being a prisoner of the system (Threshold) or becoming the final, lethal instrument of the system (the ultimate operational function). In both scenarios, he was a commodity.  
"The man on the tram gave me a name," Kain said, his voice flat. "He gave me a link to Solis. He gave me the truth about Hayden and Bell. And he gave me a message. That a terminal man stops waiting for permission."

He picked up the data chip. "The journals are mine. The investigation is mine. And the QSA is now compromised at a level that starts with you, Emily, and runs through every calculation you've ever approved."

"Kain, this is not negotiation. This is physics. You have two jumps left."

"I have two jumps left," he agreed. "And I'm using them for me."

He turned and walked toward the door, leaving her with the printout of his death certificate and the knowledge that her final, calculated utilitarian offer had failed.

"Where are you going?" she asked.

He stopped with his hand on the handle.   
"Off-network," he said. "The investigation is over. The pursuit begins now."

He left before she could move.

The Myki card went into the drain grate at the Victoria Parade stop.

Not his card — the secondary, registered to a Marcus Webb who had not existed since 2021 and whose QSA file had been administratively closed with a notation Kain had written himself: \*Subject deceased, no surviving architecture.\* The card had been in his left jacket pocket for eleven days, carried for exactly this contingency. The Pelco array tracked faces and Myki tap patterns simultaneously, cross-referencing movement vectors to build a predictive model of where a flagged subject was going. Without a tap, the model lost its anchor. Without an anchor, the system defaulted to facial recognition alone, which was slower, which required a human analyst to confirm the flag before a sweep team could be mobilised.

Human analysts worked in forty-minute review cycles.

He had, at most, thirty-eight minutes before a sweep team had a confirmed vector.

The phone went next — not into the drain, but powered down and left on the seat of the northbound tram at the Victoria Parade stop, tucked under the rear bench where it would not be found immediately but would eventually be found, which would give the Pelco system a stationary signal to chase while the analyst's review cycle ran. Emily would know it was a decoy within four minutes of the sweep team's arrival. The four minutes were not the point. The point was the analyst's attention — a stationary signal pulled focus from a moving one.

He walked south on Smith Street.

Not quickly. Speed was a behavioural flag. The Axis units on Smith Street were the older VicTrack model — fixed focal length, no thermal, a refresh rate of 1.4 seconds between frames — and at a walking pace, a man in a dark jacket was a man in a dark jacket. The facial recognition algorithm required 0.8 seconds of stable frontal exposure to return a confident match. He kept his chin at the angle that presented the orbital ridge to the camera rather than the full face — not a disguise, not evasion, simply the geometry of a man looking at the footpath ahead of him rather than at the sky.

At the intersection with Johnston Street he turned east, then immediately north through the laneway behind the terrace houses, then east again on the parallel street. The pattern was not random. It was the specific movement of someone who had memorised the Axis unit positions on this grid during the 2019 retrofit survey, when he had walked every street in a four-kilometre radius of the Fitzroy Node with a clipboard and a VicTrack contractor's badge and had noted, in a report that Emily had signed off on and filed and forgotten, exactly which corners had coverage gaps.

The gaps were still there. Infrastructure budgets did not close gaps. They documented them.

The Fitzroy Library's rear service entrance was on a lane that the 2019 survey had designated \*Coverage Class 4: Intermittent, non-continuous, insufficient for behavioural analysis.\* The designation meant a camera existed but its sightline was obstructed by the heritage oak that the council had refused to trim for seventeen consecutive years. The camera logged the lane's entrance and exit points. The middle forty metres were dark.

The service entrance lock was a Schlage B60N, the same model the QSA had specified for all 2019 retrofit sites on the grounds of standardisation. The master key on his keyring — the one he had never returned when the retrofit project closed, the one that had sat on the ring for four years as a habit rather than a plan — turned the cylinder in 0.8 seconds.

The door opened into the library's basement service corridor.

He pulled it closed behind him and stood in the dark for eleven seconds, letting his eyes adjust and running the calculation. The thermal purge protocol initiated from Alfred Hospital and moved outward along the fibre backbone at a rate of one junction every four minutes. The Fitzroy Node sat eleven junctions from Alfred. If Emily had initiated the purge the moment the proximity flag hit her terminal — which was the operationally correct decision, which meant it was what she had done — the purge had been running for approximately twenty-two minutes.

Twenty-two minutes. Five junctions severed. Six remaining.

Twenty-four minutes of connection left, at the outside.

The plant room was at the end of the service corridor, behind the HVAC control panel. The door was unlocked — plant rooms in public buildings were locked against the public, not against the building's own maintenance infrastructure, and the service corridor was maintenance infrastructure. Inside, the air handler occupied the left wall: a grey steel cabinet two metres tall, the primary fan housing bolted to the top, the ductwork running up through the ceiling in four parallel columns. The noise when it ran was not loud in the way of machinery but pervasive in the way of pressure — a low-frequency vibration that occupied the room's air rather than cutting through it, the kind of sound that made it impossible to hear anything below 400 Hz.

The warning tone for a junction severance was 220 Hz.

The bypass switch was on the control panel's lower left quadrant, a red toggle behind a hinged plastic cover, labelled in the standard VicTrack notation: \*AH PRIMARY CYCLE OVERRIDE — MANUAL HOLD REQUIRED.\* Manual hold. Not a latch. A toggle that required continuous physical pressure to maintain the override. The moment pressure was released, the air handler resumed its cycle.

The terminal was behind the HVAC cabinet, accessible by moving the cabinet's right side panel — four bolts, quarter-turn each, the panel swinging out on a hinge that had been installed during the 2019 retrofit and was not in the VicTrack maintenance records because it had not been installed by VicTrack. The terminal itself was a legacy unit: a 2019-era QSA field interface, matte grey, the screen no larger than a standard document, running the OS that predated the Pelco integration and had never been updated because it had been administratively decommissioned and administratively decommissioned systems did not receive updates.

It was also not registered as an active asset, which meant Emily's purge protocol did not know it existed.

He powered it on.

The boot sequence took forty seconds. He used them to unfold the paper from his jacket pocket.

The paper was a single sheet, printed in a monospaced font at a point size small enough to suggest the author had been conserving space rather than clarity. No header. No signature. Twelve lines of text arranged in three groups of four, each group separated by a blank line.

The first group was a string of alphanumeric characters: \*7F-3A-C2-19-B4-0E.\*

The second group was a directory path in the legacy OS notation: \*\\QSA\\ARCHIVE\\CONV\\\[KEY\]\\GEN.\*

The third group was a single instruction: \*The key is not the string. The key is the string's position in the intake manifest. Count from the bottom.\*

He had photographed the intake manifest in the conference room. The photograph was on the phone he had left on the tram. The manifest itself was in his working memory — not the full document, but the structure of it, the layout, the column headers, the order of the entries. He had read it for fourteen seconds and his working memory retained structured data at a fidelity that the QSA's intake assessment had rated at the 97th percentile for his cohort.

The intake manifest had listed subjects in reverse chronological order. Bottom of the list: the oldest entry. The string \*7F-3A-C2-19-B4-0E\* was six segments. Six segments, counted from the bottom of the manifest, produced six subject designations. The first letters of those six designations, in order, produced the directory key.

He ran it.

\*V. O. S. S. E. V.\*

The directory path resolved: \*\\QSA\\ARCHIVE\\CONV\\VOSSEV\\GEN.\*

The terminal's cursor blinked twice and opened the subdirectory.

One file. Designation: \*\*CONV-PQ-GEN-549.\*\*

Last modified: fourteen months ago.

*An absence of response.* Four years of fragmentation, of building workarounds — the grip on the glass, the pulse count, the specific route through the Pearl's car park that kept him away from the cycling sodium light. The laughing woman.

The specific, high, uncontrolled laugh — present in the corridor at Alfred, directional, occupying a physical position in space rather than arriving from behind his eyes. Not a hallucination. Not a symptom of damage. A residue of the Sphere's resonance, surfacing through the pathways the entanglement had opened, carrying a fragment of what the Sphere had been touching when his architecture was inside it.

Kain pressed the flat of his free hand against the wall.

Not the bypass switch — the wall itself, the aggregate concrete, the specific cold of a surface that had been absorbing this building's air conditioning for decades and would continue doing so after he left. He pressed it and held it and let the texture anchor the somatosensory cortex against the thing the table had just described.

Seven deployments when the fragmentation first presented. The file called that *low-frequency Bleed onset*. The fragmentation had been getting worse at a rate consistent with the distance between seven and wherever he currently was.

He counted. He arrived at eleven.

Twelve was next.

He kept reading because the alternative was to stop.

Not because the meaning required repetition. Because the meaning required the specific, physical duration of a second reading — the time it took for the data to move from the screen through the optic nerve and into the prefrontal cortex where the microtubules were, where the *TUBA1A-PQ* variant was, where the secondary binding site was degrading at a rate the QSA had been measuring for fourteen months and had not told him about, and where the number 11 sat against the number 14 with a gap of three deployments between them and a median of four to seven months between deployment 14 and ICS collapse.

The terminal's cache timer ran to zero and the screen went dark.

The plant room was very quiet. The air handler had completed its cycle. The bypass switch was in its default position. The fibre connection was gone and the terminal was dark and the Fitzroy Node was offline and the QSA's thermal purge had reached junction eleven and there was nothing left in the room to read.

He stood in the dark for a specific, measured duration — not shock, not grief, not the melodrama of a man confronting his own mortality, but the operational pause of a man updating his model of the environment he was operating in. The model had contained an error. The error was now corrected. The corrected model contained the following data points:

Eleven deployments. Buffer at 19% on the next. Emily had the journals. Emily had the file. Emily had recruited him knowing both. The M. Reyes alias pointed to Emily's operational designation. The third step of the cross-reference, which he had now completed, confirmed that the operational history attached to that designation included fourteen months of data on his own degradation.

The sweep team would have a vector in eleven minutes.

He put the folded paper back in his jacket pocket, moved the HVAC cabinet's side panel back into position, turned the four bolts a quarter-turn each, and walked back through the service corridor to the rear entrance. The heritage oak's canopy covered the lane's middle forty metres. The Axis unit at the lane's entrance logged his exit at the edge of its sightline — a dark jacket, a walking pace, a chin angle that presented the orbital ridge rather than the full face.

A man in a dark jacket.
