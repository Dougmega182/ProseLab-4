# **PROLOGUE** 

The reflection in the window finished tying its shoe a half-second late.

Chen held still, fingers frozen on the laces, and counted his own heartbeats. Outside, the Melbourne skyline caught the early light. He had looked at that view every morning for three years and it had never looked back.

He straightened slowly. His service weapon was twelve steps away, in the bedroom safe. His QSA comm device was three steps away, in the desk drawer.

He took the three steps.

The front door opened.

Chen dropped behind the kitchen counter and pressed his back against the cabinet. The comm device was in his hand before he'd consciously decided to reach for it. He activated it the way he'd activated it a hundred times on active cases — thumb, thumb, index — and set it on the floor beside him, recording; its small indicator light masked by his palm.

"—yes, I'll have the Chen report ready by Thursday. The dimensional variance data is still being processed."

His own voice. His own cadence. His current caseload.

The refrigerator opened. A mug clinked against the counter.

Chen moved on instinct, crossing to the bookshelf in four silent steps. He pulled *The Maltese Falcon* from the shelf and reversed it — spine facing in, violating his father's strictest rule. — then moved back before the footsteps reached the hallway.

The shower started.

Twelve minutes. Maybe fourteen. He'd taken a seven-minute shower every morning for three years and the replacement would likely not know that, would take whatever time it wanted, and that gave him the kitchen table, the hook by the door, the laminated schedule he updated every Sunday.

He checked the schedule first. The replacement had written it in his hand — close enough that his own graphology team wouldn't flag it, not close enough that he would miss it. The pressure was wrong. The spacing between letters was a fraction wide, the pressure of someone reconstructing rather than producing. He photographed it with the comm device and set it back exactly where it had been.

O seven hundred workout.  O seven thirty tram. Coffee with cream.

He took the tram at o seven fifteen. He drank his coffee black. He had worked out at o six hundred every morning since the divorce because the only thing he could control at six a.m was whether he got out of bed.

None of that was in any QSA file.

He moved to the hook by the door. His security pass hung from the bottom hook. He had used the top hook for three years — not a decision, but a habit—an accumulation of doing the same thing enough times that the reasoning dissolved and only the motion remained. He left the pass where the replacement had put it and moved instead to the kitchen counter.

The white mug. He had never used the white mug.

The shower stopped.

And for a moment he simply looked at what was in front of him. His apartment. His books. His laminated schedule. His father's novel, facing the wrong way. The person who had assembled these things had been very careful and had not been careful enough.

The bathroom door opened.

He moved toward the front door.

The replacement stepped into the hallway and his own face looked back at him across the length of the room, and the expression on it was not surprise.

Recognition.

"You're still here," the replacement said. "That's not supposed to happen."

"Who are you."

"I'm you." The smile was his smile and completely wrong, the muscles moving through the correct positions without arriving at the correct result. "The version of you this reality needs."

"You're a copy."

"I'm an improvement." The replacement stepped forward. "You were flagged eighteen months ago. Behavioural inconsistencies. The Agency decided—"

"The Agency doesn't replace its own agents."

"The QSA you know doesn't."

It moved faster than he was prepared for — faster than any calibration he had for a human body — and his wrist gave with a wet pop at the joint, the angle arriving before the pain did, and he hit the floor and the comm device skittered across the hardwood. 

The replacement retrieved the device from where it had skittered. Turned it over once, found the control surface, pressed stop — with the precision of something that had identified a threat and neutralised it. Then it turned toward Chen. The body gave no preparatory warning, no lean, no gathering of weight before commitment. The distance between them ceased to exist in the way a frame gets cut from film — Chen had one clear impression of the room's geometry being wrong.

The replacement stood over him with the device still in one hand. It looked at him like a solved problem. 

"You left evidence," it said. 

"That's very you. Very pattern-oriented." It turned the device once more. 

"But evidence requires someone to find it. Someone to understand it. And the people who might understand are already optimised." 

Chen pushed himself up on his good arm. His wrist hung wrong. 

"Someone will notice," he said. 

"Someone will see the differences." 

"The differences are the point." The replacement crouched beside him. 

"You took the 7:15 tram because you were afraid of crowds. You drank black coffee because you were punishing yourself for the divorce. You worked out at 6:00 because you couldn't sleep past five anyway." 

The smile again, the muscles achieving their positions without arriving at the expression. 

"I take the 7:30 tram because I'm confident. I drink coffee with cream because I allow myself small pleasures. I work out at 7:00 because I sleep well." It leaned closer. 

"I'm not copying your life, Marcus. I'm living the life you were too broken to live. And no one—no one—will miss the version of you that was afraid of everything." 

Chen looked at the device in the replacement's hand. The recording had stopped. But the device had a secondary function — a dead-man's switch that transmitted the last sixty seconds to a secure QSA server if the agent's biometrics flatlined while in contact with the casing. 

He just had to touch it. 

He closed his hand around the device where the replacement held it — not wrenching, not lunging, just his palm pressing against the casing, his biometrics alive against the contact surface. 

The replacement looked down at his hand. Recognition moved through its face — not quite fast enough. It began to pry his fingers back with its free hand. Chen held on. The replacement's grip shifted. Its fingers found his throat with mechanical precision, pressing against the carotid with the accuracy of something that did not need to estimate. Chen's vision narrowed. His pulse throbbed through his palm against the casing, slowing, slowing. He held on. His own face hovered above him. The device warmed between their hands. 

The Melbourne skyline caught the morning light through the window and the room was entirely ordinary except for the two men in it and what was being decided between them. 

Someone will find this. 

The device emitted a single, soft beep.
