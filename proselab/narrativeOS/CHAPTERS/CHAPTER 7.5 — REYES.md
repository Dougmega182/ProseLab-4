# **CHAPTER 7.5 — REYES**

The call came through a relay that added four seconds of latency and routed through three jurisdictions before it reached the phone in Reyes's coat pocket. Four seconds was the price of operational security. Reyes had been paying it for eleven years and had stopped noticing the pause the way people stopped noticing the sound of their own breathing.

The café occupied the ground floor of a building on Flinders Lane, narrow-fronted, the kind of establishment that had survived three cycles of Melbourne's hospitality economy by being exactly what it was without apology — dark wood, espresso machines that predated the quantum era's aesthetic preferences, a clientele that came for the coffee and stayed because the ambient noise level made conversation private without requiring effort. Reyes sat at the corner table with a long black and a newspaper that had not been read and answered on the second ring.

"The investigator made contact," the replacement said. Its voice carried the slight flatness of someone maintaining a vocal register they had learned rather than developed. Even through four seconds of relay latency, the quality was audible to someone who knew what to listen for. "Alfred Hospital, second floor research corridor. He used a Level 4 QSA field credential for the access points."

"Did he make the visual?"

"Partial. I terminated the engagement before he could complete it."

Reyes set the newspaper down. "How partial?"

"Three seconds at four meters. Corridor lighting. He was experiencing a neurological episode during the final approach — I observed the onset. His motor control degraded. He went to one knee."

The café's espresso machine produced a sound like a controlled detonation. Reyes waited for it to finish. "He went to one knee in the corridor."

"Yes."

"And you terminated the engagement."

"The withdrawal protocol was the operative instruction."

Reyes picked up the long black and held it without drinking. The ceramic was warm against the palm — a specific, grounding warmth, the kind that came from a cup that had been filled recently and had not yet begun to cool. "The withdrawal protocol applies to standard investigative contact. A dormant carrier running an active investigation with a documented neurological episode in the field is not standard investigative contact." A pause. "What was the episode?"

"Environmental superimposition. He pressed both hands against the wall and counted. Approximately fifteen seconds total duration. He recovered full motor function before I exited the room."

Fifteen seconds. Reyes ran the arithmetic without moving. A dormant carrier, activated by the Sphere's entanglement field, running an investigation under a 1.4x Bleed multiplier from sleep deprivation, experiencing environmental superimposition severe enough to produce motor failure. The medical literature on dormant carrier degradation was not widely available — the QSA had classified the relevant research within six months of the programme's first operational results. But Reyes had read the unclassified precursors, the papers that had been published before the classification order, and the arithmetic they contained was not complicated.

The carrier was approaching a threshold.

"What is his current operational status?" Reyes asked.

"Active. He returned to the atrium after the episode. He was on his phone — I assume coordinating with his handler. He re-entered the research wing at ten twenty-two."

"He came back."

"Yes."

Reyes set the cup down. The ceramic made a small sound against the wood of the table — precise, contained, the sound of something placed rather than dropped. "A dormant carrier experiencing motor failure from a Bleed episode, operating on thirty-plus hours without sleep, with a partial visual on a network asset, came back to the location where the asset had already made him." A pause. "What does that tell you?"

The replacement did not answer immediately. The four-second relay latency made the silence longer than it was. "That he prioritizes the investigation over his own physical integrity."

"It tells you he already knows he's terminal," Reyes said. "A man who believes he has time manages his resources. A man who knows he doesn't spends them." The espresso machine produced another controlled detonation. Reyes waited. "Who is his handler?"

"The credential was issued under QSA field operations. The handler identity is not in the accessible personnel registry."

"It's Emily Voss." Reyes said it the way people say things they have known for long enough that the knowing has become structural. "She's been running him since the Solis investigation. She has the journals."

Another pause — this one longer than the relay latency accounted for. "If Voss has the journals, she has the origin point of the replacement methodology."

"She has the journals and she hasn't used them," Reyes said. "Which means she's holding them for something. Which means she has her own operational agenda that is not identical to the carrier's agenda." The long black had cooled to the temperature at which coffee became drinkable rather than impressive. Reyes drank it. "This is the situation. You have a dormant carrier who is terminal and knows it, running an investigation he cannot complete before the Bleed takes him, being handled by an operator who is withholding the one piece of information that would allow him to complete it. Do you understand what that situation is?"

"An asset," the replacement said.

"An asset," Reyes agreed. "The network has been looking for a dormant carrier with active Sphere exposure for fourteen months. The QSA's programme produced eleven. Three survived. Two of the three survivors are non-functional. The third is in a care facility and has not spoken in four years." A pause. "This carrier is functional. He is degrading, but he is functional, and he is motivated, and he is already inside the investigation. The network does not need to recruit him. The network needs to point him."

"Point him at what?"

"At Voss." Reyes stood up from the table, leaving the newspaper and the empty cup. The café's ambient noise continued its work of making conversation private. "She has the journals. He doesn't know she has them — or he knows and hasn't moved on it yet, which amounts to the same operational problem. If the carrier discovers that his handler has been withholding the origin point of the replacement methodology, he will stop trusting her. If he stops trusting her, he stops being manageable. If he stops being manageable, he becomes unpredictable." A pause. "Unpredictable dormant carriers with active Sphere exposure and terminal Bleed progression are not useful to anyone."

"You want to tell him about the journals."

"I want him to find out about the journals in a way that feels like his own discovery." Reyes moved toward the café's door, unhurried, the coat buttoned against the morning's rain. "The intake form. The M. Reyes name. He photographed it."

"Yes."

"Good. That name connects to a QSA internal file that connects to Voss's operational history. The connection is not obvious — it requires three steps of cross-referencing that a Level 4 field credential can access but that a handler would not expect a field operative to pursue independently." A pause at the door. "He will pursue it independently. That is what terminal men do — they stop waiting for permission."

"And when he finds the connection to Voss?"

"He will confront her. She will make a decision about whether to show him the journals. If she shows him, the investigation accelerates and the network benefits from the carrier's remaining functional capacity. If she doesn't show him—" Reyes pushed through the door into the rain. "Then the carrier becomes unpredictable, and the network has a different kind of asset."

The call ended.

Reyes walked north on Flinders Lane in the rain, past the gallery frontages and the loading bays. He moved through the particular Melbourne architecture of laneways designed for service access that had become, over decades of incremental human decision-making, spaces people chose to inhabit.

The rain came down on the bluestone, the wet street reflecting the morning's grey light. Reyes walked through it with the unhurried efficiency of a man who had already made the only decision that mattered, simply moving through the time that followed it.

The carrier's name was Kain.

Reyes had known about him for six weeks — since the first anomaly in the B-734 investigation had flagged a dormant carrier with active Sphere exposure in the QSA's field operative registry. Six weeks of watching him work, watching the Bleed progress, watching him make the specific decisions that terminal men made: the decision to continue, the decision to withhold his own degradation from the people around him, the decision to go back to the location where he had already been made rather than retreat and reassess.

The network had not created Kain's situation. The network had simply recognized it for what it was.

A dormant carrier with active Sphere exposure, terminal Bleed progression, and a handler who was withholding the origin point of the replacement methodology was not a threat to the network. He was a mechanism. A mechanism that the network had not built and did not need to maintain and would not be responsible for when it reached the end of its operational life.

The rain intensified slightly. Reyes turned up the collar of the coat and kept walking.

The carrier had perhaps thirty jumps left before the Bleed became irreversible. Thirty jumps, or the equivalent neurological load from continued Sphere exposure without jumping — the medical literature was ambiguous on the precise conversion rate, but the order of magnitude was consistent. Thirty jumps. At the current pace of the investigation, that was enough.

Probably enough.

Reyes turned onto Swanston Street and disappeared into the morning crowd, and the rain came down on Melbourne, and somewhere in Alfred Hospital a man with thirty jumps left was looking at an empty room and deciding what to do next, and the network had already decided what it wanted him to do, and the distance between those two things was the operational space the network intended to occupy.
