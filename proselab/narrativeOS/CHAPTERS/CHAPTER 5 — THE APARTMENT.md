# **CHAPTER 5 — THE APARTMENT**

The intake station occupied the last twenty metres of the Threshold facility's exit corridor — a narrowing of the passage that was architectural rather than accidental, designed to ensure that nothing left the building without passing through the sensor array embedded in the walls on both sides. The array was passive. It did not ask. It read.

The technician behind the desk was a woman in her thirties with the specific economy of movement that belonged to people who performed the same precise actions many times a day and had eliminated everything unnecessary. She did not look up when Kain approached. She held out her right hand, palm up, without breaking her attention from the terminal in front of her.

He placed his wrist against the scanner she held. The device was warm from continuous use. It ran its read in four seconds — the standard biometric sweep, pulse and temperature and the subcutaneous markers that the QSA used to track personnel movement through secured facilities.

And then the PQ genotype flag. He knew the moment it hit because the technician's eyes moved — not to him, not to the terminal, but to a point approximately thirty centimetres to the left of both, the specific unfocused gaze of someone processing information they had not expected and were now deciding what to do with.

One second. Two.

She withdrew the scanner. Her eyes returned to the terminal. Her fingers moved across the input surface with the same precise economy as before, logging the exit, completing the record.

"Clear," she said.

He walked through.

The corridor beyond the intake station ran twelve metres to the facility's outer door. He walked it at the same pace he had walked every other corridor in the building — not slow, not fast, the pace of someone with somewhere to be and no reason to hurry. His right hand was in his jacket pocket, thumb against the seam. The tremor ran its low, reliable frequency.

Behind him, the technician's terminal would now carry a flag in his biometric record. Not a diagnosis — the QSA's intake system didn't carry diagnoses, only markers. But the marker would be there. And whoever had access to the intake logs would see it.

He filed this and kept walking.

---

Chen's apartment was in Southbank, on the fourteenth floor of a building that had been constructed in 2139 and had aged with the particular grace of structures built during the period when architects had briefly believed that quantum-adjacent materials would self-maintain. They did not self-maintain. The building's facade carried the faint iridescent patina of coherence-reactive polymer that had been exposed to too many years of Melbourne's variable electromagnetic environment and had begun, at the molecular level, to give up.

Emily had given him the access code at 6:00 AM, before he left Threshold. She had not come with him. She had a meeting with the QSA's internal liaison — the official version of the investigation, the one that existed on paper and pointed in a direction that was not the direction Kain was walking. He understood the division of labour. He did not entirely trust it.

The building's lobby held a Gen-3 autom-unit at the concierge station — older than the Pearl's Gen-4, its joint articulation carrying the micro-hesitation that marked the generation. It registered his entry and cross-referenced his access code without comment. The lift took him to fourteen.

Chen's door was sealed with a QSA evidence lock — a secondary device clamped over the building's standard biometric panel, its surface carrying the agency's classification stripe in the same blue tape that marked the Threshold facility's internal doors. He entered the code. The lock released.

He put on gloves. He went in.

---

The apartment held the specific quality of a space that had been lived in and then stopped. Not abandoned — the QSA's forensic team had been through it, and forensic teams left a particular kind of order behind them, the order of people who had moved everything and replaced it with precision and had thereby made the space feel like a reconstruction of itself rather than the thing itself. The furniture was where it had been. The objects were where they had been. The air carried the faint chemical signature of the scanning agents the team had used on the surfaces.

Kain stood in the doorway for thirty seconds and looked at the room without moving through it.

The living area ran left to right — kitchen at the far end, a window facing north toward the river, a desk against the eastern wall, a sofa, a low table. Standard configuration for a fourteen-floor Southbank apartment of this size. Nothing immediately wrong.

He started at the bookshelf.

The *Maltese Falcon* was there — a physical paperback, its spine facing out, positioned on the third shelf from the top between a hardbound collection of Chandler stories and a QSA policy manual that Chen had apparently kept at home. The spine faced out. That was correct. The QSA's passive optical record had documented the reversal — the spine turned inward, the title hidden — as one of the eighteen-month anomalies. The forensic team had replaced it spine-out, which was where it had been for nine years before the replacement arrived.

Kain pulled it from the shelf.

A left-handed man who read physical books developed specific habits around them. The spine crease — the point where the book had been held open most often — concentrated on the left side of the binding in a left-handed reader, because the left hand carried the book's weight while the right turned pages. The *Maltese Falcon*'s spine crease ran down the right side.

The replacement had held it in the right hand. For eighteen months, every time it read this book, it had held it in the right hand because the right hand was dominant and the residue — Chen's left-hand dominance, encoded in nine years of muscle memory — had not extended to the grip on a paperback. Too small a habit. Too far below the threshold of the things the replacement had been given to memorize.

He replaced the book and moved to the bathroom.

---

The shower's water temperature control was a manual dial — a heritage fitting that the building had retained during the 2139 construction because the developer had been selling the apartments on the basis of their period detailing. The dial ran from blue to red, with Chen's preferred setting marked by a faint calcium deposit at the seven-o'clock position — the accumulated residue of nine years of the same temperature, the same duration, the same hand reaching for the same point on the dial.

The QSA's passive monitoring had flagged the shower duration anomaly: fourteen minutes, up from Chen's established average of eight. The forensic team had noted it. Their report had classified it as a lifestyle variation.

Kain looked at the dial.

The calcium deposit was at the seven-o'clock position. The dial's current setting — where it had been left, last used — was at nine o'clock. Warmer. The replacement ran the shower hotter than Chen had. Not dramatically hotter. Two positions on a dial. The kind of difference that registered as preference rather than discontinuity.

But the fourteen minutes was not about preference. The replacement ran the shower for fourteen minutes because it needed fourteen minutes. Eight minutes had been sufficient for Chen because Chen had been washing a body he had inhabited for fifty-one years and knew with the automatic efficiency of long familiarity. The replacement needed the additional six minutes because it was washing a body it was still learning — still checking, still mapping, still running its hands across the surface of a physical form that was not its original and that it had not yet made automatic.

Fourteen minutes was the duration of a replacement that had not yet stopped being careful.

He left the bathroom and crossed to the desk.

---

The desk was left-handed. Not in its construction — it was a standard rectangular surface, nothing custom — but in its configuration. The terminal sat on the right side of the desk, which placed the keyboard in the center and left the left side clear: the working space, the space where a left-handed man spread papers and rested his arm and kept the things he reached for without thinking. The QSA's policy manual had been on the left side for nine years, according to the passive optical record. The forensic team had replaced it there.

The desk lamp was on the right side.

Kain looked at the lamp. Then at the terminal. Then at the clear space on the left.

A left-handed man positioned his desk lamp on the left — not because he had thought about it, but because the lamp needed to illuminate the working space, and the working space was on the left, and the positioning of the lamp was the kind of decision that got made once, in the first week of using a desk, and never revisited. The passive optical record showed the lamp on the left for nine years.

The forensic team had replaced it on the right.

He checked the optical record on his tablet — the full eighteen-month log, which Emily had transferred to his device before he left Threshold. He scrolled to the lamp.

Month one of the anomaly period: lamp moves from left to right. Flagged by the passive system as a workspace reorganization event. Classified within normal parameters.

The replacement had moved the lamp because it was right-handed and the lamp on the left side was in its way. It had not known the lamp's position was a record. It had not known that nine years of left-side positioning had left a faint circular mark in the desk's surface — the slight compression of the material under the lamp's base, visible only at a low angle with the overhead light off.

Kain crouched. Angled his tablet's light across the desk surface.

The compression mark was on the left side. The lamp was on the right.

He stood up.

---

The peripheral fragmentation hit without warning — his left visual field dropping out for a full second, the eastern wall of the apartment simply absent, replaced by the specific non-colour of a visual cortex running without input. He stopped moving. Pressed two fingers to his carotid. Counted.

Eighty-eight. High.

The fragmentation passed. The eastern wall returned — the desk, the terminal, the lamp on the wrong side, all of it reassembling with the slightly-too-sharp quality that followed a Bleed episode, the visual cortex overcorrecting for the interruption.

One second. That was longer than the Fitzroy North episode four years ago, which had been half a second. Longer than the car park behind the Pearl two nights ago, which had been three-quarters.

The window was fourteen months at the median. He was not at the median's beginning.

He filed this and kept working.

---

The kitchen drawer beside the sink was the last thing.

Chen's passive optical record showed a consistent pattern across nine years: the drawer opened with the left hand, always, because it was positioned to the left of the sink and a left-handed man reaching for it while standing at the sink reached with his dominant hand. The drawer held the items Chen used most frequently at the sink — the dish brush, the small knife for cutting fruit, the cloth he used to wipe the counter.

The drawer's handle carried a wear pattern. Nine years of the same grip, the same angle of approach, the same pressure — the metal slightly more polished on the left side of the handle, where the thumb of a left hand had contacted it thousands of times.

The wear was on the left side of the handle.

The smudge on the handle — the most recent contact, still faintly present on the brushed metal surface — was on the right side.

Kain stood at the kitchen sink and looked at the drawer handle and thought about Martina Solis writing on page forty-three of her second journal: *The replacement does not know why it reaches for the coffee with the left hand. It simply does.*

The replacement in Chen's apartment had not reached for the drawer with the left hand. It had reached with the right. Because the residue — Chen's left-hand dominance — had not encoded the drawer. The drawer was too small. Too peripheral. The replacement had been given Chen's major habits, his professional patterns, his institutional memory. It had not been given the drawer.

Nobody had given it the drawer because nobody had thought the drawer mattered.

Kain photographed the handle. The compression mark on the desk. The shower dial. The spine crease in the *Maltese Falcon*. He logged each one against the passive optical record, timestamped, with a notation that cross-referenced the residue theory from Solis's journal.

Four anomalies. Four instances of a right-handed person moving through a left-handed life and leaving the specific, invisible evidence of the mismatch in the places too small to memorize and too habitual to notice.

The QSA's instruments had missed all of them.

He stood at the kitchen window and looked at the river fourteen floors below — the Yarra running its brown, patient course through the city, indifferent to the apartment above it and the dead man who had lived in it and the thing that had lived in it after him.

His right hand was in his jacket pocket. The tremor ran its frequency. His left visual field held.

He had four anomalies and a methodology and fourteen months, median probability.

He took out his tablet and began writing the report.
