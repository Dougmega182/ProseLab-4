# **CHAPTER 7 — THE MIRROR HUNT**

Emily's call came at nine-fourteen.

Kain sat in the atrium, in the chair with the sightline to the elevator bank. A porter wheeled an empty gurney toward the service corridor with the particular unhurried efficiency of someone whose route had become muscular rather than cognitive. The atrium had filled in the twenty minutes since he had sat down — the morning's patient load arriving in the specific rhythm of a hospital that ran on appointment schedules, people moving through the space with the focused purposefulness of those who knew where they were going and the slower, more uncertain movement of those who did not.

He answered before the second ring.

"Winters badged in at seven fifty-two this morning," Emily said. No preamble. "That's forty-three minutes earlier than her average arrival time across the last eleven years of entry logs. The standard deviation on her arrival time is six minutes. Forty-three minutes is seven standard deviations outside her mean."

"What's her earliest recorded arrival?"

"Eight twenty-one. Three years ago. The day her research grant was approved."

Seven standard deviations. The number was not a statistical anomaly. It was a signature — the specific kind of error a replacement made when it had absorbed the subject's external schedule but not the internal logic that governed it. Winters arrived when her work required her to arrive. The replacement had arrived when its operational timeline required it to arrive, and had not understood that those two things were different.

"The nine o'clock appointment," Kain said.

"Booked four days ago through the hospital's internal scheduling system using Winters's credentials. The patient name is redacted, but the appointment type is listed as a follow-up consultation." Emily paused. "Winters doesn't do follow-up consultations. Her research clinic runs initial assessments and data collection. Follow-ups go to the clinical team."

"The replacement booked an appointment with its own network contact. Using Winters's system access to create a legitimate-looking meeting."

"That's the most probable interpretation."

"I need the floor plan for the second-floor research wing. Every exit, every camera position, every room that connects to the corridor outside the clinic."

"Sending now."

The floor plan arrived on his phone thirty seconds later — a clean architectural drawing annotated in Emily's hand with camera positions in red and exit routes in blue. He studied it for forty seconds, building the geometry of the space in his mind, mapping the replacement's most probable movement patterns against the available exits.

The replacement had arrived forty-three minutes early. The nine o'clock appointment had ended fourteen minutes ago. The replacement remained in the clinic, moved through the building, or had already vanished.

He stood and moved toward the elevator bank.

\*\*\*

People now occupied the second-floor corridor. A shift change had occurred sometime in the last twenty minutes — the nurses' station at the far end staffed by two people, the three closed doors on the left side of the corridor carrying the particular quality of spaces that were occupied rather than vacant. A cleaner moved a mop bucket along the far wall with the slow, methodical progress of someone working to a pattern rather than a deadline. A man in a visitor's lanyard stood outside the middle door with his arms crossed and his weight on his left foot, the posture of someone who had been waiting long enough to stop pretending he was comfortable with it.

Kain walked the corridor at the pace of someone who belonged there — not hurried, not casual, the specific tempo of a person with a destination and a reason for it. He passed the nurses' station without stopping, his attention on the appointment board. The nine o'clock slot showed a check mark beside the redacted patient name. The ten-thirty data review session was unchanged.

The nine o'clock appointment was complete. The replacement had been in this corridor within the last fourteen minutes.

He stopped at the window overlooking the internal courtyard and used his phone screen as a partial mirror to observe the corridor behind him without turning. The cleaner. The man with the visitor's lanyard. A nurse moving between the station and the far door. No one whose movement pattern carried the specific quality he was looking for.

The residue theory was not complicated in principle. A replacement absorbed the subject's external presentation — appearance, credentials, behavioral habits documented in observable records. What it could not absorb was the subject's relationship to physical space. The way a person moved through a familiar environment was the product of years of accumulated micro-decisions: which side of the corridor they favored, how they navigated around obstacles, the specific rhythm of their interaction with doors and equipment they had used hundreds of times. A replacement navigating Winters's environment for the first time moved through it with a fractional excess of attention — the attention of someone reading a space rather than inhabiting it.

He needed to find that excess.

He turned from the window and walked to the nurses' station.

The nurse behind the desk was in her mid-thirties, her attention split between a screen and a printed schedule she was annotating in pencil. She looked up when he reached the counter.

"I'm looking for Dr. Winters," he said. He produced the QSA field credential — not the full identification, which would trigger a reporting protocol, but the civilian liaison card that established him as a government health researcher. "I had a nine o'clock with her. I think I may have the wrong floor."

The nurse's expression moved through three registers in under two seconds: mild irritation at the interruption, the professional recalibration that followed, and then something more careful. "Dr. Winters's nine o'clock was in room two-fourteen. That's—" She stopped. "Can I ask what this is regarding?"

"Cohort data review. She's been consulting on a neurological study we're running through the health department."

The nurse looked at the schedule in front of her, then at the screen. "Dr. Winters checked out of the building at nine-twelve."

Nine-twelve. The appointment had ended at nine, or close to it. The replacement had been in the building for twelve minutes after the appointment concluded — long enough to remove the white coat, retrieve whatever it had come for, and execute the withdrawal.

"Did she say where she was going?"

"She doesn't usually—" The nurse stopped again. The careful expression returned, more pronounced this time. "Is there something wrong?"

"No," Kain said. "I must have the time wrong. I'll reschedule."

He moved away from the station before she could ask another question.

Room two-fourteen was the second door on the left — the one with the locked keycard reader he had noted on his first pass through the corridor. He stopped outside it and looked at the door without touching it. The keycard reader's indicator light showed green. Someone had unlocked it recently and not re-engaged the lock on exit.

He pushed the door open.

The room was small — a consultation space, not a research lab. Two chairs facing each other across a low table, a desk against the far wall with a monitor and keyboard, a single window overlooking the internal courtyard. The desk surface was clear except for a pen and a printed form that had been placed face-down. He turned the form over.

It was a standard patient intake document, blank except for a name in the top field: \*M. Reyes.\* No date of birth, no contact information, no medical history. The name had been written in a hand that was careful rather than natural — the deliberate letter formation of someone copying a signature they had practiced rather than producing their own.

He photographed the form and left the room.

\*\*\*

The staff corridor beyond the STAFF ONLY door was narrower, lower-ceilinged, the institutional green replaced with unpainted concrete. Service infrastructure ran along the upper walls — conduit, pipe, the bundled cabling of a building retrofitted with successive generations of technology without ever being fully rewired. The lighting was fluorescent and unshielded, tubes mounted directly to the ceiling without diffusers, producing a harsh, flat illumination that eliminated shadow and made the space feel simultaneously exposed and claustrophobic.

The elevator at the far end of the main corridor had opened while he was in room two-fourteen. He had not seen who stepped out. But the STAFF ONLY door showed the same tell as the keycard reader in two-fourteen — the mechanism not fully returned to its resting position, the door sitting a millimeter proud of the frame.

Someone had come through it in the last four minutes.

He pushed through into the staff corridor.

A woman stood twenty meters ahead of him.

She wore a white coat over dark clothing, a hospital ID badge on a lanyard, and carried a tablet in her left hand. Her hair was dark, pulled back, and her age was consistent with the photograph in Winters's personnel file. She moved down the corridor at a pace that was correct — not hurried, not uncertain — her attention on the tablet in her hand.

But her left hand was wrong.

The tablet was in her left hand, and she scrolled with her right thumb, which meant the tablet was oriented with the screen facing away from her body. Winters was left-handed. The personnel file noted it in the medical history section — a minor detail, the kind that appeared in records because it was clinically relevant to certain neurological assessments. A left-handed person holding a tablet in their left hand would orient it with the screen facing their body, scrolling with their left thumb or their right index finger. The orientation the woman used was the orientation of a right-handed person who had been told to carry the tablet in their left hand.

Kain moved after her.

She turned left at the end of the corridor, through a door marked STAFF ONLY that she opened with a keycard without breaking stride. He reached the door four seconds after it closed and used his Level 4 clearance to follow her through.

The corridor beyond was narrower still — a service passage, the walls bare concrete, the floor a rubberized surface that absorbed footsteps. The woman was fifteen meters ahead. He maintained the distance, keeping her in his peripheral vision rather than his direct sightline, moving with the same unhurried tempo she used.

She stopped.

Not the stop of someone who had reached a destination. The stop of someone who had registered a presence behind them and was deciding what to do about it. She stood with her back to him for three full seconds, the tablet still in her left hand, the screen still facing away from her body.

Then she turned around.

Her face was Winters's face — the same bone structure, the same coloring, the same arrangement of features that appeared in the personnel file photograph. But the expression was wrong. Winters's photograph showed a woman in her mid-forties with the particular quality of attention that came from years of research work — focused, slightly distracted, the expression of someone whose mind was always partially elsewhere. The expression on the face in front of him was different. It was the expression of someone running a rapid assessment of a situation and arriving at a conclusion.

"Can I help you?" she said.

The voice was correct. The cadence was correct. The slight upward inflection at the end of the question was consistent with the audio recordings in Winters's public lecture archive.

"I think I'm turned around," Kain said. "I'm looking for the research clinic."

"You're on the wrong floor." She pointed back the way he had come. "Main corridor, elevator to two, turn left."

"Thank you."

He turned and walked back toward the door. Behind him, her footsteps resumed  — not in the direction she had been heading, but back the way she had come. The assessment had concluded. The withdrawal protocol had activated.

He went through the door and stopped on the other side of it, his back against the wall, and counted to five.

Then he went back through.

The corridor was empty.

\*\*\*

The Bleed arrived without warning at the corner where the service passage turned toward the fire exit stairwell.

It did not arrive as a gap this time. It arrived as an addition — a layer of sensory data superimposed over the concrete corridor with a completeness that made the corridor itself seem like the intrusion. The baseline room. The room from the Sphere's calibration sequence, with its too-white walls and the specific quality of ozone that the entanglement field generated as a byproduct of the magnetic levitation system. The smell arrived first — sharp, chemical, the smell of air ionized by eleven megawatts of continuous electromagnetic output. Then the walls, pressing in from the periphery of his visual field, white over concrete, the two surfaces occupying the same space with equal insistence.

He stopped walking.

The concrete, conduit, and fluorescent tubes remained. The building's ventilation system hummed somewhere below the floor. But the baseline room existed with equal force, its ozone smell overriding the concrete dust and recycled air of the service passage, its white walls asserting themselves against the grey of the unpainted surface in front of him. The two environments did not alternate. They coexisted, each one fully present, each one demanding the full processing capacity of a nervous system that could not divide itself between them without losing coherence in both.

He pressed his right hand flat against the concrete wall.

The texture was specific — aggregate visible in the surface, the slight roughness of unfinished concrete against his palm, the cold of a material that had been absorbing the building's air conditioning for decades. He held the sensation and used it as an anchor. The baseline room's ozone smell did not disappear. It receded by degrees, the way a sound recedes when you move away from its source — still present, still identifiable, but no longer dominant.

The white walls pulled back to the periphery of his visual field.

Eleven seconds. He had lost eleven seconds.

He went around the corner.

The corridor beyond was empty. Thirty meters of straight concrete, two doors on the right side, a fire exit at the far end with a push bar and a small reinforced window. The push bar showed the same displacement he had learned to read — the mechanism not fully returned to its resting position, the left end sitting a fraction of a millimeter proud of the housing.

Someone had gone through it in the last sixty seconds.

He pushed through into the stairwell beyond.

\*\*\*

The stairwell connected to the hospital's external loading dock on the ground floor. He looked down through the gap between the stair flights and saw the ground floor landing lay empty; the door to the loading dock swung closed. 

He went down.

The loading dock was a covered concrete apron at the rear of the building, accessible from Commercial Road via a service lane. Two delivery vehicles parked against the far wall, their drivers absent. A hospital orderly sat on a loading bay step with a phone in his hand, not looking up. The service lane ran twenty meters to the street, and at the end of it, turning left onto Commercial Road, was the woman in the white coat.

She had removed the coat. She carried it folded over her left arm — the tablet gone, the ID badge gone, the white coat now a piece of clothing rather than a credential. The transformation had taken less than sixty seconds in the stairwell. The replacement had known the exit route before it entered the building.

Kain moved through the loading dock and into the service lane.

Commercial Road at nine-thirty on a Tuesday morning carried the specific density of a major arterial in a medical precinct — delivery vehicles, taxis, the slow accumulation of traffic around a hospital that drew its own sustained current of human movement. The footpath held the particular mix of people that gathered around large medical facilities: staff on breaks, visitors arriving and departing, the occasional patient moving with the careful deliberateness of someone recently discharged.

The woman was forty meters ahead, moving south at a pace that had changed. The correct, unhurried tempo of the corridor was gone. She moved now with the specific efficiency of someone executing a planned withdrawal — not running, not drawing attention, but covering ground at the maximum rate that did not constitute flight.

He followed.

The gap closed to thirty meters. Twenty-five. The footpath narrowed at a construction hoarding that extended from a building site across half the pedestrian space, compressing foot traffic into a single-file channel between the hoarding and the road. She entered the channel. He was ten seconds behind her.

The Bleed returned in the channel.

This time it was the laughing woman, and this time she was not behind his eyes. She occupied the space two meters ahead of him in the channel, her presence directional, located, as physically specific as the hoarding to his left and the road to his right. She laughed for four seconds — high, uncontrolled, the laugh of someone who had found something genuinely funny in a situation that contained nothing funny — and the sound had a position in space, a source point, as though she stood in the channel and was laughing at something she could see that he could not.

He stopped moving. The person behind him walked into his back and said something he did not process.

The plywood was cheap, slightly damp from the morning's rain, the grain of the wood raised under his fingertips where the moisture had lifted it. He pressed his left hand flat against it and held the texture and did not move until the laughing woman stopped.

Four seconds.

He pushed through the remaining length of the channel and emerged onto the open footpath beyond, scanning south, north, the road, the opposite footpath.

Nothing.

She had used the Bleed. Not deliberately — she could not have known about the Bleed, could not have predicted the episode. But the timing had been exact, and the result was the same regardless of intent: fifteen seconds of combined operational blackout had given a person moving at her pace enough distance to disappear into the crowd.

He stood on the footpath in the rain and ran the inventory.

He had confirmed the replacement was operational. He had confirmed it used Winters's credentials and had a network contact inside the hospital — \*M. Reyes\*, a name written in a practiced hand on a blank intake form. He had confirmed it knew its exit routes and had a planned withdrawal protocol. He had confirmed it moved like someone reading a space rather than inhabiting it, and he had confirmed that the tablet orientation was a behavioral tell that would appear again.

He had not seen its face clearly. Twenty meters in the service corridor, her back to him for most of it. The face-to-face exchange had lasted three seconds at a distance of four meters, and his attention had been on the voice, the cadence, the assessment running behind her eyes. He had the bone structure. He had the coloring. He did not have enough.

He pulled out his phone and dialed Emily.

She picked up on the first ring.

"I lost it on Commercial Road," he said. "South of the loading dock. Planned withdrawal — coat off, credentials gone, exit route pre-mapped. It knew the building before it entered."

"How long did you have eyes on it?"

"Six minutes. Maybe seven."

"Did you get a visual?"

"Partial. Not enough." He started walking north, back toward the hospital. "The intake form in room two-fourteen has a name. M. Reyes. Run it against the QSA civilian contact registry and the Alfred patient database."

"That'll take twenty minutes."

"I'll be inside." He paused. "The ten-thirty data review session is still on the board. The replacement booked it. It may come back to complete it."

Silence on Emily's end. Not the silence of processing — the silence of someone deciding whether to say the thing they were about to say. "You think it'll return to a location it just fled."

"I think it has a network contact named Reyes and an unfinished data review session and a replacement protocol that requires both of those things to be completed before it moves to the next phase. Abandoning the session creates a gap in Winters's record that flags the breach. It won't want that gap."

"You're betting on its operational discipline."

"I'm betting on its instructions. There's a difference."

He ended the call and pushed back through the loading dock door.

\*\*\*

The atrium at ten-fifteen held a different population than the one at nine o'clock. The morning's appointment rush had passed through and dispersed into the building's upper floors, leaving behind the slower, more settled density of people who were waiting rather than arriving. The family near the information desk had rearranged themselves — the sleeping child now awake and eating something from a paper bag, the adults in the same configuration but with the particular quality of people who had stopped pretending the wait had a predictable end.

Kain took a different chair this time — one with a sightline to both the elevator bank and the main entrance, positioned behind a structural column that broke his silhouette without obstructing his view. He had twelve minutes before the ten-thirty session. He used four of them to review the photograph of the intake form on his phone, studying the handwriting in the name field. The letters were formed with the careful deliberateness of someone who had practiced the motion rather than developed it — the \*R\* in Reyes slightly too upright, the \*y\* descending at an angle that was geometrically correct but not natural.

The replacement had written the name. Not Winters. The replacement had created a patient record for its own network contact using a name it had been given rather than one it had generated, and it had written that name in a hand that was not Winters's hand because it had not yet had enough time in the building to absorb the fine motor residue of Winters's writing.

His phone buzzed. Emily.

"M. Reyes doesn't appear in the QSA civilian contact registry," she said. "The Alfred patient database has a Marcus Reyes, sixty-three, cardiac patient, last appointment fourteen months ago. No connection to the neurology research clinic."

"It's a cover name. The contact came in as a patient, used the intake form to pass something to the replacement, and left before the appointment officially ended."

"What did they pass?"

"I don't know yet." He kept his eyes on the elevator bank. "The data review session at ten-thirty. Is it still showing in the system?"

The pause lasted three seconds. "Yes. It's still active."

"Then the replacement is coming back."

At ten twenty-two, the elevator doors opened.

The woman who stepped out wore different clothing — dark trousers, a grey jacket, no white coat, no ID badge. Her hair was still pulled back. She carried nothing in either hand. She moved through the atrium toward the corridor that led to the research wing with the same correct, unhurried tempo she had used in the staff corridor, and her left hand swung at her side with the natural looseness of someone who was not carrying a tablet and had not yet remembered to adjust the behavioral pattern that the tablet had been anchoring.

Her left hand was still wrong. The fingers held a slight curl, the thumb angled outward — the residual posture of a hand that had been gripping a tablet for the last ninety minutes and had not yet released the muscle memory of it.

Kain stood up from the chair.

He followed her into the corridor at a distance of twenty meters, and the Bleed did not return, and his depth perception was off by an estimated eight percent, and the fluorescent light arrived at his eyes a fraction of a second after it should have, and none of that mattered because the replacement was twelve meters ahead of him and moving toward a room it had booked under a dead woman's credentials, and he had eleven minutes before the session was scheduled to begin, and he had not seen its face clearly, and he was not going to let that happen again.

He closed the distance to ten meters.

Eight.

The replacement reached the door to the research wing and badged through with Winters's keycard. The door swung closed behind it. He reached it four seconds later and used his Level 4 clearance to follow.

The corridor beyond was empty except for the replacement, now six meters ahead, moving toward room two-fourteen with the same correct tempo. He was close enough to hear her footsteps on the linoleum — a sound that was almost right, almost the sound of someone who had walked this corridor a thousand times, but not quite, the weight distribution fractionally off, the heel strike a degree too deliberate.

She reached room two-fourteen and stopped with her hand on the door.

And then she turned around.

She turned immediately and completely — not the cautious pivot of someone registering a noise, but the turn of someone who had known he was there for thirty seconds and had decided this was the moment to stop pretending otherwise.

He was five meters away. The corridor lighting was the same cold fluorescent that rendered every face the color of old bone. Her face was Winters's face — the bone structure, the coloring, the arrangement of features. But the expression was not Winters's expression, and it was not the rapid-assessment expression he had seen in the service corridor. It was something else. Something that had decided the withdrawal protocol was no longer the operative instruction.

She opened her mouth to speak.

The Bleed took him.

Not the baseline room this time. Not the laughing woman. Something new — a complete sensory replacement, the corridor gone, the fluorescent light gone, the replacement's face gone, replaced by a darkness that was not the darkness of a closed room but the darkness of the Sphere's interior, the matte black surface two point eight meters in every direction, the subsonic hum of eleven megawatts moving through the platform below his feet, the ozone smell so concentrated it registered as a taste at the back of his throat.

He was inside the Sphere.

He was in the corridor.

Both were equally true and neither was navigable.

He pressed both hands against the wall to his right — the linoleum surface, the slight give of the plasterboard beneath it, the cold of the building's air conditioning in the material — and held the texture and counted his pulse and did not move.

When the Sphere's interior receded, he was on one knee in the corridor with no memory of going down.

The door to room two-fourteen was closed.

The corridor was empty.

He stayed on one knee for four seconds, running the inventory. Vision: degraded, peripheral field showing residual darkness from the Sphere's interior. Auditory: clear. Cognition: sequential reasoning present but slow, working memory showing significant gaps. Motor: gross motor compromised, fine motor unknown.

He stood up.

The door to room two-fourteen was locked. The keycard reader showed red. He used his Level 4 clearance and the reader cycled to green and he pushed through.

The room was empty. The desk surface was clear. The intake form with M. Reyes's name was gone.

He pulled out his phone and dialed Emily.

She picked up on the first ring.

"It's gone," he said. "And I still don't have the face."

The pause on Emily's end lasted two seconds. "Come back to the office."

"Not yet." He looked at the empty desk. "The intake form is gone. It took it. Which means whatever Reyes passed to it was on that form, and it didn't want to leave the paper trail."

"Kain." Her voice carried the specific quality of someone who had been deciding whether to say something for the last twenty minutes and had arrived at a decision. "Your last three transmissions have had a combined delay of eleven seconds. That's not a network issue."

He said nothing.

"Come back to the office," she said again. "Now."

He ended the call and stood in the empty room for a moment longer, and the Sphere's darkness did not return, and the laughing woman did not return, and the corridor outside was empty and fluorescent and exactly as long as it had always been, and he walked back through it toward the elevator with both hands at his sides and his depth perception eight percent off and the specific knowledge that the replacement had seen his face clearly, at a distance of five meters, in good light, for at least three seconds before the Bleed had taken him.

Which meant the network now knew he was here.  
Which meant the geometry of everything had changed.
