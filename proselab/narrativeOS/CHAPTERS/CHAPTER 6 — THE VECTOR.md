# **CHAPTER 6 — THE VECTOR**

The QSA field office occupied the third floor of a building designed to look like it served no particular purpose. The exterior was glass and brushed aluminium, indistinguishable from the financial services firms on either side of it, and the lobby contained a reception desk staffed by a woman who directed visitors to floors one and two with the practiced efficiency of someone who had never been asked about floor three. The elevator required a keycard not issued to visitors. The stairwell door on the third floor required a second keycard not issued to anyone below a Level 4 clearance.

Kain had a Level 4 clearance. He had not slept in thirty-one hours.

The ninety minutes in the car outside the Solis building did not constitute sleep in any clinical sense. His nervous system had entered a shallow holding pattern — not rest, not recovery, but a temporary reduction in the rate at which it was consuming itself. The Bleed's 1.4x multiplier existed as a physical reality that manifested in the specific way the fluorescent lighting in the stairwell arrived at his eyes a fraction of a second after it should have, as though the signal traveled through a medium slightly denser than air. His depth perception was off by a margin too small to measure and too large to ignore. The third step from the top of the stairwell landing looked closer than it was. He adjusted his stride without breaking it.

He pushed through the stairwell door and walked toward the briefing room at the end of the corridor.

Emily was already there.

She sat at the table with a coffee she had not touched, her hands wrapped around the cup in the way people hold things when they need something to do with their hands. The original expression that crossed her face when he entered — relief, followed immediately by the suppression of relief — lasted approximately half a second before she replaced it with something more neutral. The suppression was its own kind of information.

The briefing room hummed. The server rack in the adjacent equipment bay pushed a low-frequency vibration through the shared wall, a sound below the threshold of conscious hearing that Kain's body registered as a faint pressure behind the sternum. The room was cold — the kind of cold that came from air conditioning calibrated for equipment rather than people, the kind that made the coffee in Emily's cup go from hot to lukewarm in under four minutes. He had been in this room enough times to know the exact rate of that cooling. The cup in her hands had been sitting there for at least twelve minutes before he arrived.

She had been waiting.

"You look terrible," she said.

"I found something in B-734." He sat down across from her and placed the data chip on the table between them. "Chen's terminal had a secondary access log. Someone ran a query through the hospital's civilian research database three days before Chen was replaced."

Emily's eyes moved to the chip, then back to him. "What kind of query?"

"PQ Genotype research cohort. Specifically, the anonymized patient files connected to a neurologist named Sarah Winters. The query pulled her entire research dataset — methodology, patient outcomes, unpublished findings." He paused. "The search parameters were precise. Not exploratory."

"That's not a casual breach," she said.

"No."

She picked up her coffee, held it, set it back down without drinking. The cup left a ring on the brushed steel table. She did not wipe it. "Winters is QSA-adjacent. Her research is civilian, but the cohort data is flagged under the medical data-sharing protocol. Accessing it requires a Level 3 clearance minimum."

"Or a cloned keycard and six minutes with the central server."

The silence that followed contained more than one conversation. Emily's eyes moved to the chip, then to a point slightly to his left that was not the wall but was not him either. The server rack hummed through the wall. The fluorescent light above the table had a barely perceptible flicker — a cycle of approximately four seconds — that his sleep-deprived visual cortex had locked onto and could not release.

"The network is interested in Winters's research," she said.

"The network is interested in what Winters's research proves." He kept his voice level. "There's a difference."

"Which is?"

"Winters has been documenting PQ Genotype expression in civilian carriers for eleven years. Unpublished. The QSA knows the research exists but hasn't moved to classify it because classifying it would require acknowledging that the civilian carrier population is larger than the official figures suggest." He let that land. "Someone outside the QSA just pulled that data. Someone who already knew it existed and knew where to find it."

Emily's hands moved on the table. The same small adjustment — fingers spreading, then returning. "That's a significant operational capability."

"Yes."

"It suggests resources. Infrastructure. A network that's been building for longer than the current anomaly sequence."

"Yes."

She looked at him directly. "What else did you find?"

The question was precise. Not *did you find anything else* — which would have been casual, exploratory — but *what else*, which assumed the existence of additional information and asked him to produce it. He had been waiting for that question since he walked through the door. He had been deciding, since before he walked through the door, exactly how to answer it.

"The replacement's behavioral pattern in B-734 is consistent with the other three anomalies," he said. "Same methodology. Same error signature. Same window of operational deployment before the errors compound." He paused. "Four anomalies with identical methodology suggests a single architect. Someone who built the replacement protocol and is running it across multiple realities simultaneously."

"That's what the evidence supports," Emily said.

"That's what the evidence supports," he agreed.

He did not tell her about the journals. He did not tell her that he knew she had taken them from Solis's apartment before the sequestration, that he had found the gap in the evidence chain where the journals should have been and traced the gap back to her. He did not tell her that the journals were the piece of the puzzle that connected the replacement methodology to its origin point, and that whoever held the journals held the thread that unraveled the entire network.

He did not tell her because she already knew all of it, and telling her would tell her that he knew, and knowing that he knew would change the geometry of everything that followed.

Instead, he said, "I need to get to Winters before the network does."

"She'll be at Alfred on Tuesday morning." Emily said it the way people say things they have already decided to say — without the micro-pause that preceded genuine recall. "Research clinic."

"Today is Tuesday."

"Yes."

He stood up from the table. The movement cost him more than it should have — a half-second lag between the decision to stand and the execution of it, his body running slightly behind his intentions in the way that happened when the Bleed was active and the neural pathways operated at reduced efficiency. He covered it by reaching for the data chip, a purposeful movement that explained the pause without drawing attention to it.

Emily's attention did not move from his face. She had the particular quality of focus that came from knowing someone well enough to read the gaps in their performance — the places where the presentation and the reality diverged. He had seen her apply that attention to other people. He had not, until recently, understood that she had been applying it to him.

"When did you last sleep?" she asked.

"I'm fine."

"That's not what I asked."

"It's the answer I have." He pocketed the chip. "I'll contact you from Alfred."

"Kain."

He stopped at the door.

"The network moved on Winters's data three days ago," she said. "If they're following the same pattern as the other anomalies, they've already made their next move. You won't be tracking them. You'll be following them."

"I know."

"Then you know that going to Alfred without knowing what you're walking into is—"

"Necessary," he said. "It's necessary."

He left before she could find a word for what it actually was.

\*\*\*

The Bleed hit him in the stairwell.

He was on the landing between the second and third floors when it arrived — not as a gradual dimming but as a hard interruption, a cut in the continuity of sensory experience so complete that the four steps preceding it simply ceased to exist in his memory. One moment he was pushing through the stairwell door at the top. The next moment he was standing on the landing with his right hand on the railing and no account of how it had gotten there.

The sound came before anything else.

It originated behind his eyes rather than in his ears — not a directional sound, not something that arrived from a specific point in space, but a sound that existed inside the architecture of his skull, pressed against the back of his orbital bones from the inside. A woman laughing. High, uncontrolled, the laugh of someone who had found something genuinely funny in a situation that contained nothing funny. It lasted three seconds and then it was gone, but gone was the wrong word for what happened to it — it did not stop, it receded, pulling back through the neural tissue the way a wave pulls back through sand, leaving the texture of itself behind.

He stood on the landing and did not move.

The stairwell existed around him in two registers simultaneously. The first was the physical reality of the building: the hum of the ventilation system, the distant sound of Commercial Road traffic filtering through the concrete, the fluorescent tube above the landing that buzzed at a frequency slightly higher than the one on the third floor. The second was something else — a pressure at the periphery of his visual field, a sense of additional space that the stairwell did not contain, the ghost of a room that was not this room pressing itself against the edges of his perception.

B-734.

The dormant PQ architecture in his neural tissue had no mechanism for sorting the sensory data it absorbed during a transition. An active carrier — someone whose architecture had been conditioned through the QSA's protocols — maintained a functional boundary between baseline reality and the residual imprints of the realities they passed through. His architecture was dormant. The boundary it maintained was the boundary of a structure that had never been designed for load-bearing. The Sphere's entanglement field had forced it into service anyway, and the microscopic scarring in the microtubule structures that resulted from that forced activation did not heal between exposures. It accumulated. Each episode of scarring widened the gaps in the boundary. Each gap allowed more residual data through.

The laughing woman was not a hallucination. She was a memory that did not belong to him — a fragment of B-734's sensory record that had adhered to his neural tissue during the transition and was now surfacing through the damaged pathways at intervals he could not predict and could not control. The 1.4x multiplier from sleep deprivation did not create the damage. It accelerated the rate at which the existing damage expressed itself. It thinned the boundary further, made the gaps wider, reduced the interval between episodes.

He counted his pulse against the railing. Eighty-nine beats per minute. Elevated, but not critically. His vision had stabilized — the ghost of the additional room had receded to a faint pressure at the far edge of his peripheral field, present but not dominant. The laughing woman had not returned.

He descended the remaining stairs and pushed through the lobby door into the morning light.

\*\*\*

Alfred Hospital was twenty minutes south in ordinary traffic. The traffic on St Kilda Road at eight-fifteen on a Tuesday morning was not ordinary. It was the specific density of a city that had decided, collectively, to be somewhere else at the same time, and the result was a compression of vehicles that moved in increments of three to five meters before stopping again, each stop accompanied by the particular sound of engines idling at the edge of their patience.

The rain had come in off the bay sometime in the last hour. Not heavy — a fine, persistent mist that the windscreen wipers handled at their lowest setting, a slow sweep every four seconds that cleared the glass and then watched it fog again. The city outside the windows was the grey of wet concrete and brake lights, the kind of grey that flattened depth and made distances unreliable.

Kain drove with both hands on the wheel and his attention divided in a way that was not safe and could not be corrected. The primary division was between the road and the data chip in his jacket pocket — not the chip itself, but what it represented, the thread it pulled on, the specific shape of the network's interest in Winters's research. The secondary division was between the road and the stairwell landing, the laughing woman, the ghost of the additional room pressing against the edges of his visual field.

The traffic light at the intersection of St Kilda Road and Domain Road cycled through green while he was still four cars back. He had seen it cycle. He had registered the green. The four seconds between the green and his foot moving from the brake to the accelerator were four seconds he could not fully account for — not a gap, not a blackout, but a thinning of attention so complete that the interval had passed without leaving a clear record.

The car behind him sounded its horn.

He moved through the intersection.

The 1.4x multiplier was not a number. It was the specific experience of operating a vehicle through Melbourne rain while a portion of his cognitive architecture was occupied with sensory data from a reality he had left eight hours ago. It was the experience of his depth perception arriving at conclusions slightly after the situation that had prompted them. It was the experience of knowing, with clinical precision, exactly what was happening to him and being unable to stop it, because stopping it required sleep and sleep required time and time was the one resource the network was not going to give him.

He parked on a side street two blocks from the hospital and sat in the car for ninety seconds with the engine off, running a manual inventory of his current functional state. Vision: stable, depth perception degraded by an estimated eight percent. Auditory: clear, no residual intrusions in the last eleven minutes. Cognition: sequential reasoning intact, working memory showing gaps at the margins. Motor: fine motor control within acceptable parameters, gross motor slightly delayed.

Acceptable, if not good.

He got out of the car and walked toward the hospital in the rain.

The street folded. Not metaphorically. The wet bitumen of Flinders Lane simply ceased to be the surface under his feet and became the linoleum of the Sydney corridor, the overhead fluorescents replacing the sodium streetlights in a single frame-cut that his visual cortex registered as a physical blow behind the left eye.

He stopped walking.

His right hand went to his jacket pocket. Not a decision — a reflex, the same reflex that had sent his hand to the same pocket every day for nine years without producing anything useful. The lighter was there. It was always there. His fingers closed around it: the rectangular weight of it, the worn brass casing, the engraving on the face worn smooth enough that he had to press his thumbnail into the letters to read them by touch. *N.K. — Solis — Case 7\.* The flint wheel sat under his thumb, ridged and familiar.

He rolled it.

Nothing. The dry rasp of metal on spent flint, the faint chemical ghost of fuel that had evaporated sometime in the previous decade, and then silence. No spark. No catch. The same result it had produced every time he'd tested it in nine years, which was not often, because he did not smoke, had not smoked since the week after Solis handed it to him across the table at the Punt Road debrief and said *keep it, you earned it, and you should quit anyway.* 

He had quit. He had kept the lighter. He had never replaced the flint.

The Melbourne street reasserted itself. The fluorescents dissolved back into sodium. The linoleum became bitumen.

He stood in the rain with the dead lighter in his fist and waited for his visual field to stabilise.

Forty-three seconds. Then the overlay was gone.

He put the lighter back in his pocket and kept walking.

The main atrium of Alfred Hospital operated at a frequency that had nothing to do with sound. It was a frequency of human density — the specific compression of bodies moving through a space that had been designed for throughput rather than comfort, each body carrying its own particular weight of urgency or dread or the particular exhaustion of people who had been here before and knew what waiting in this building meant.

The smell arrived before the visual data resolved. Antiseptic over something organic that the antiseptic had not fully covered, and beneath both of those, the particular staleness of air that had been breathed by too many people in too short a time. His olfactory system processed it and cross-referenced it against the residual sensory data from B-734, where the hospital had smelled almost identical — the same antiseptic, the same organic undertone, the same staleness — but with a fractional difference in the chemical composition of the cleaning products that his nervous system had catalogued during the transition and had not yet released.

The atrium was large enough to contain its own weather system. The ceiling rose to four stories, glass and steel, the morning's grey light pressing through it in a diffuse wash that eliminated shadows and made everyone in the space look slightly unwell. A coffee kiosk near the main entrance generated a queue of eight people. A family of four occupied a row of chairs near the information desk, the youngest child asleep across two seats, the adults arranged in the specific geometry of people who had been sitting in the same configuration for long enough that the configuration had become load-bearing.

Kain moved through the atrium toward the elevator bank and did not stop moving.

The sensory density of the space pressed against the thinned boundary in his neural architecture with a persistence that required active management. Every sound — the PA system announcing a name, the wheels of a trolley on polished linoleum, the specific acoustic signature of a space where sound bounced off hard surfaces and arrived from multiple directions simultaneously — carried the potential to trigger a cross-reference with B-734's residual data. He managed it the way he managed everything: by keeping his attention narrow, his focus specific, his cognitive resources allocated to the immediate task rather than the peripheral noise.

The elevator arrived. He stepped in. The doors closed.

In the silence of the elevator, the laughing woman returned for exactly two seconds.

He pressed the button for the second floor and did not move until the doors opened.

\*\*\*

The research clinic occupied a wing on the hospital's second floor, accessible through a corridor that branched off the main atrium and was marked with signage deliberately understated — a small placard, white text on grey, that read NEUROLOGICAL RESEARCH — LEVEL 2 in a font size suggesting the clinic had no interest in visitors who had not already been told where to find it.

The corridor outside the clinic was empty at eight forty-seven in the morning. Three doors on the left side, each one closed. A nurses' station at the far end, currently unmanned. A window at the corridor's midpoint that looked out over the hospital's internal courtyard — a square of grey concrete with a single tree that had been planted with optimism and had responded with the kind of survival indistinguishable from resignation.

He stopped at the nurses' station and looked at the appointment board.

Winters's name appeared twice. The first entry was at nine o'clock — a patient appointment, the name redacted under the privacy protocol. The second entry was at ten-thirty, marked not as a patient appointment but as a data review session. The notation beside it read: \*W. methodology cross-ref — see attached protocol.\*

He looked at the notation for a long time.

The replacement's behavioral pattern across all four anomalies included a specific error: the tendency to maintain the replaced individual's external commitments while subtly altering the internal methodology. The meetings stayed on the calendar. The signatures stayed consistent. But the underlying logic of the decisions shifted — small deviations that accumulated over days until the pattern became visible to someone who knew what to look for.

\*W. methodology cross-ref\* was not language Winters would use to describe her own work. It was the language of someone describing Winters's work from the outside — someone who had read the research dataset pulled from Chen's terminal and was now attempting to replicate the methodology without fully understanding it.

The replacement had already been here.

He turned away from the appointment board and pulled out his phone. He needed to know when the data breach at Chen's terminal had occurred relative to Winters's last confirmed appearance at the clinic. He needed to know whether the person who had attended the nine o'clock appointment this morning was Sarah Winters or someone who had spent three days learning to be her.

He dialed Emily.

She picked up on the second ring.

"The replacement is already operational," he said. "I need Winters's biometric entry log for the last four days. Every time she badged into this building."

A pause. Not the pause of someone who needed time to process the request, but the pause of someone deciding how much of their own information to reveal in response to his. "I can get that."

"How long?"

"Twenty minutes."

"I'll be in the atrium."

He ended the call and stood in the corridor for a moment, looking at the appointment board, at the notation that was not Winters's language, at the evidence of a replacement that had been operational for long enough to schedule its own data review sessions.

The laughing woman did not return.

He descended to the atrium and found a chair with a clear sightline to the elevator bank and the main entrance and sat down to wait, and the rain continued outside the glass ceiling, and the family near the information desk maintained their load-bearing geometry, and his nervous system continued its slow, measurable work of consuming itself.

Twenty minutes. He had twenty minutes.

He used twelve of them before the next gap opened.
