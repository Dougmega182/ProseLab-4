# **CHAPTER 1 — BLACK PEARL BAR**

Kain killed Betty's engine and sat in the dark.  
The car park behind the Black Pearl. Three sodium lights. One dead for six weeks, one cycling on a four-second burn, one steady above the rear door. He'd chosen this bar for the same reason Emily had — it ran off the QSA grid, its mesh node buried under the foundation slab and shielded well enough that nothing transmitted in or out without the owner's say-so. He'd been coming here for four years. She had known that when she sent the file.

The rear door remained motionless for ninety seconds.

He got out.

\*\*\* 

The fragmentation started on the walk in.  
Not severe. A half-second stutter in his left peripheral field — the neon above the depot next door breaking into its component frequencies before snapping back to red. He stopped walking and pressed two fingers against the inside of his left wrist, counting his pulse the way the QSA's neurologist had taught him when the Bleed first presented. Sixty-two. Steady. The stutter passed.

Dormant PQ carriers didn't fragment. That was the clinical position, and it was wrong in the specific, careful way that clinical positions are wrong when the data set is too small and the agency funding the research has reasons to prefer a particular outcome. Kain had been fragmenting for four years. He hadn't told anyone.

He went inside.  
\*\*\* 

The Black Pearl began as a shipping office, became a warehouse, spent six months as a gallery, and gave up. The current owners kept the exposed brick and the loading-bay ceiling and put a bar along the eastern wall that ran the full fourteen metres of the room. The effect was accumulated rather than designed. It looked like what it was.  
A Gen-4 autom-unit worked the bar. The joint articulation at the wrist marked it as a Gen-4 — that slight over-precision, the absence of the micro-hesitation that plagued the Gen-3s when orders stacked. It registered his entry through the passive array in the bar's surface and began pouring before he reached the stool.

"Preference profile on record," it said. "Kain, N. Last visit: forty-one days ago. Karuizawa, neat."

"Pour it."

The bar ran off the grid. That was the only reason Emily had chosen it. The mesh node didn't transmit. It remembered his drink and kept the memory to itself, which was the point of the Pearl and the point of the meeting both.

He drank. The scotch was correct.

\*\*\* 

His second was half-gone when she cleared the door.

Eleven years. The shape of her movement  — the ambient social awareness of someone who had grown up in cities, who read exits and emotional temperature as ordinary habit — cut through the room. He had watched her work a conference room in 2146, a dinner table in 2149, a deposition in 2152\.  
The woman crossing the Pearl's floor moved differently.

She wore civilian clothes — dark trousers, a grey jacket too well‑cut to be off the rack — and carried nothing. No bag. No device. Her hands were empty with the deliberate quality of someone who had decided emptiness was a message. She crossed the room without registering a single patron, the room already assessed before she’d come through the door, the target fixed. She was moving, simply, through the space between herself and him.

She stopped beside his stool. Looked at the bar.

A familiar tension gripped the left side of her jaw — the way her jaw clenched when she was managing information rather than sharing it. He had seen it twice before. Once in 2149, when she told him the Reyes case was being closed over his objection. Once, four years ago, when she told him his clearance had been revoked.

He picked up his glass. His grip on it was deliberate — thumb and two fingers, the ring and little finger curled under. It kept the tremor in his right hand below the threshold of visibility. The fragmentation in the car park had left a residue. It always did.

The Gen-4 appeared at Emily's position. She ordered water without looking at it.  
Neither of them spoke.

He let the silence run. She had prepared for this conversation, and she was deciding which angle of approach to deploy. The silence was information.

"You didn't open the file," she said.

"No."

"Why not?"

"Because *you* sent it." He set the glass down. "And you don't send files to this address unless you need something off the record. I wanted to hear it from you directly."

She was quiet for a moment. The silence shifted — the prepared version abandoned, something more careful taking its place.

"How long has it been?" he asked. 

"Long enough that the answer doesn't change what I'm about to ask you."

"Try me."

"Kain—"  
"You're in a bar that runs off the QSA grid," he said. "You came in without a device. You're wearing a jacket you bought somewhere that doesn't log purchases. You've been running protocol since before you walked through that door." He turned the glass a quarter rotation. "Eleven years, Emily. I know what your protocol looks like. I've never seen you run it on me."

The tension in her jaw held. She looked at the bar.

"There's a man named Marcus Chen," she said. "Senior Policy Architect, Civilian Integration Directorate. Level 6 clearance. He died four days ago in his apartment. Cardiac arrest, according to the record."

"According to the record."

"He was fifty-one. Ninety-third percentile biometric profile. No cardiac history." She paused. "The record is wrong."

He said nothing. Let her build it.

"Chen's apartment geometry is monitored by passive QSA surveillance. A baseline layout. Six weeks ago, the physical routine broke." She delivered it without inflection, the way she delivered things she knew sounded impossible and had decided to deliver anyway. "Chen took a seven-minute shower every morning for three years. On Tuesday, he took fourteen minutes."  
"Water pressure?"

"Normal." She turned to him. "Forty minutes later, he moved a book on his living room shelf. The Maltese Falcon. He reversed the spine so it faced inward."

"People move books."

"His father was a rare book dealer. He was raised with strict archival habits. He hadn't reversed a spine in his life."

"You're tracking a Level 6 Architect's shower routine and home geometry."

"We track everyone's home geometry," she said. "Our instruments flagged it as lifestyle variation. Within normal parameters."

"Because they're calibrated for coherence signatures and biometric deviation." He turned the glass again. "Not for the sudden absence of a psychological habit."

"Yes."

"Negative space."

"Yes."  
The room moved around them. The QI maintenance crew in the corner booth had dropped their voices. The woman with the paperback had stopped turning pages. The Gen-4 worked the bar with its fluid, optimized economy, indifferent to the conversation happening three metres away.

A tremor vibrated in Kain's right hand. He flattened it against the bar.

"How many?" he asked.

"Chen is the fourth confirmed case in fourteen months. Three probable. Possibly more — we don't know what we're looking for, which means we don't know what we've missed." She held his eyes. "Whoever is running this understands quantum insertion at a level that shouldn't exist outside the QSA. They're replacing operatives in positions of institutional influence. And the replacements don't know they are replacements."

"And the QSA can't investigate internally."

"If this becomes an official operation, it becomes a political event. The Accords are the only thing preventing every nation with a quantum program and a grievance from going dark. If it becomes known that we've been compromised at this level—" She stopped. "The containment standards that have held for sixty years don't hold without the Accords."

"The directive," he said.

"CDIR-CONV-2618-03. Article 7 emergency provisions. Non-QSA civilian contractors, off-book operations, institutional liability threshold." She didn't look away. "Operational records erased on mission completion. Including the contractor's involvement." 

"Meaning if this goes wrong."

"Neither of us existed. Yes."

He picked up his glass. The tremor in his right hand had settled to a vibration to faint to move glass's surface. He drank.

"You chose this bar," he said, "because the mesh node doesn't transmit. This conversation isn't logged anywhere. Which means the directive is already active. You didn't come here to ask me. You came here to brief me."

The tension shifted from her jaw. Not released — redirected. She had expected him to get there. She had been waiting to see how long it took.  
"I came here," she said carefully, "because you're the only person I know who can read the negative space in an empty apartment. Our instruments can't do it. Our analysts can't do it. You did it four years ago with Elias Varn, and you did it because you think in a way that our training actively selects against." She paused. "I need that. The case needs that."

"The Varn case," he said.

"Yes."

It always came back to Varn. Everything in his professional life traced back to Varn — not because Varn was the source of anything, but because Varn was the point at which the gradient changed and everything that followed ran downhill.

"We'll talk about Varn," he said. "But not here."

She nodded once. The prepared version of the conversation had been abandoned twenty minutes ago. What remained was something older and more complicated — the quiet, practiced negotiation of two people who knew exactly where the other one’s leverage was.

His peripheral field held. No stutter. The Bleed was quiet for now, which meant nothing about what it would do in an hour, or tomorrow, or in whatever QSA facility she was about to take him to. He hadn't told her about the fragmentation. He wasn't going to.

"When do we start?" he asked.

"Now," she said. "We start now."
