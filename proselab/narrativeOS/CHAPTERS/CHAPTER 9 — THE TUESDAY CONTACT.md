# **CHAPTER 9 — THE TUESDAY CONTACT**

The laptop ran hot on the cheap aluminum table.

It was 4:30 AM in the Fitzroy North temporary office, a room rented with cash and configured with exactly two pieces of QSA-adjacent technology: the router, which was routed through three jurisdictions Kain had prepaid using untraceable crypto, and the laptop, which ran a Level 4 field credential. The rest of the equipment was civilian-grade—a thermal printer, a second monitor, and the two coffee mugs that held the last of the instant caffeine he had found in the cupboard. He had a two-hour window before the local council’s automated sweep detected the router’s operational signature and flagged the IP block for a manual review. Two hours to complete the third step of the cross-reference.

The M. Reyes alias had provided the necessary first step. Reyes, M. — cardiac patient, sixty-three, last appointment fourteen months ago. The name was a cover, but covers required construction, and construction required source material. Kain was looking for the seam where the cover story connected to the QSA’s institutional architecture.

He ran the query on a non-QSA terminal, routing through a secure proxy server he had maintained since the Varn extraction, using his Level 4 credential to pull the QSA civilian contact registry. The search parameters were narrow: M. Reyes, all operational liaisons between the QSA’s Civilian Interface Division (CID) and the Alfred Hospital system over the last five years.

The first pass yielded nothing. Reyes was a civilian. His cardiac file was clean. The link to the neurology research clinic, which the replacement had used to pass the note, was non-existent. The connection was buried deeper than the name.

Kain reset the query. He ran the second step: all CID operational files between the dates of January 2153 and June 2154—the fourteen-month window preceding Chen’s replacement—cross-referenced against the Alfred Hospital’s staff movement logs. He was looking for a pattern of anomaly: a QSA operative who had been near the hospital at the wrong time, or whose documented activity failed to match the clinical reality.

The query returned 38,000 entries. Too large. He needed a pin.

He took the folded paper from his jacket pocket—the one the target had left on the tram. He unfolded it now. It was blank. The target had left him nothing but the name, the warning, and the confidence that he would do the rest.

Kain crumpled the paper and threw it into the wastebasket. The confidence was the message.

He returned to the terminal and reframed the problem using the residue theory. The network was efficient. They didn't invent components; they repurposed them. If the Reyes alias was a functional cover, it had been drawn from a QSA asset who was already connected to the M. Reyes cardiac file.

He ran a new search: Alfred Hospital Staff, all departments, cross-referenced against Civilian Interface Division active and suspended personnel, all common surnames.

The query narrowed the list to 412 names. Still too large.

He needed a logical gap. He returned to the cardiac file. Marcus Reyes, age 63\. Two appointments over five years. Annual check-ups. The file was dull, boring, and medically inert, which was the point. The only deviation was a single insurance claim for a missing personal item filed in May 2154, eight months before Chen’s replacement. A watch. Claim denied.

Kain had read 38,000 files in the last two hours. The watch was the only thing that had been lost.

He cross-referenced the denial of the watch claim with the QSA’s internal financial management system. QSA operatives were prohibited from claiming personal losses sustained during operational duties through civilian insurance. The operative’s personal loss was absorbed by the QSA’s Operational Reserve Fund. The denial of the claim was the administrative process working correctly.

But Marcus Reyes was not an operative. He was a civilian cardiac patient.

The only reason a civilian’s claim would be denied under QSA protocols was if the item was lost by a QSA operative who had been using the civilian’s credentials.

He opened the Operational Reserve Fund ledger. The claim was listed: Loss of Personal Timepiece \- M. Reyes \- Operational Denial \- Ref: OPS-CONV-784-C.

The reference number was the pin.

He ran the final query: OPS-CONV-784-C – Full Operational History.  
The result populated the screen with a single, clear file:

* Designation: Operative Delta-6 (Voss, E. \- Civilian Interface Division)  
* Operational Context: Acquisition and sequestration of compromised field asset (Solis, M. – Threshold Program Director)  
* Asset Used: Marcus Reyes (Civilian Identity, Cardiac Cohort)  
* Summary: Operative Delta-6 utilized Reyes’s civilian identity for a three-hour window to access the Alfred Hospital’s off-network archive. Loss of personal timepiece (wrist chronometer, QSA issue, traceable via GPS tag) sustained during a logistical error in the extraction corridor. Operative Delta-6 filed the claim under Reyes’s civilian insurance to cover the loss of the watch, which was denied per Section 3A, Paragraph 4, Sub-clause ii. The item was subsequently recovered.  
* Note: The Reyes alias is now flagged for internal QSA audit. Operational use prohibited pending review.

Kain set his coffee mug down.

The target on the tram—the one who had his face, who had an ICS of 32, who had moved with the unsteady gait of a failing man—had passed a note through an alias that Emily had personally used to betray the Threshold Program's director, Martina Solis, four years ago. The QSA had flagged the Reyes alias as compromised and prohibited its use. The replacement network had used it anyway, in the one location—Alfred Hospital—that connected Kain's investigation to Emily's history.

The message was complete. The network was not just chasing Emily. It was using her own institutional crimes to point Kain directly at her.

He looked at the digital clock on the laptop screen. 6:28 AM. The time was gone. The two-hour window for the municipal sweep was over. The router's operational light was still green. He had run the audit in a blind spot.

He closed the terminal, pulled the encryption key, and placed the laptop in the travel bag. He had the name of the file—Solis, M. – Threshold Program Director—and the knowledge that Emily's operational history was tied directly to the Reyes alias. The tram was his next move.

He needed to run the confrontation on the tram with the precision the QSA expected of a man who had not slept in two days, who had a terminal diagnosis, and who had just discovered his handler was running a conspiracy inside a conspiracy. 

\*\*\* 

The 96 tram ran on schedule.

That was the first anomaly.

Kain had spent eleven days on this route — Bourke Street to Nicholson, the long diagonal cut through the city's eastern grid — and the 96 ran on schedule perhaps twice in a working week. Today it arrived at the Bourke Street stop at 7:43 AM, which was the published time, which meant the platform cameras at the Bourke Street Mall interchange had a clean, unobstructed sightline to every boarding passenger for the full forty seconds the doors stood open. No crowd surge. No umbrella obstruction. No delivery vehicle blocking the kerb lane.

The QSA surveillance array was not subtle about what it was. Three cameras per stop, mounted at the standard VicTrack height of 4.2 metres, each unit housing a dual-lens configuration — one optical, one thermal — with a refresh rate that the QSA's technical division had confirmed could resolve a face at twelve metres in 0.3 seconds. The array did not belong to VicTrack. VicTrack's cameras were the older Axis units, grey housings, fixed focal length. These were the newer Pelco Spectra series, matte black, with the slight convex bulge of a varifocal lens that could track a moving subject across three consecutive frames without operator input.

Kain had counted fourteen of them between Bourke and Parliament.

He boarded at the rear doors, tapped his Myki against the reader, and moved to the standing section without looking at the seats. The tram pulled away from the stop. The morning light came through the windows at a low angle, the kind of flat winter light that made every surface look provisional, and the carriage smelled of wet wool and the particular chemical sweetness of hand sanitiser applied over coffee breath.

Forty-three passengers. He had counted them at the stop.

The target was not among them.

\*\*\*

Collins Street. The tram slowed into the stop with the hydraulic sigh of the braking system, and the doors opened onto the grey stone canyon of the financial district. Eleven passengers disembarked. Seven boarded. The Pelco unit above the Collins Street shelter rotated three degrees on its mount — a micro-adjustment, the kind the system made when it was tracking a face rather than scanning a crowd — and Kain shifted his weight to his left foot, putting the standing pole between himself and the camera's primary axis.

Not evasion. Evasion triggered a flag. This was positioning — the difference between a man who knew where the cameras were and a man who was hiding from them.

The doors closed.

The tram moved.

He ran the morning's data. The café on Punt Road had produced a contact at 9:15 AM on three consecutive Tuesdays. The contact was male, mid-thirties, presenting in civilian clothing — no operational tells in the clothing itself, but the seating choice was consistent: the table nearest the rear exit, back to the wall, sightline to the street. The contact arrived before the target. The contact left after the target. The contact never paid — the target settled both bills, which was either a social convention or a financial control mechanism, and Kain had spent two days deciding it was the latter.

The target's name had been in the conference room for fourteen seconds.

He had read it. He had cross-referenced it against the personnel files. He had been three steps into the QSA internal registry when the Bleed had taken the conference room and given him back the Punt Road footpath, and when the Punt Road footpath had resolved back into the conference room, the name was gone from the screen and from his working memory simultaneously, which was not how Bleeds worked — Bleeds took the environment, not the cognition — which meant the erasure was not a Bleed artefact.

It meant something else had cleared the screen.

He had not reported this to Emily.

\*\*\*

Parliament Station. The tram surfaced from the underground section into the grey morning, and the light changed quality — harder, more direct — and the carriage shifted as passengers redistributed their weight in response to the gradient. Kain moved with the shift, keeping his position relative to the rear doors, and the Pelco unit at the Parliament stop tracked the tram's approach with the slow, deliberate rotation of a system that was not looking for a specific face but was logging all of them.

Fourteen cameras between Bourke and Parliament.

He had not touched the target at any of the previous surveillance points. He had not approached. He had not made contact. He had maintained the prescribed distance — twelve metres minimum, the threshold below which the QSA's behavioural analysis software began assigning intent scores to proximity — and he had documented the contact pattern and submitted the documentation to Emily, who had read it and said: \*"Continue observation. Do not engage."\*

That instruction was now forty-eight hours old.

The tram crossed the intersection at Spring Street. The State Library's stone facade passed on the left, and then the residential grid of East Melbourne began — narrower streets, older buildings, the particular density of a neighbourhood that had been expensive for long enough that it had stopped trying to look expensive. The surveillance array thinned here. VicTrack's older Axis units replaced the Pelco series at the Lansdowne Street stop, and the thermal imaging capability dropped out entirely.

Kain noted the gap.

He noted it the way a man notes a loose board in a floor he crosses every day — not with surprise, not with calculation, but with the automatic registration of a structural fact that might become relevant.

\*\*\*

Nicholson Street. The tram stopped.

The target boarded at the front doors.

The recognition was not a shock. It was not a revelation. It was the specific cognitive experience of a pattern completing — the way a word surfaces from the edge of recall, not arriving but simply \*present\*, as though it had always been there and the mind had only just stopped blocking it. The face was the face from the intake photograph. The face was the face from the QSA file. The face was the face that had been in the conference room for fourteen seconds before the screen cleared.

The face was his own.

Not similar. Not a resemblance that required interpretation. The bone structure was identical — the particular geometry of the orbital ridge, the jaw's angle, the way the hairline receded at the left temple two centimetres further than the right. The man was thirty-five years old and six feet tall and the rest of the physical data arrived in fragments, because the rest of the physical data was wrong.

The gait was wrong.

Not wrong in the way of injury — no lateral compensation, no favoured side. Wrong in the way of a system that had lost its calibration. The man moved through the boarding passengers with the specific, over-deliberate placement of someone who could not fully trust the information his feet were sending upward. Each step was a decision rather than a reflex. The left hand found the overhead rail before the body needed it, two beats early, the grip closing with a force that whitened the knuckles — not for balance, but for the anticipatory certainty that balance was about to fail.

The tram lurched forward from the stop.

The man's right hand shot out and caught the seatback beside him. The movement was fast and graceless, the movement of a reflex that had been trained into a body that no longer responded to training at the speed training required. He held the seatback for three seconds after the tram had stabilised. Then he released it, and the release was slow, the fingers uncurling one at a time, as though the hand required individual instructions.

He sat in the third seat from the rear. Left side. Window.

The sitting was not a single motion. It was a sequence — the left hand to the seatback, the controlled lowering, the right hand braced against the armrest, and then the final drop of the last few centimetres that the body could not manage with precision, the small, involuntary impact of a man whose proprioceptive system was no longer reporting the distance between himself and the surface below him accurately. He absorbed the impact without expression. The expression of someone who had been absorbing it for long enough that it no longer registered as an event.

Kain did not move.

The Pelco unit at the Nicholson Street stop had logged the boarding. The system had a face. The system was now running its cross-reference against the QSA registry, and the cross-reference would return a result in approximately ninety seconds, and the result would flag the proximity of two registered subjects — one active field operative, one flagged surveillance target — within the same enclosed transit environment, and the flag would go to Emily's terminal before Kain could reach the next stop.

Ninety seconds.

He counted them.

\*\*\*

The tram moved through the Fitzroy grid. Brunswick Street approaching. The morning traffic thickened at the intersection, and the tram slowed, and the carriage swayed with the deceleration, and the man in the third seat from the rear did not look at the window.

He looked at his hands.

The right hand was on his knee, palm down, and the tremor in it was not large — not the gross motor failure of advanced neurological collapse, but the fine, persistent oscillation of a system fighting to maintain stillness against a signal it could no longer suppress. The fingers moved in a rhythm that had nothing to do with the tram's movement. When the tram swayed, the hand swayed with it. When the tram steadied, the hand did not. The tremor continued at its own frequency, independent of the external environment, the output of an internal process that had stopped responding to the body's attempts at regulation.

The left hand held the folded piece of paper.

The paper was the kind that came from a printer rather than a notebook, folded and unfolded enough times that the creases had gone soft. The left hand held it with a grip that was too tight for the weight of the object — the grip of a hand that had learned to compensate for the right hand's unreliability by over-committing to everything it touched.

Kain's proximity to the rear doors put him at eleven metres from the target.

One metre inside the behavioural threshold.

The QSA's software would be assigning an intent score now. The score would be low — eleven metres in a crowded tram carriage was not operationally significant — but it would be logged, and the log would be appended to the flag, and Emily would have both pieces of data within the next forty seconds.

The man looked up.

Not at Kain. At the carriage ceiling — the specific unfocused look of someone running an internal calculation rather than observing the environment. The look of a man taking inventory of what his body was doing and deciding whether any of it required intervention. Then the look dropped, and it landed on Kain, and it stayed there for two seconds, which was one second longer than a stranger's glance, and in that second the man's expression did not change, which was itself a tell, because a stranger who makes accidental eye contact with another stranger for two seconds either looks away or adjusts their expression, and this man did neither.

He said, quietly, in the register of someone speaking to a person they know rather than a person they are meeting: "You photographed the intake form."

Not a question.

Kain said nothing.

"The M. Reyes name." The man's voice was level. No affect. The voice of someone delivering a briefing rather than initiating a conversation, and the voice was steady in a way the hands were not — whatever the ICS collapse was taking, it had not reached the vocal cords yet, or he had learned to hold the voice still by spending the steadiness he no longer had elsewhere. "You ran it."

"Partial run." The words came out in the same register — flat, operational, the shorthand of two people who understood that the environment was hostile and that every syllable was a data point for the system logging them. "Three steps. Stopped."

"Why."

"Screen cleared."

The man absorbed this. The right hand on his knee continued its independent oscillation. He did not look at it. "The third step connects to a QSA internal file. The file connects to an operational history. The operational history has a name attached to it that your handler has not given you." A pause. The tram slowed for the Brunswick Street stop. "She has the journals."

Kain said nothing.

"The origin point of the replacement methodology." The man's voice did not change. "She has had them for fourteen months. The intake form was the first time the M. Reyes alias appeared in a document you could access. She did not expect you to photograph it."

The Brunswick Street stop. Four passengers disembarked. Three boarded. The Axis unit above the shelter — the older VicTrack model, no thermal, fixed focal length — logged the stop without the Pelco system's cross-referencing capability. The gap in the array.

Kain noted it again.

"The third step," he said.

"Voss." The man said the name the way a man says a word he has been carrying for a long time — not with weight, but with the particular flatness of something that has been handled until it lost its texture. "Emily Voss. The file is under her operational designation, not her name. You would not have found it on the first pass."

The tram moved.

Kain ran the architecture. The M. Reyes alias on the intake form. The QSA internal file. The operational history. Three steps of cross-referencing that a Level 4 field credential could access — Emily had said that herself, in a different context, about a different file, and he had logged it and not used it because there had been no reason to use it until now. The intake form had been in the conference room. He had photographed it. Emily had not known he photographed it, or she had known and had not moved on it, which was the same operational problem.

"She recruited you," the man said. "You know why."

"Dormant carrier." Not a question. The word had been in the QSA file under his own name for three years, flagged as clinically insignificant, which was the designation the QSA applied to things it considered significant enough to monitor but not significant enough to explain.

"The Sphere needed a baseline." The man's voice was still level. Still operational. "A dormant architecture that would not reject the exposure. She needed to know what happened when a carrier with your genotype encountered active Sphere entanglement." A pause. "She has the data now. Fourteen months of it."

The tram crossed the intersection at Alexandra Parade. The Pelco array resumed here — the surveillance gap had lasted four stops, which was not long enough to have done anything useful, but long enough to have had a conversation that the system had not fully logged. Kain ran the calculation. The flag to Emily's terminal would have gone out at the Nicholson Street stop. She would have the proximity data. She would not have the audio.

"The journals," Kain said.

"Alfred Hospital. She keeps them off-network." The man looked back at his hands. At the right hand and its persistent, involuntary frequency. He looked at it the way a man looks at a structural report on a building he still has to live in — with the specific, exhausted attention of someone who has moved past the stage of hoping the numbers are wrong. "The third step will tell you the designation. Run it from a terminal she doesn't monitor."

The tram slowed for the stop at Victoria Parade.

The man stood.

The standing was the sitting in reverse, and worse. The left hand went to the seatback first, the grip closing before the body had committed to the movement, and then the legs engaged in the wrong sequence — the right knee straightening before the left had taken the weight, a coordination failure that sent the upper body forward and to the left, and the left hand on the seatback was the only thing that converted the stumble into something that looked, from a distance, like a man simply rising from a seat. He held the seatback for a full two seconds after he was upright. The carriage had not moved. The tram was still braking into the stop. The instability was not the tram's.

He released the seatback.

The right hand went into his coat pocket, and the pocket was not for warmth — it was to remove the hand from the visible environment, to take the tremor off the page, to present a body that was managing rather than failing. The left hand, still holding the folded paper, dropped to his side.

"Why," Kain said. One word. The question was not *why are you telling me this* — that question had an obvious answer, and obvious answers were not worth the syllables. The question was *why now, why this stop, why this conversation on a Tuesday morning on a tram with fourteen surveillance cameras and a QSA flag already in transit to his handler's terminal*.  
The man stood in the aisle, his weight distributed with the careful, over-deliberate stillness of someone who had learned that stillness was now a performance rather than a default state. The tram completed its deceleration. The doors opened.

"Because terminal men stop waiting for permission," he said. "You know that. You've known it for three weeks."

He moved to the doors.

The movement across the four metres of aisle between his seat and the exit took longer than it should have. Not dramatically longer — not the lurching, visible struggle of a man in crisis. The kind of longer that only registered if the observer was already calibrated to the baseline, already running the comparison between what the body was doing and what the body's architecture should have been capable of. The right foot landed two centimetres further left than the stride required. The left hand touched the overhead rail once, briefly, at the midpoint of the crossing — not gripping, just confirming the rail's location, the way a man confirms a wall in the dark. The right hand stayed in the pocket.

He stepped off the tram onto the Victoria Parade footpath.

The doors closed.

Kain felt the tram shudder through the track points, the vibration a low-frequency hum through his soles, and the man with his face was gone, replaced by the specific configuration of an empty seat on a moving rail carriage. Then the hum became something else—a vibration at the specific frequency of the Sphere’s entanglement field, and the carriage was no longer moving through Melbourne.

The Bleed arrived as a physical theft. It began with the loss of the face—the target’s face, his own face, the precise configuration of the orbital ridge and the jawline that had just delivered the final, devastating piece of institutional betrayal—and the face did not fade. It was yanked from his working memory with a physical, ripping sensation that felt like tearing silk. One moment he knew the face. The next moment, the neural address where the knowledge was stored was blank, replaced by a searing, white heat that drove a spike of pain behind his left eye.

He could no longer recall the face. Or the voice. Or the content of the conversation.  
He stood in the tram carriage, his vision intact, his hearing intact, and he could not account for the preceding sixty seconds. There was the audit, the file, the confirmation of the Voss designation—and then a gap, a chasm of lost data where the culmination of his two-day investigation should have been. The absence was the message. The Bleed had not simply fractured his perception; it had executed a localized, targeted erasure of the information that was most critical to his operational success.

The carriage was full of people. The noise of the tram was a low, insistent rumble. The window showed the Fitzroy grid passing at 40 kilometers per hour, the brickwork and the steel and the winter sky. All of it was present-tense, undeniable, tactile reality. The memory was the lie.

He pressed his right hand flat against the advertising panel beside the door. The metal was cold, slightly slick with condensation. He forced his cognitive focus onto the coldness, onto the precise, granular sensation of the metal, using it as an anchor. The internal pain—the white heat behind his eye, the raw neurological static—did not recede. It intensified, the system fighting itself, the foreign, intrusive signal of the Bleed attempting to override the tactile reality he was feeding it.

He began counting.

One second. The tram swayed slightly as it took the corner at Brunswick Street. He tracked the movement with his legs, adjusting the distribution of his weight, forcing his body to remain in the carriage he was actually standing in.

Five seconds. The pressure in his head intensified to a crushing, physical force, the external reality of the tram beginning to fracture at the edges. The sound of the engine warped—the high-frequency whine slowing to a deep, agonizing growl, the acoustic signature of reality decoupling from the physical event.

Ten seconds. The face was gone, but the word remained. Not the name on the file, not the designation, but a single, abstract noun that the Bleed had failed to erase: Solis. Martina Solis. The name was a key, a link to the journals, a final piece of leverage against Emily. He locked onto the word, forcing the neurological signal to prioritize the abstract truth of the name over the sensory lie the Bleed was attempting to impose.

Twenty seconds. The scent arrived. Not ozone, not the sharp, synthetic smell of the Sphere, but something organic and cloying—the smell of burning fenugreek. He knew the scent. He had documented it in Hayden’s jump logs, the sensory residue of a parallel transition, a scene that belonged to the other man, an intrusion of a domestic memory that was not his own. The smell choked him, replacing the neutral air of the carriage with the intimate, invasive reality of a life he had never lived.

Thirty seconds. He coughed, a dry, involuntary hack that drew the attention of the passenger nearest the rear door. He did not look at the passenger. He kept his attention locked on the cold metal of the advertising panel, his fingers digging into the seam between the panel and the doorframe. The train was now traveling through two simultaneous environments: the wet-wool smell of the Melbourne tram, and the hot, invasive scent of the Hayden/Bell safe house.

Forty seconds. The physical collapse started in his left leg. Not a tremor, but a loss of tone—the muscle fiber relaxing its grip on the bone, the limb threatening to buckle beneath his weight. He shifted his weight, compensating with the right leg, a movement so precise and rapid that the passenger beside him did not register it as a failure. He had practiced this compensation for four years. The Bleed was now attacking the architecture he had constructed for survival.

Fifty seconds. He closed his eyes, cutting off the visual input, forcing the internal chaos to resolve itself in the auditory field. The sound of the tram was a violent, multi-directional screech, the noise of two separate vehicles traveling on the same track at incompatible speeds. He focused on his own pulse: fast, irregular, a frantic drumming against the cold metal. The pulse was the ultimate anchor. The pulse was the clock.

Sixty seconds. The fenugreek scent receded, replaced by the faint, miserable smell of old coffee in the tram’s floor. The white heat behind his eye began to dim, withdrawing to a dull, localized throbbing. The neurological static slowed to a manageable hum.

Sixty-one seconds.

He opened his eyes. The tram was still in Fitzroy, the architecture outside the window solid, specific, and real. The passenger was still looking at him, a flicker of concern in their expression. Kain nodded, a small, controlled gesture of acknowledgment that meant I am fine, move on. The passenger looked away.

The face was still gone. The specifics of the conversation were gone. He knew, abstractly, that the man on the tram was his own replacement, a terminal agent with a message. He knew the conversation had contained three pieces of data: a link, a designation, and a confrontation with Emily.

But the final piece of the conversation, the specific information about Voss and the operational designation that linked her to the Reyes alias—that was gone. The Bleed had erased it entirely, taking the highest-value data and leaving only the visceral, physical certainty of the attack.

He leaned his forehead against the cold metal of the door. The contact was solid, cold, real. He ran the inventory again, checking the internal systems against the physical clock. Sixty-one seconds. The longest collapse to date. The first one to leave a permanent hole in his working memory.

The tram slowed for the stop at Victoria Parade.

The memory was lost, but the instruction remained. The target was Emily. The confrontation was now immediate.

He pushed off the door and moved toward the frontof the carriage. He needed a secure line, a clean room, and twenty minutes to run the audit before she could flag his presence in the city and shut down the operation entirely.   
\*\*\*   
The folded paper was still on the seat. The Pelco unit was still logging. His phone was vibrating in his jacket pocket with the particular rhythm of Emily's direct line — not a message, a call, which meant the flag had arrived and she had not waited for the audio analysis.

Six minutes had been optimistic.

He let it ring.

He had six stops before the terminus. Six stops in which the Pelco array would log his position, his stillness, his failure to retrieve an object that the behavioural software would eventually flag as a deliberate leave-behind or an operational drop, depending on which analyst was running the morning's review. He had six stops in which Emily's call would go to voicemail and she would decide what that meant. He had six stops in which the QSA's cross-reference engine would finish its work on the Nicholson Street boarding data and produce a report that would sit in Emily's queue alongside the proximity flag and the unanswered call, and the three data points together would form a picture that Emily was already capable of reading.

She had been capable of reading it for fourteen months.

The tram slowed for the next stop.

Kain reached across the aisle and picked up the folded paper.

The Pelco unit logged the movement. The behavioural software assigned it a score. The score would be in Emily's queue in ninety seconds, appended to everything else, and the picture would be complete, and she would know that he had it, and she would know that he had chosen to take it rather than leave it, which was the same as telling her that the conversation on the tram had happened and that he had listened and that he had not immediately reported it.

He put the paper in his jacket pocket without unfolding it.

The tram moved.

His phone stopped vibrating.

In thirty seconds it would start again.
