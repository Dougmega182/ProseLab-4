# **EPILOGUE — THE WAKING WORLD**

Three weeks after the Alfred, the QSA sent Emily.

Not officially. Officially, the QSA had entered what the internal communications — Kain had seen two of them, forwarded from a source he had not asked to identify — described as a \*structural review period\*, which was the organisation's preferred language for the process of determining which of its senior personnel had known about the Replacement Network and which had merely suspected it and said nothing, and whether the distinction between those two categories was legally meaningful. The review was being conducted by a committee whose membership was classified at a level above the committee's own access clearance, which was either an administrative error or a deliberate architecture, and Kain had spent approximately four minutes considering which before deciding it did not affect his immediate situation and setting the question aside.

Emily came on a Tuesday, in the late afternoon, when the light through the Fitzroy flat's west-facing window was the specific amber of autumn in Melbourne, low and long and full of suspended dust. She knocked twice. He had heard her on the stairs — her footfall was distinctive, a slight favouring of the left foot that she had carried since the Canberra incident three years ago and had never had properly assessed — and he had already moved the CDIR file from the table to the kitchen bench before she reached the landing.

He opened the door with his right hand. His left arm hung at his side, the hand in the position it had settled into over the past three weeks: fingers loosely curled, palm inward, the wrist at a slight angle that the physiotherapist at the Royal Melbourne had described as \*the resting posture of a denervated limb\* and that Kain had begun to think of simply as the hand's final opinion on the matter.

Emily looked at the arm. Then at his face. Then at the iridescent fringe at the left edge of his visual field, which she could not see but which he was aware of in the way he was aware of all persistent data — as a constant, low-level input that had been integrated into his baseline and no longer required active processing.

"You look terrible," she said.

"Come in," he said.  
\*\*\*

She sat at the table. He made coffee — one-handed, which he had been practising, the kettle held by the handle with his right hand, the mug steadied against the bench by his hip — and set it in front of her without comment. She wrapped both hands around it and looked at the table's surface, which was bare except for a ring-stain from a previous mug and a small crack in the laminate that ran diagonally from the near edge toward the centre and stopped.

He sat across from her and waited.

"The committee has a name for what happened," she said. "They're calling it a \*dimensional sovereignty incident\*. The language is being standardised across all internal documentation."

"That's efficient," he said.

"Twelve senior network coordinators have been suspended pending review. The Replacement Network's operational infrastructure — the relay nodes, the insertion points, the monitoring architecture — has been taken offline in forty-one of the forty-seven identified branch realities." She paused. "The remaining six are under what they're calling \*supervised continuity\*."

"Meaning they're still running."

"Meaning the committee hasn't decided what to do with them yet." She looked up from the table. "The official position is that the near-collapse of the baseline was a contained event with no civilian exposure. The Alfred's sublevel has been decommissioned. The housing has been filled with concrete."

He thought about the Sphere's lattice, the 847 coordinates of copper wire, now entombed four floors below a hospital that was still treating patients, still running its ordinary machinery of illness and recovery above a sealed room that contained the physical record of the closest the baseline reality had come to ceasing to be itself. He thought about this for a moment and then stopped thinking about it, because the thinking did not change the concrete.

"And Hayden," he said.

Emily's hands moved slightly on the mug. "The committee's position is that Hayden Marsh ceased to exist as a discrete biological entity at 14:09:47 on the fourteenth. His QSA file has been closed. His designation has been retired."

"That's not what I asked."

A pause. Outside, a tram passed on Smith Street, its bell sounding once at the intersection — a clean, single note that arrived through the window and moved through the room and was gone. Kain noticed that he note's resonance lingered a fraction longer.  than it should have, a half-second of sustained frequency in the flat's air that was not an echo, because the room's acoustics did not produce echoes, and that registered at 7.83 Hz if he had been equipped to measure it, which he was not, but which his nervous system — the dormant architecture, the PQ pathways that had been built without his consent over eleven months and that were not going away simply because the map was closed — registered as a quality of the air, a density, the specific atmospheric weight of a presence that was not a presence but was not entirely an absence either.

The tram's bell faded. The room returned to its ordinary acoustic properties.

"No," Emily said quietly. "That's not what you asked."

She did not answer the actual question. He had not expected her to. The actual question did not have an answer that the committee's language could contain, and Emily was, above everything else, a precise woman who did not reach for words that did not fit.  
He picked up his coffee with his right hand and drank it. 

\*\*\*

She left the CDIR file on the table when she went. Not the redacted version — the operational one, his operational one, the file that documented eleven months of his own case history in the QSA's clinical register. She set it down without comment, the way you set down something that belongs to someone and that you have been carrying for them longer than was appropriate.

He did not open it until she was gone and her footsteps had descended the stairs and the street door had closed below.

The file was 340 pages. He read the first twelve and the last four and set it aside. The first twelve established what he already knew about the PQ architecture's progression. The last four contained the committee's formal assessment of his operational status, which was: \*indefinite suspension pending physiological review\*, which was the QSA's language for \*we do not know what you are now and we are not prepared to find out\*.

He found that he did not have a strong feeling about this.

He closed the file and left it on the table and went to the window.

\*\*\*

The street below was doing what streets did in the late afternoon in autumn: filling with the specific pedestrian density of people moving between the day's obligations and the evening's, their trajectories purposeful and overlapping, their faces carrying the ordinary preoccupations of people who had not spent the previous three weeks recalibrating their sensory baseline to account for a permanent iridescent fringe in their left visual field. A woman with a pram negotiated the kerb cut. Two men in high-visibility vests stood outside the café on the corner with takeaway cups, their conversation inaudible through the glass. A dog on a lead investigated the base of a power pole with the focused attention of an animal for whom the present moment was the only moment.

The iridescent fringe caught the amber light and refracted it slightly, laying a thin prismatic edge along the left side of everything he looked at — the power pole, the café's awning, the far edge of the woman's pram — so that the world appeared, in his left peripheral vision, to be very slightly more than itself. Not distorted. Not overlaid. Simply edged with a quality of light that was not quite the light that was there, a permanent record of the threshold he had crossed written into the optics of his own eye.

He had asked the Royal Melbourne's neurologist whether it would resolve.

The neurologist had said: *probably not*.

He had said: *all right* and made a note of it and moved on, because the fringe was data, and data was not a problem to be solved, it was a condition to be incorporated, and he had been incorporating conditions for eleven months and was reasonably practised at it.

\*\*\*  
The lighter was on the kitchen bench.

He had taken it out of his pocket on the first night back in the flat and set it there, and it had remained there for three weeks in the specific way that objects remain in a place when you have not decided what to do with them but are not ready to decide yet. He picked it up now with his right hand — the tremor was present, fine and persistent, the hand's new baseline — and held it.

The brass casing was warm from the afternoon light that had been falling on it through the kitchen window. The engraving: \*N.K. — Solis — Case 7.\* His thumbnail found the letters by habit, pressing into the worn grooves. The flint wheel sat under his thumb, ridged and familiar and entirely spent.

He thought about Solis in the Punt Road flat, in the kitchen that was not a kitchen, with the red pen and the solved file and the coffee that was still hot. He thought about the specific quality of her not looking at him when she said \*you're going back anyway\*, the way she had always not looked at him when she already knew the answer to a question and was giving him the courtesy of arriving at it himself.

He did not think about this for long. It was not useful to think about it for long.

He crossed the flat to the window and stood there with the lighter in his right hand and looked at the street below, at the woman with the pram now halfway down the block, at the two men in high-visibility vests finishing their coffee, at the dog completing its investigation of the power pole and moving on with the satisfied air of an animal whose data collection had been thorough and whose conclusions were sound.

Then he set the lighter on the windowsill.

Not a ceremony. Not a placement with intention. He simply set it there, in the amber light, where it would be visible from the street if anyone looked up, which no one did, because people on streets in the late afternoon do not look up at the windows of Fitzroy flats, and the lighter sat on the sill with its engraving facing outward and its flint wheel pointing at the glass and its fuel nine years gone, and it was exactly what it was: a broken object that had done its work.

He left it there and went back to the table and sat down.

\*\*\*

His left hand rested on the table's surface, curled in its final posture, the palm against the laminate. He could feel the laminate's texture pressed through the hand — not clearly, not with the resolution of a functional sensory pathway, but as a faint pressure, a ghost of contact, enough to confirm that the hand was touching something real. He pressed it slightly harder against the table. The pressure increased. The laminate was cool and slightly rough where the surface coating had worn near the edge, and the crack ran diagonally from that worn edge toward the centre and stopped, and the ring-stain from the previous mug was a pale circle with a darker outer rim, and the afternoon light lay across all of it in long amber bars through the venetian blinds, and the dust moved in the light the way dust moves when the air in a room is still and warm and undisturbed.

He sat with his dead hand on the real table in the real room and looked at the dust moving in the light.

Outside, another tram passed. Its bell sounded at the intersection — one clean note, and then the sustained resonance that was not quite an echo, the half-second of frequency that the room's acoustics could not account for, that arrived and held and then released, the way a breath releases, the way a held thing is finally let go.

The dust moved. The light moved with it, slowly, the amber deepening toward the specific gold of late afternoon giving way to evening, the day's last hour doing what it always did, which was to make ordinary things look briefly like more than they were, and then to let them be what they were, which was enough.

Kain sat in the quiet and let the room be the room.
