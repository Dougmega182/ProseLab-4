# **CHAPTER 14 — THE INFILTRATION**

Kain knew this because the relay's bearing — forty-seven degrees east of magnetic north — required distance to resolve into direction, and direction required a fixed reference point, and the only fixed reference point the scrubbed building had left him was the compass geometry of its own walls.

Twenty seconds — not for decision-making, but because at 19% buffer efficiency the body required that interval to establish that the air beyond the door was a categorically different environment from the air in the relay plant room. The crossing would register in the binding sites the way a depth change registers in a diver's ears — not gradually, but as a pressure event, immediate and total. 

He opened the door.

It came at the same fine-misted angle as it had all morning, the kind of rain that Melbourne in July produced as a default condition rather than a weather event — not falling, exactly, but present, occupying the air at a density that made outdoor and indoor a matter of degree rather than distinction. His boots found the front path. The gate latch was a standard residential fitting, mechanical only, the QSA scrub protocol having no interest in garden gates. It opened.

Not the administrative emptiness of the safe house — the absence of people who had been removed. The ordinary emptiness of a residential street at whatever hour this was, in a city that was conducting its infrastructure operations and its commuter flows and its civic rhythms on the other side of a QSA scrub that had excised one address from its registry while leaving every adjacent address intact. The terrace on Alfred Crescent's right still drew power. The one on the left still connected to the grid. The gap between them — the gap that had been a house and was now a documented absence — did not register on any instrument the city used to know itself.

The QSA did not need to kill a building to erase it.

Forty-one metres.

He moved at the pace of a man with an ordinary destination — the specific tempo of someone crossing a residential street in the rain without an umbrella, purposeful but not hurried, the universally understood posture of a person who accepted that they were already wet and had somewhere to be. The journals under his left arm absorbed the rain across the top surface of the uppermost notebook in the specific, rapid way that institutional paper absorbed moisture: from dry to damp in twenty seconds, from damp to heavy in forty. He shifted the stack inward, pressing the spine edges against the underside of his arm, attempting to shield the pages from the rain's angle with the waterproofed material of his jacket's inner panel.
