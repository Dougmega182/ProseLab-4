# CHAPTER 2 — THE MOUNTAIN**

Betty's headlight — the surviving one — carved a tunnel through the rain as Kain turned south onto the M1.

Emily sat in the passenger seat with the tablet on her lap, its glow the only light in the cabin. She hadn't spoken since they'd left Brunswick Street. Kain hadn't pushed. He was still turning Chen's schedule over in his mind, rotating every piece of evidence slowly, from every angle, looking for the gap.

7:30 tram. Coffee with cream. Workout at 7:00.

Three deviations. Three markers left by a man who knew he was about to disappear. Not random. Deliberate. Evidence requiring the reader to already know the original pattern.

Chen hadn't left a message. He'd left a lock. And the key was knowing him.

The M1 south of the city was a different road at midnight. During the day it carried the dense, stop-start traffic of the freight corridors — the heavy-lift hover-utes running produce and manufactured components between the port and the inland distribution hubs, their magnetic seals generating the low-frequency vibration that residents of the Dandenong corridor had long since stopped registering as sound and had begun to register as weather. At midnight the road belonged to the long-haul operators and the occasional private vehicle, and Betty's single headlight moved through the dark with the particular loneliness of something that had no business being where it was.

The rain came in from the south-west, driving hard against the windscreen. The wipers ran at their highest setting and still left a half-second of blindness at the top of each stroke. Kain drove at a speed that acknowledged the conditions without conceding to them.

"How far?" he asked.

"Two hours. Maybe more in this." Emily nodded at the rain.

"You want to tell me where we're going?"

"Wilsons Promontory. The mountain."

"There's nothing on that mountain except hiking trails and a car park."

Emily didn't answer. Her fingers moved across the tablet, pulling up a route Kain had never seen — a road that branched off the main trail and climbed the eastern face of Mount Oberon in a series of switchbacks that looked, on screen, like a wound.

"That road doesn't exist," Kain said.

"It does if you know where to look."

\*\*\*   
They passed through Dandenong at 12:40. The city's southern edge had the quality of a sentence that had been abandoned mid-thought — the heritage-listed terraces giving way to the flat, functional geometry of the logistics precincts, which gave way in turn to the dark, wet nothing of the Cranbourne arm of the South Gippsland Highway. The sodium lights thinned. The road narrowed. The rain intensified.

"Seventy-two hours to erase someone and put a copy in their place," Kain said.

"Yes."

"And you're telling me this because it's interesting, or because; there's a clock running right now?"

Emily didn't answer immediately. That was answer enough.

"Three weeks ago," she said, "we intercepted a communication suggesting the next replacement is scheduled for someone in Melbourne. Someone connected to the original investigation into Chen's death."

Kain's hands tightened on the wheel. 

"How long?"

"The intercept was timestamped six hours before I contacted you. If the seventy-two-hour pattern holds, we have roughly sixty-five hours from now."

"Who's the target?"

"We don't know. But the connection to Chen's investigation narrows the list." She paused. "It's a list that includes me. And now it includes you."

They drove in silence for twenty minutes. The Strzelecki Ranges pressed against the skyline to the south‑east — not visible yet, but there, the way large landmasses are present in the dark, a density in the air, a change in the way sound moved. The road began its first long curve away from the coast and into the ranges proper, and the temperature dropped two degrees in the space of a kilometre.

Emily broke the silence.

"I need to tell you something," she said. "Before we get there."

"Another thing you're not telling me, or one of the ones you are?"

She didn't answer immediately. He kept his eyes on the road.

"Elias Varn," she said.

The name landed. He kept his hands on the wheel and said nothing, and she let the silence run.

\*\*\*  
Fifteen years ago, Elias Varn had been a mid-level procurement officer in the Department of Infrastructure's quantum relay division — the bureaucratic layer responsible for managing the civilian contracts that maintained the entanglement relay towers across Victoria's eastern corridor. He had held the position for six years. His performance reviews were adequate. His security clearance was Level 2\. He was, by every institutional measure, unremarkable.

Kain had been assigned to him by accident. A routine audit of the relay division's contractor payments had flagged an anomaly in the billing cycle — a rounding error, the kind that accumulated over years and eventually surfaced as a discrepancy too small to be fraud and too consistent to be mistake. The audit had been assigned to Kain because he was available and because the division chief had wanted it closed quickly and quietly. It was not the kind of case that made careers.

He had spent the first week reviewing Varn's contractor files and finding nothing. He had spent the second week reviewing Varn's movement logs and finding nothing. He had spent the third week sitting in a coffee shop across the road from the Department of Infrastructure's Level 4 entrance and watching Elias Varn arrive at work.

Varn arrived at 8:47 every morning. Not 8:45. Not 8:50. 8:47, with a variance of plus or minus ninety seconds that Kain had tracked across eleven consecutive working days. He arrived from the north, which meant he took the 8:31 tram from Fitzroy North — a route that required a transfer at Smith Street and delivered him to the Flinders Lane stop at 8:44, leaving him three minutes to walk the two blocks to the Department entrance. The timing was not coincidental. It was the product of a man who had optimized his commute to the point where it had become automatic — where the 8:31 tram was not a choice but a reflex, as fixed as a heartbeat.

On the twenty-third day of Kain's observation, Varn arrived at 9:12.

9:12, from the south — wrong time, wrong direction, which meant he had taken a different tram from a different stop on a different line. He carried a coffee from a vendor Kain had never seen him use — a flat white in a paper cup with a blue logo, from the chain that operated out of the Southern Cross concourse. He walked faster than usual. He held the coffee in his left hand, which was wrong, because Elias Varn was right-handed and had carried his coffee in his right hand on every one of the twenty-two preceding days.

Kain had gone back to his desk and pulled Varn's full movement history for the preceding six months.

The 8:31 tram from Fitzroy North. Every working day. Without exception. For six months.

Until the twenty-third day.

He had then pulled Varn's personal records — the kind that required a Level 4 clearance and a written justification that Kain had spent forty minutes constructing with the care of someone building a legal argument. Medical history. Financial records. Social contact logs. The passive optical record from Varn's apartment building, which showed Elias Varn entering his apartment at 6:23 PM on the twenty-second day and not emerging until 7:41 AM on the twenty-third.

Except that the man who had entered the apartment at 6:23 PM had a gait pattern — the specific, measurable geometry of stride length, hip rotation, and weight distribution — that differed from the man who had emerged at 7:41 AM by a margin of 4.3 percent.

4.3 percent. Within the range of normal daily variation. Within the range that every automated system was calibrated to accept as noise.

Kain had flagged it anyway. His supervisor had told him he was wasting the department's time. His Commander had told Kain the same thing, with less patience. He had filed the flag regardless, attached it to a report that nobody read, and continued watching Elias Varn.

Over the following six weeks, he had documented thirty-seven additional anomalies. The coffee. The commute. The hand. The way Varn now held his security pass — right hand, badge facing out — when he had previously held it left hand, badge facing in, for six years. The way he no longer stopped at the third-floor vending machine on Tuesdays, because the machine stocked a specific brand of salted crackers that the fourth-floor machine did not. The way he now took the lift instead of the stairs, despite the fact that the original Elias Varn had taken the stairs every day because he had a mild but documented knee condition that made the lift's acceleration uncomfortable.

The replacement had Varn's face. His voice. His fingerprints. His retinal pattern. His complete institutional history, memorized to a depth that had fooled every colleague, every automated system, and every security protocol the Department of Infrastructure employed.

It had not memorized the crackers.

Kain had taken his report to the QSA's Civilian Interface Division — the only body with the authority and the technical capability to act on what he had found. He had presented thirty-seven anomalies, six weeks of movement logs, and a gait analysis that he had commissioned privately, at his own expense, from a biomechanics laboratory in Carlton that specialized in forensic motion capture.

The QSA had taken the report. They had thanked him. They had told him the matter was under investigation and that he would be contacted when there was something to report.

He had never been contacted.

Three months later, his position at the Department of Infrastructure had been eliminated in a restructure. His contractor licence had been suspended pending a review that had never concluded. His access to the government movement log database — the database he had used to build the Varn case — had been revoked on the grounds that his clearance level no longer justified it.

He had spent the following four years doing the kind of work that didn't require a clearance level. Missing persons. Insurance fraud. The occasional domestic surveillance job that he took because the rent was fixed and his principles were not. 

\*\*\*   
"You knew," he said.

Emily's hands rested on the tablet. She didn't move them.

"I was the one who received your report," she said. "I was a senior analyst by then. Seven years in. I read it, I assessed it, and I escalated it to my division director with a recommendation that it be acted on immediately."

"And?"

"And my division director agreed with the recommendation. The operation to extract and assess the Varn replacement was authorized within forty-eight hours of your report landing on my desk." She paused. "The operation was successful. The replacement was removed. The intelligence gathered from that extraction informed the QSA's understanding of the Replacement Network for the next decade."

The road climbed. The ranges closed in on both sides — with the slow, deliberate pressure of old terrain that had been folding itself for longer than the city behind them had existed, ungiven to drama, ungiven to the sudden verticality of the alpine country further north. The eucalypts pressed against the road's edge, their bark pale and wet in Betty's headlight, their canopy invisible above the rain. 

"And my career," Kain said.

"Was a necessary operational cost." Well aware of what she said it came without inflection or apology. "If you had remained in your position with your clearance intact, the network would have identified you as the source of the Varn extraction. You would have been a target."

"So you made me unemployable instead."

"We made you invisible. There's a difference."

"There isn't," Kain said. "Not from where I was standing."  
He had spent those four years learning to breathe through the phantom limb of a career she had amputated without anaesthetic. It wasn't just the revoked clearance; it was the systematic dissolution of a decade's worth of professional capital. Every contact he'd cultivated in the Department — people he'd traded favors with, analysts who understood the shorthand of his intuition — had gone cold the moment his status flipped to "administratively terminated." He changed lanes without signalling, then signalled anyway, a beat too late, for no one. The high-fidelity access to government movement databases had been replaced by the scraping of public registries and the desperate, low-stakes data-mining of insurance fraud claims. To survive, he'd had to build a secondary, cynical architecture within himself — a self-contained bunker where he filed his resentment next to his principles, both of them becoming equally negotiable in the face of a mounting rent deficit. She had made him invisible to protect the QSA, but in doing so, she had forced him to inhabit a version of himself he neither recognised nor particularly liked.  

He drove. The rain drove. The ranges absorbed both.

\*\*\*   
The road the QSA used branched off the main Wilsons Prom. access track at a point that was not marked on any civilian navigation system and was not visible from the main track unless you knew the precise angle of approach. Emily directed him to it with the calm specificity of someone reading from memory rather than from the tablet — left at the second fire trail marker, then two hundred metres on the unsealed surface, then right at the stand of mountain ash that had been struck by lightning at some point in the preceding decade and had grown back in a shape that was less a tree than an argument.

The unsealed surface was wet clay over granite, and Betty's tyres found purchase with the reluctant, incremental grip of something that was trying its best under difficult conditions. The switchbacks climbed the eastern face of Mount Oberon in the series of tight reversals that had looked, on the tablet's screen, like a scar. Up close, in the dark and the rain, they felt like one.

At the third switchback, Kain's stomach turned.

Not from the road. The road was bad, but it was navigable. The nausea came from somewhere else — a pressure with no obvious source, a low-frequency wrongness that registered in the gut before it registered anywhere else. His vision held. His hands were steady. Something in the air was wrong in a way his body had no prior category for. 

"Proximity field," Emily said, without looking up from the tablet. "You'll adjust in a few minutes. Most people do."

"Most…."

"The ones who don't are usually the ones we're more interested in." She glanced at him. "You're adjusting."

The field — a coherence-suppression perimeter designed to prevent any quantum-capable device from operating within a two-kilometre radius of the facility without authorisation — had been installed in 2119, when the QSA was consolidating its eastern Australian infrastructure after the Canberra relay network collapsed. The mountain had been chosen for the same reasons mountains had always been chosen for things that needed to be hidden. Granite bedrock absorbed certain electromagnetic frequencies with an efficiency no engineered material had matched. Elevation and isolation did the rest.   
The facility itself had no name in any document that a civilian clearance level could access. Internal QSA communications referred to it as the Threshold. The name had been chosen, Emily said, by the facility's first director — a woman named Martina Solis, who had spent twenty-two years building the quantum insertion program from a theoretical framework into an operational reality, and who had retired in 2141 with a commendation that was classified at the same level as the program itself.

"Solis," Kain said. "That name is in Chen's file."

"Chen worked under her. He was one of her original research team." Emily's voice carried something calibrated — the register of someone managing information rather than sharing it. "Her notes on the early insertion trials are part of what we're going to be working from."

"Where is she now?"

Emily looked back at the tablet. "Retired," she said.

The word landed with the dead weight of words that are technically accurate and functionally incomplete. Kain filed it and kept driving.

The nausea had not gone. It had settled — moved from the acute wrongness of the first contact to a background pressure, a constant low-level reminder that the body's instruments were reading something they had not been designed to read. He drove through it the way he drove through everything: by keeping his hands on the wheel and his eyes on the road and his attention on the next thing that needed to happen.

The facility's entrance resolved out of the dark at the top of the final switchback — a section of the mountain's face that was not quite the same texture as the rock on either side of it. A door, set flush into the granite, with no visible handle and no visible lock and a single recessed panel at shoulder height that glowed a faint, cold blue in the rain.

Emily got out of the car. The rain hit her immediately, and she didn't react to it. She crossed to the panel, pressed her palm flat against it, and held it there for four seconds.

The door opened inward — a sound like a held breath releasing, the hydraulic seals disengaging with a low, pressurised exhale, and then the steel's weight taking over, pulling it through its arc with the slow inevitability of something that had been waiting a long time to move. The light beyond it reached the wet ground before Kain's eyes had adjusted — white and absolute, the kind of light that made shadows look like accusations.

Kain killed Betty's engine. He sat for a moment in the dark, in the rain, in the low persistent pressure of the proximity field. He thought about Elias Varn's crackers. About the 4.3 percent variance in a gait pattern that every automated system had been calibrated to accept as noise. About the three years he had spent doing insurance fraud because his principles were not fixed and his rent was.

He thought about Martina Solis, retired.

He pulled the door handle and got out.
