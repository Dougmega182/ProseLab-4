The study door opened inward. The hinges had been oiled within the last year — the swing was smooth, no resistance, no sound beyond the displacement of air against the frame.

The room was seven metres by four. Kain did not enter. He stood at the threshold and let the space tell him what it carried.

Shelving covered three walls, floor to ceiling, industrial steel with adjustable brackets at fifteen-centimetre intervals. The shelves held journals. Hardbound, A5, dark green covers. He counted the first shelf: fourteen. The second: fourteen. The pattern held across the visible rows. Seven shelves per wall section, three wall sections. He stopped counting at two hundred and ninety-four because the bottom shelf on the far wall was obscured by a filing crate.

The spines were unmarked. No titles, no dates, no indexing system visible from the door. The green dye had faded unevenly — volumes nearest the single window had bleached to something closer to sage, while those on the interior wall retained their original pigment. The window faced south. The fading was consistent with years of indirect light.

He stepped inside. The air carried dust — fine, mineral, the kind that accumulated on paper stored in a room where the climate control had been set and never adjusted. The thermostat on the wall read nineteen degrees. The humidity indicator beside it read forty-two percent. Archival conditions. Someone had calibrated this room for preservation.

Kain pulled the nearest journal from the shelf at shoulder height. The binding cracked — a sound that placed the last opening at months, possibly longer. The pages were unlined. The handwriting was small, controlled, left-leaning, consistent across the visible spread. He turned to the inside front cover.

No adhesive label. No barcode. No manifest reference number.

He checked the second volume. The same. The third. The same.

The personal effects manifest — stamped SOLIS, M. / PERSONAL EFFECTS MANIFEST / INTAKE DATE [REDACTED] — listed forty-seven items. Clothing, electronics, one photograph, two books, one wristwatch. No journals. The manifest was complete. These journals had never been logged.

He returned to the first volume and read the opening entry. The date was partially redacted — day and month visible, year removed with a single stroke of black ink applied by a different hand than the text. The entry described a calibration sequence for Subject 1. Insertion parameters. Coherence thresholds. Recovery intervals measured in hours, then minutes, then seconds as the methodology refined across the page. The clinical language was precise and self-correcting — annotations in the margins where the original phrasing had been struck through and replaced, sometimes twice, the replacements growing more specific each time.

He turned pages. Subject 2. Subject 3. The methodology tightened. Recovery intervals shortened. By Subject 5, the entries had developed a notation system — shorthand symbols in the margins that recurred with enough regularity to constitute a private index. Kain did not recognise the symbols. He photographed three pages with the comm device and moved on.

The second shelf held journals from a later period. The handwriting was the same but the ink had changed — blue-black to a darker formulation that sat heavier on the page. The entries here were longer. Subject 7 appeared on the fourteenth page of the volume he opened. First clean insertion at sixty-eight hours. Behavioural integration at ninety-four percent at seventy-two hours. The remaining six percent was described in a passage he read twice:

*The residue — the behavioural patterns so deeply encoded in the original subject's neurology that they survive the transfer and surface in the replacement as habits the replacement cannot account for.*

He closed the journal. Replaced it on the shelf at the exact angle from which he had taken it — a reflex from fifteen years of evidence handling that operated whether or not the evidence would ever be formally catalogued.

The room contained no desk. No chair. No reading lamp. No surface where someone might sit and work with these volumes. The journals had been stored here. They had not been used here.

He stood in the centre of the room and turned a full rotation. Two hundred and ninety-four journals, minimum. One notation system he could not read. One methodology that refined itself across what appeared to be years of continuous work. One room calibrated for preservation at specifications that exceeded residential standards.

The thermostat had been set. The humidity had been set. The shelving had been installed with institutional hardware. Someone had built this room to keep these journals intact for longer than the journals' author would need them.

Kain photographed the thermostat, the humidity gauge, the shelving brackets, and the window. He did not photograph the journal contents beyond the three initial pages. The comm device's storage was finite and the apartment had more rooms.

He stepped back into the hallway and left the study door open at the angle he had found it.

The mug occupied the second shelf of a wall-mounted cabinet whose door stood ajar by four centimetres. Deep blue ceramic, unglazed base, gold lettering on the side facing outward: A&S. No period, no additional text. The gold had been applied by hand — slight variation in the downstroke of the A, the irregularity of a brush held by a person.

The cabinet's other contents: four white mugs, identical, handles oriented right. Procurement standard. The blue mug's handle faced left. Placed by a different hand, or by the same hand operating under a different intention.

Kain photographed it with the tablet's rear camera. No flash. Available light from the window over the sink — late afternoon, south-facing, colour flattened but detail preserved. The gold lettering resolved cleanly against the blue ground. He did not touch the mug.

Below the cabinet, a single sheet of cream notepaper lay under a magnetic clip. The clip was QSA standard. The paper was not. One line in a hand he did not recognise, small and left-slanting: *14.11.38 — received.* November fourteenth, 2138. Three years before Solis's official retirement. He photographed the note.

Of the thirty-one items he had accounted for in the apartment, twenty-eight occupied positions consistent with institutional arrangement — objects placed for documentation, edges parallel, coats buttoned on identical hangers, books shelved to a single plane with no volume pulled forward from recent handling. Three items broke the pattern. The journals, absent from the manifest. The notepaper with its unfamiliar hand. The blue mug with its gold letters.

He closed the cabinet door to its original gap. Confirmed the notepaper's position against his entry photograph. Checked the linoleum for shoe prints, the bench surface for finger oils under the angled light. None.

At the front door he turned the latch with his sleeve over his fingers. The lock engaged behind him. Everything in the apartment remained where it had been, arranged with the precision of something assembled for viewing that had never expected to be read.
