The lock was a residential-grade Lockwood 001 deadbolt with six pins. The pick took eleven seconds. Kain held the tension wrench steady with his left hand and did not enter until the bolt had cleared the strike plate by its full throw.

The hallway was narrow. Coats on three hooks, the fourth hook empty, its mounting screw carrying a faint oxidation ring where a coat's weight had prevented airflow for years. Kain stood in the doorway and let the space arrive.

Four years of managed air. The QSA's property holding structure had maintained climate control. The thermostat on the hallway wall read nineteen degrees, its programming unchanged since the last human adjustment. No dust on the thermostat's upper edge. Dust on every horizontal surface within sight.

Someone had been inside. Recently enough that the thermostat had been checked. Not recently enough to clean.

The living room opened to his left. Two plant pots on the windowsill, both dead, the soil pulled away from the ceramic in the contracted geometry of sustained dehydration. A reading lamp beside the armchair was unplugged, its cord coiled with the precision of someone who coiled cords the same way every time.

Kain gave the living room four seconds. The study door was open at the end of the hall.

Inside, the second journal opened to page twelve. The spine held the position without resistance, the binding softened by repeated use at this specific spread.

Solis's handwriting ran smaller here than in the first volume. The left-leaning script compressed at the ends of lines, crowding the margin as though the page had been shorter than anticipated. The ink was absorbed into the paper rather than held on the surface. Consistent pressure across sentences that did not pause for revision.

Subject 7 appeared on the third line.

*Subject 7 presents the first clean insertion at 68 hours. Behavioral integration is 94% at 72 hours. The remaining 6% is what I have begun calling the residue.*

Kain read the entry twice. The second reading was not for comprehension. Comprehension had arrived on the first pass, complete and without ambiguity. The second reading was verification. The same function as photographing a document before turning the page.

Sixty-eight hours. The 2128 trials had modelled ninety-six. Solis had found the discrepancy and written it down in a journal that had never been logged, in handwriting belonging to the woman who spent twenty-two years building the program before her removal.

The entry continued below in the same script: *The replacement does not know why it reaches for the coffee with the left hand. It simply does.* A margin note in bluer ink, added later, read: *Detection method: look for what is right in a way the replacement cannot explain.*

Kain turned to page forty-four. The paper here was thinner at the edges from handling. Someone had returned to this page more than once.

*The residue is not a flaw. The residue is the proof. It is the evidence that the original subject existed.*

He photographed the spread. The shutter click was the only sound. No annotation followed. Solis had written the detection method fourteen years ago, and the program had continued. He closed the volume and set it beside the first.

He turned from the journal and the mug was there.

Not on the mug tree. The mug tree held four white mugs, identical and institutional, handles oriented at consistent angles. This one sat on the counter beside the stove, deep blue glaze worn at the rim where a thumb would rest. Gold lettering on the side, two characters: A&S. Below the letters, a hairline crack ran from the lip to the midpoint of the body. A single clean fracture, consistent with a fall from height.

Kain did not touch it. He photographed it from two angles. The gold was legible; the crack was visible. Position relative to the burner: documented.

The mug was not on the manifest. He had read it twice. Forty-seven items. Four white mugs were logged under item 16, domestic ceramics. No blue mug. No gold lettering or provenance for the object sitting eleven centimetres from the burner.

He looked at the kitchen again. White mugs on the tree. Blue mug on the counter. Less than a metre between them, and between what they represented, a distance the manifest had not been calibrated to measure.

Someone had been in the apartment whose presence was not in any QSA document. A person who drinks from a commissioned blue mug. Whose initials, printed in gold, survived the search team's visit and the four years of lease renewals in a home whose occupant was not coming back.

Late winter light striped the counter. The journals waited on the table. Four white mugs hung on the tree, and the blue one sat where someone had left it.

Kain filed the photograph in his partition. He closed the door without looking back. The lock engaged, returning the space to the work of holding what had been left inside it.
