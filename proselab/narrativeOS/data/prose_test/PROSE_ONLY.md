# Scene 01

The Black Pearl's car park had three sodium lights. One dead, one cycling, one steady. Same configuration as January.

Inside, the Gen-4 registered his entry through the passive array in the bar's surface. The loading-bay ceiling held its usual geometry of exposed steel and riveted joins. Fourteen metres of bar along the eastern wall. Two patrons at the far end, neither of them Emily.

Kain did not sit down.

The camera was mounted above the service corridor entrance, where the brick met the original loading-bay frame. Matte black housing. Varifocal lens. Dual optical and thermal configuration. Pelco Spectra series, at VicTrack standard height of 4.2 metres. VicTrack had no jurisdiction over a private establishment whose mesh node was buried under the foundation slab.

Or had been.

The tremor in his right hand — ring finger, little finger — kept its usual cadence. He put the hand in his jacket pocket. His thumb found the lighter's wheel, traced the worn engraving without pressing.

The Gen-4 set a glass on the bar at his usual position. Preference profile, still on file.

Kain was already at the door.

# Scene 02

The kettle was on the second shelf. Hayden's left hand located the handle — four centimetres from the shelf lip, angled toward the wall. The fingers closed. The grip held for two seconds before the calcification in the metacarpal joints produced a lateral drift that reclassified the handle from held to resting-against.

He moved the kettle to the counter with his right hand.

The apartment was empty. The fridge cycled. A bathroom tap dripped at its standard interval — every eleven seconds, consistent across nine days at this address. No other sounds.

Water. The element engaged. He set a cup on the counter. White ceramic, one of four Bell had sourced from the Footscray op shop during the second week. Three in the cupboard.

His right hand was reliable. His left reported position at the wrist but not at the fingertips. Three months. The body stabilising against four hundred and seventeen transitions with the only material available to it.

The kettle took ninety seconds. He counted.

The cup received the water. No tea. The box sat twelve centimetres to the left, and he had been facing it while the kettle boiled and had not processed it as an instruction.

Temporal anchoring: present. Intermittent. The thread between this moment and the previous one held. The thread between the previous moment and the one before it was absent. He was in the kitchen. He was making tea. These did not require continuity to be true.

He put the teabag in the cup. The water had cooled. He did not reboil it.

# Scene 03

The cafe had been a printer's workshop until 2041. The pressed-tin ceiling was original. Two fluorescent tubes ran the length of the room, one cycling at a frequency visible only peripherally.

Kain had been sitting for eleven minutes. His coffee had cooled past the point where the surface tension changed. His right hand rested on the table, the tremor in the ring and little fingers present with the constancy of a measurement that had become biographical. His left hand was in his lap. It had been in his lap since he sat down.

The woman who came through the door at 10:14 was not someone he knew. She ordered a long black and took the table nearest the window, two tables from his. The knot in her bag strap was a bowline, tied by someone who counted things rather than estimated them.

She did not look at him for nine seconds. Then she did.

"Kain," she said.

He did not ask how she knew.

Emily came through the door ninety seconds later. She stopped two steps inside. Her jaw shifted on the left side. She sat at the empty table between them and did not order.

"You're not here officially," Kain said.

"No."

Bell set her cup down. The ceramic contacted the saucer with a sound that was precise and very small.

"He wrote about you," she said. "In the early logs."

Kain's right hand moved to his jacket pocket. The lighter was there, rectangular, worn brass. He did not take it out.

"I know," he said.

Nobody spoke for four minutes. A tram passed on Lygon Street, and the bell carried a resonance that arrived at Kain's left peripheral field as a faint iridescent compression. It held for two seconds, then released.

None of them had planned this. None of them left.

# Scene 04

The 11:40 tram ran seven minutes late. Kain stood at the Punt Road stop with his left hand in his jacket pocket and his right on the rail. Tremor in the ring and little finger — eight months of it now, long enough that the body had stopped filing reports.

The flat was on the third floor. The stairwell smelled of latex primer, water-based, the kind residential management companies specified for turnover units. The door was grey. It had been green.

He did not have a key.

He stood in the corridor for eleven seconds. The iridescent fringe at his left visual margin registered the fluorescent tube above the door and did nothing with the information. His PQ pathways processed light the way they processed everything now. Continuously. Without request.

The kitchen was behind the grey door, or a kitchen was. The window would still face the car park. The bench where Solis had handed him the lighter and said *keep it, you earned it, and you should quit anyway* would be the same bench, or a different bench in the same position, which was not the same thing.

He went back down.

On the return tram, the bell's decay carried the 7.83 Hz resonance through the rail and into the bones of his right hand. The left hand, denervated, registered nothing.

He did not think about the kitchen for long.

# Scene 05

Hayden's left hand found the brachial plexus through Jacob's uniform collar. Two fingers, index and middle, positioned against the nerve cluster at the junction of neck and shoulder. The placement took one and a half seconds longer than it should have. The calcification in his finger joints delivered every command on delay — training could compensate, the joints themselves could not.

Jacob's body began to turn. Hayden's right hand came up under the jaw. Chin redirected. Airway extended. The weight of the skull leveraged against the cervical spine's natural resistance to lateral rotation. Jacob outweighed him by thirty kilograms. The geometry did not require equal mass. It required the correct angle.

The nerve compression took four seconds. Jacob's legs failed first. Then the hands. The security badge clipped to his chest pocket swung once as he folded, caught the fluorescent light, swung again. Hayden held the compression for two seconds past the collapse. The protocol margin. His index finger tremor was present throughout. He counted it at three cycles per second, which was faster than Tuesday.

Jacob's breathing continued. Shallow, autonomous, steady enough.

Hayden released. His fingers did not straighten immediately.
