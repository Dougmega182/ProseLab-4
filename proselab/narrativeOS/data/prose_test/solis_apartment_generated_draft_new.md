The lock was a residential-grade Lockwood 001 deadbolt with six pins. The pick took eleven seconds. Kain held the tension wrench steady with his left hand and did not enter until the bolt had cleared the strike plate by its full throw.

The hallway was narrow. Coats on three hooks, the fourth hook empty, its mounting screw carrying a faint oxidation ring where a coat's weight had prevented airflow for years. Kain stood in the doorway and let the space arrive.

Four years of managed air. The QSA's property holding structure had maintained climate control. The thermostat on the hallway wall read nineteen degrees, its programming unchanged since the last human adjustment. No dust on the thermostat's upper edge. Dust on every horizontal surface within sight.

Someone had been inside. Recently enough that the thermostat had been checked. Not recently enough to clean.

The living room opened to his left. Two plant pots on the windowsill, both dead, the soil pulled away from the ceramic in the contracted geometry of sustained dehydration. A reading lamp beside the armchair was unplugged, its cord coiled with the precision of someone who coiled cords the same way every time.

Kain gave the living room four seconds. The study door was open at the end of the hall.

The study was small. Desk against the window, chair pushed in, a bookshelf along the north wall holding two published volumes and eleven years of QSA internal reports in matching grey spines. The desk surface was clear except for a lamp and a pen holder containing three pens, all capped.

The journals were on the bottom shelf. Three hardbound A5 volumes, dark green covers faded at the corners to grey, the spines unmarked. Dates in handwriting that was small and precise and slightly left-leaning. Kain checked each inside front cover. No adhesive label, no barcode. The manifest reference field was blank on all three.

He opened the first volume to the first entry. Fourteen years old. The paper absorbed ink rather than holding it on the surface.

*Coherence degrades at the cellular level within 71 hours of initial quantum state transfer. The 2128 trials indicated 96. The discrepancy is in the substrate. We have been modeling against synthetic tissue. The living substrate responds differently. It resists.*

Kain turned pages. The handwriting maintained its pace and pressure across entries spanning weeks, then months. Clinical notations on insertion windows, substrate response curves, cellular rejection thresholds. The language was operational throughout.

He found Subject 7 on page thirty-one.

*Subject 7 presents the first clean insertion at 68 hours. Behavioral integration is 94% at 72 hours. The remaining 6% is what I have begun calling the residue. The behavioral patterns so deeply encoded in the original subject's neurology that they survive the transfer and surface in the replacement as habits the replacement cannot account for. The replacement does not know why it reaches for the coffee with the left hand. It simply does.*

Kain read the entry twice. He photographed the page, then the facing page, then the next entry. The camera's shutter sound was the only noise in the apartment besides the circulation fan running from a vent in the hallway ceiling.

He moved to the second journal. Page forty-four. The handwriting had changed: lighter pressure, the letters compressing at the ends of lines.

*The residue is not a flaw. The residue is the proof. It is the evidence that the original subject existed. It is the thing the replacement cannot erase because it does not know it is there.*

Kain photographed the page. He closed the journal and replaced it on the shelf, spine aligned with the other two.

Through the study doorway, the kitchen held four white mugs on a mug tree, identical, purchased in sets. A kettle on the left side of the bench. A single tea towel, folded once, draped over the oven handle.

On the windowsill behind the desk lamp: a fifth mug. Deep blue ceramic, smaller than the institutional whites. Gold lettering faced the room. A&S. The glaze was worn at the handle where a thumb would rest, and a single clean fracture ran from the rim to a point two centimetres below it, sealed with age rather than repaired.

Kain photographed it from two angles without touching it. The gold was legible. The crack was visible.

Two initials on a personal object in an apartment where every other object was professional. He filed the letters without attribution. The circulation fan cycled in the hallway. Light from the desk lamp stopped at the edge of the windowsill, leaving the mug half in shadow.

He replaced the lens cap and checked the study from the doorway. Journals aligned, chair pushed in, no surface disturbed. Everything in position, everything accounted for. A space maintained rather than occupied.

Kain let himself out and pulled the door closed until the latch engaged.
